# Create an Open Graph image that keeps your logo visible on WhatsApp and social media

<LeadIntro>
**Creating an Open Graph image** involves more than designing a wide banner. Facebook may show almost the complete image, LinkedIn may scale it differently, and WhatsApp may turn it into a small square preview beside the title. Keep the logo, short core message and main subject in a centred safe zone. Use the outer areas mainly for backgrounds, atmosphere and decoration that can disappear without losing meaning.
</LeadIntro>

You share a page on WhatsApp expecting a polished wide image. Instead, a small square appears on the left. The logo is outside the crop, the title has been cut in half and the contact details are too small to read. The same link may look fine on Facebook. This is not necessarily random: every platform places the image inside its own card, aspect ratio and available space.

You do not need a completely different website image for every channel. Design one strong **Open Graph image** with a clear hierarchy and central safe area. The essential information then remains recognisable when an app crops the left and right edges.

This guide explains what an OG image is, why WhatsApp often displays a square crop, which dimensions provide a practical master and where to place the logo, copy and subject. It covers WhatsApp, Facebook, LinkedIn, X and Instagram, then provides the technical tags and a publication checklist.

For the wider website project, use the [complete guide to building a website in Limburg](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/complete-guide-to-building-a-website-in-limburg/) and the [new website checklist](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/new-website-checklist/).

<QuoteBlock>
Design the outer area for atmosphere. Design the centre for recognition.
</QuoteBlock>

## What is an Open Graph image in plain language?

An OG image is the image a website provides when someone shares a link. OG stands for **Open Graph**. The page places information in its source code, including its title, description, URL and image.

When someone pastes the link into WhatsApp, Facebook or LinkedIn, the platform tries to retrieve that information and create a link preview. It normally contains:

- an image;
- the page title;
- a short description;
- the domain name;
- sometimes additional information about the page type.

The official Open Graph specification includes `og:title`, `og:type`, `og:image` and `og:url`. A page can also provide the image width, height, file type and descriptive alt text.

An OG image is not automatically the same as the hero image, featured photograph or Instagram post. It can use the same photography, but the composition needs to work in small previews and several crops. Plan strong underlying imagery through our guide to [what corporate photography is](/en/kennisbank/fotografie/what-is-corporate-photography/).

## Why the same sharing image looks different everywhere

A website can provide the source image and metadata, but it cannot control exactly how each app renders the result. Platforms need to accommodate screen width, list views, chat bubbles, feed cards, mobile, desktop and changing interface variants.

A 1200 x 630 pixel image has an aspect ratio of approximately 1.91:1. That works well for wide link cards. A compact WhatsApp view may show an almost square thumbnail beside the text. To fill that square, the app must crop part of the wide image.

When a logo sits at the far left and the subject at the far right, a central crop may omit both. When all essential information fits in a compact area around the centre, at least the core remains.

Do not ask only where a logo looks attractive in the wide banner. Ask what remains visible when only the centre is shown.

<NoticeBox variant="tip" title="A platform crop is not an image error">
A sharing image can be technically correct and still appear differently from what you expected. The source remains wide, but the app selects a smaller crop for its interface. Design for several crops rather than one fixed example card.
</NoticeBox>

## The practical standard: 1200 x 630 pixels

For one general sharing image, **1200 x 630 pixels** remains a strong, broadly supported foundation. Meta recommends at least 1200 x 630 pixels for high-resolution link shares. It cites a width of at least 600 pixels as the minimum for larger link cards.

Do not use the wide format as an excuse to fill the entire canvas with copy. It still needs to work when a substantial part disappears from both sides.

Use this practical guide for a 1200 x 630 canvas:

```text
Complete OG image: 1200 x 630 px
Centred square crop: 630 x 630 px
Inner critical safe zone: approximately 500 x 500 px
```

On a 1200 pixel-wide image, the central square starts approximately 285 pixels from the left and ends about 285 pixels before the right edge. The complete 630 pixel height remains available.

The inner area of approximately 500 x 500 pixels provides extra breathing room. Keep everything that absolutely must remain recognisable there:

- the logo or compact brand mark;
- one short title or subject line;
- the main product, face or design;
- a recognisable colour or graphic element;
- optionally, one short supporting line.

Do not rely on long copy, phone numbers, URLs and small details as essential image information. They become illegible in a small WhatsApp thumbnail, while the card already displays a title, description and domain.

## Where should you place a logo on an OG image?

Do not automatically place the logo in the top-left corner simply because that feels familiar in an advert or presentation. A centred square crop often removes that area first.

Three compositions provide stronger recognition.

### 1. Compact logo above or below the centre

Place a small logo immediately above or below the central subject. It remains visible without covering the main image. This works for articles, case studies and service pages.

### 2. Logo inside a central card

Place a compact card, badge, screen, book cover or frame in the centre containing:

- the logo;
- a short title;
- one recognisable image;
- optionally, a short category.

A WhatsApp link preview shows why this can work. The broad image adds atmosphere on both sides, while the central card remains effective as a standalone square.

### 3. Brand mark in the centre, wordmark as an extra

If the identity includes a compact icon and wider wordmark, place the icon or monogram in the critical area. Use the full wordmark across the wider composition as an additional layer. If the sides disappear, the brand remains recognisable.

## How much text should the sharing image contain?

An OG image is not a brochure. The platform normally shows a title and description beside it already. Do not repeat the full SEO title, meta description, URL, phone number, email address and five services inside the image.

As a practical guideline, use:

- one main line of approximately three to seven words;
- optionally, one small category or brand label;
- a visible logo;
- sufficient empty space.

For a sitemap page, the central title `Sitemap` is stronger than a sentence listing every service. The WhatsApp card copy can explain that it is VisualVibe's complete sitemap.

Contact details may look useful at full size but disappear in small previews. Use them only as optional branding. Rely on the landing page, domain and link-card copy for contact.

## WhatsApp: design for the small square

WhatsApp makes the safe zone particularly important. Depending on the device, screen width and interface, a link may appear with a compact, almost square thumbnail on the left and the title, description and domain on the right.

The app may crop a wide image rather than scale it down completely. Anything outside the centre disappears from the thumbnail.

Use this process:

1. Create the source image at 1200 x 630 pixels.
2. Draw a temporary centred square measuring 630 x 630 pixels.
3. Keep the logo, main subject and short title inside it.
4. Keep the most important information within an inner area of about 500 x 500 pixels.
5. Remove the guides before export.
6. Test the real link on at least one Android and one iOS device when the preview matters commercially.

Meta's WhatsApp link-preview documentation says `og:image` should use an absolute image URL. For its documented Business Messaging preview, it specifies an image at least 300 pixels wide, an aspect ratio no greater than 4:1 and a file under 600 KB. An optimised 1200 x 630 JPEG or PNG fits comfortably within that ratio and provides sufficient resolution.

<NoticeBox variant="warning" title="The exact WhatsApp crop is not a fixed design contract">
Shape and crop can change with app version, device and available space. The central square is a robust design strategy, not a guarantee that every WhatsApp version displays exactly the same pixels.
</NoticeBox>

### What needs to remain visible in the WhatsApp thumbnail?

The thumbnail should make sense without small copy. At a heavily reduced size, check whether:

- VisualVibe remains recognisable;
- the page subject is clear;
- the main image remains intact;
- contrast is sufficient;
- the design does not resemble a random photograph;
- the thumbnail works without contact details.

If these points pass, wider Facebook and LinkedIn placements can show extra context without making the WhatsApp preview unusable.

## Facebook: a wide image with the core kept central

Facebook reads Open Graph metadata and recommends an image at least 1200 x 630 pixels for high-resolution displays. Many feed cards show nearly the complete wide image.

Still keep the core central. Links also appear in compact lists, notifications, mobile cards, messages and changing interfaces. Facebook may also cache an older version.

For Facebook, provide:

- a 1200 x 630 image;
- an absolute HTTPS URL;
- correct `og:image:width` and `og:image:height` values;
- appropriate `og:image:alt` text;
- a fast image available without a login;
- a refreshed crawl through Meta Sharing Debugger when required.

A wide photograph can tell more of the story on Facebook, but essential text should not touch the side edges. The central safe zone protects other placements.

## LinkedIn: Open Graph, visual restraint and caching

LinkedIn uses Open Graph or oEmbed to determine the title, description and image for a shared URL. The same 1200 x 630 master can therefore work on LinkedIn.

A calm professional image usually performs better than an overcrowded advert. Use a recognisable logo, one business title, a strong project image or clear graphic concept, sufficient negative space and no unreadable row of services.

LinkedIn may retain an earlier version. The official Post Inspector lets you inspect and refresh a URL for new posts. Existing posts containing that URL do not update automatically.

Before publication, check that the final image is live, the URL is public, `og:title`, `og:description` and `og:image` are correct, Post Inspector shows the intended preview and the image remains recognisable at a smaller size.

## X: use the same safe master and separate card tags

X can create a card around links in posts. Supply its card metadata alongside Open Graph so the title, description and image are explicit.

```html
<meta name="twitter:card" content="summary_large_image">
<meta name="twitter:title" content="Page title">
<meta name="twitter:description" content="Short page description">
<meta name="twitter:image" content="https://visualvibe.media/images/example-og.jpg">
```

The 1200 x 630 image can be reused. Keep important content central because card display and crop can differ between screens and interfaces. The `twitter:*` names remain common in web implementations even though the platform is called X. Test a genuinely shared link after changes because validators and documentation routes can change.

## Instagram: an OG image is not an Instagram post

Instagram differs from Facebook, LinkedIn and WhatsApp. A URL in a normal feed caption does not become a large clickable Open Graph card. Feed posts, Reel covers and Stories normally require separate uploaded media in the correct aspect ratio.

An OG image remains useful when the link is shared outside Instagram or receives a preview in a message, but do not build Instagram content solely around the OG master.

Create two product families:

- **OG master:** 1200 x 630 pixels for shared website links;
- **social variants:** for example, 1080 x 1080, 1080 x 1350 and 1080 x 1920 pixels for Instagram.

Keep the brand style consistent while recomposing for each channel. [Brand photography](/en/diensten/photography/brand-photography/) and a reusable visual system prevent every format from starting from zero. Our guide to [preparing a corporate photo shoot](/en/kennisbank/fotografie/how-to-prepare-a-corporate-photo-shoot/) explains how to capture such an image set efficiently.

## Example of a robust composition

Imagine sharing an article about the VisualVibe sitemap.

### In the critical central area

- a compact card or screen mock-up;
- the VisualVibe logo;
- the word `Sitemap`;
- one recognisable orange accent.

### In the outer areas

- subtle images representing websites, photography and video;
- additional icons;
- soft background patterns;
- information not required to understand the topic.

### Beside the image through metadata

- title: `Sitemap | VisualVibe`;
- description: `Complete VisualVibe sitemap: all services, regions, case studies and knowledge articles.`;
- domain: `visualvibe.media`.

If WhatsApp shows only the central square, the logo and `Sitemap` remain visible. If Facebook uses the full width, the extra context creates a richer image.

## Technical Open Graph tags for a correct preview

A strong image works only when the website outputs the right metadata.

```html
<meta property="og:type" content="article">
<meta property="og:title" content="Create an OG image that keeps your logo visible everywhere">
<meta property="og:description" content="Keep your logo and essential content visible across different social media previews.">
<meta property="og:url" content="https://visualvibe.media/en/kennisbank/webdesign/create-open-graph-image-safe-zone">
<meta property="og:site_name" content="VisualVibe">
<meta property="og:locale" content="en_GB">

<meta property="og:image" content="https://visualvibe.media/images/knowledge-base/web-design/og-image-social-safe-zone.jpg">
<meta property="og:image:secure_url" content="https://visualvibe.media/images/knowledge-base/web-design/og-image-social-safe-zone.jpg">
<meta property="og:image:type" content="image/jpeg">
<meta property="og:image:width" content="1200">
<meta property="og:image:height" content="630">
<meta property="og:image:alt" content="Open Graph image with a centred square safe zone for WhatsApp, Facebook, LinkedIn and X">

<meta name="twitter:card" content="summary_large_image">
<meta name="twitter:title" content="Where should you place a logo on an OG image?">
<meta name="twitter:description" content="Use a centred safe zone so the logo remains visible on WhatsApp, Facebook, LinkedIn and X.">
<meta name="twitter:image" content="https://visualvibe.media/images/knowledge-base/web-design/og-image-social-safe-zone.jpg">
```

Always use an absolute `https://` URL. A relative path such as `/images/og.jpg` may work in your browser, but an external scraper needs the complete address.

The image must be publicly available without login, cookies, JavaScript or a short-lived token. Check that the server returns the correct MIME type too.

## JPEG, PNG or WebP for an OG image?

JPEG and PNG remain safe choices for broad sharing-image compatibility.

### JPEG

Useful for photography and designs with many colour gradients. File sizes normally remain modest. Use enough quality to prevent text and logos from becoming blocky.

### PNG

Useful for sharp graphic designs, flat colours and transparency. The file may become larger, particularly when it contains photography.

### WebP

WebP is efficient on websites, but support among external scrapers and older tools may be less predictable than for JPEG or PNG. When uncertain, use an optimised JPEG or PNG for the OG version even if the web page itself uses WebP or AVIF.

Keep the image light. Meta's documented WhatsApp preview specifies an upper limit of 600 KB. Other platforms also retrieve a compact image more quickly and reliably.

## Why a polished OG image matters commercially

A link preview is often a page's first impression, whether in a WhatsApp conversation with a potential customer, a LinkedIn post, Facebook Messenger or a case study forwarded by an employee.

A consistent sharing image supports brand recognition, trust, clarity about the topic, more professional social posts, the likelihood that someone opens the link and a consistent appearance between website and campaign.

The image itself is not a direct SEO ranking factor in the way page content is. A good preview can support content distribution and click-through. Technical metadata also helps platforms understand the correct title, description and image.

OG design belongs within professional [web design](/en/diensten/web-design/), not a final check five minutes before publication. Its technical implementation supports [SEO and online visibility](/en/diensten/seo/), while original [professional photography](/en/diensten/photography/) prevents the preview from looking like a generic stock card.
