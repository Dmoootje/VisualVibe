# Realization photography for contractors: from site to strong case

<LeadIntro>
**Project photography for contractors** documents a construction or renovation project for use in your portfolio, website, quotations and wider communications. A useful set does more than present an attractive end result. It makes the scale, material choices, technical details, craftsmanship and your company's role clear.
</LeadIntro>

The best photo series is rarely created when someone quickly takes a few images after completion. By then, temporary constructions will have disappeared, technologies will have been removed and residents or users will sometimes already be present. As a result, you miss exactly the evidence with which you later want to explain a method or difficult detail.

Treat photography as part of the project plan. Decide which phases cannot be recreated, who will arrange access, who may be recognisable and which files you need. Our guide to [corporate photography](/en/knowledge-base/photography/what-is-corporate-photography/) covers the wider company image library. Here, the focus is specifically on completed work in construction, renovation, interiors and technology.

<QuoteBlock>
A finished project attracts attention. The right detail and context show what work your team really did there.
</QuoteBlock>

## What should project photography prove?

A project photo is more than decoration. A future customer wants to assess whether your experience matches his assignment. An architect or construction partner looks at detailing and collaboration. A prospective employee wants to see which projects and with which materials he will work. Your photo series should be able to support these different questions without promising more than the project proves.

Strong project photography therefore combines three layers:

Photography does not guarantee quote requests. It does make your story more verifiable when image, case description, customer context and service information correctly represent the same project.

## First choose the purpose of using the images

Don't start by asking how many photos you want. First make a note of where they end up. A portfolio hero requires space for a title. A detail in a technical explanation may be more closely framed. A yard sign or trade fair wall requires a lot of resolution, while a vertical social post needs a different composition.

A construction company often uses a project series for multiple purposes: a case on the website, a project sheet with quotations, social updates, employer branding, press communication and submissions for partners or awards. Those channels do not have the same information value or aspect ratio. Therefore, make it clear for each application what is essential and what is a bonus.

## Build a briefing that the photographer can execute

A good briefing identifies the project, the contribution of your company, the target group and the concrete applications. Include plans or simple reference images if they help you understand a position. Also mention what has not yet been completed, which zones are sensitive and who can make decisions on the day itself.

Do not provide a long list of nouns such as facade, kitchen, bathroom and garden. Explain why each element matters. "Custom cabinet" says very little. "Show how the custom cabinet uses the sloping wall and meets the existing floor" gives the photographer direction for the overview, detail and viewpoint.

<NoticeBox variant="tip" title="Indicate the contribution of each partner">
A project can combine the work of an architect, main contractor, installer, interior designer and supplier. Make a note in advance of what your company has done and who should be correctly mentioned. This prevents the case from unintentionally attributing the entire result to one party.
</NoticeBox>

## Create a shot list with an overview and detail

A shot list protects the essential images, but leaves room for circumstances on location. Divide the list into priorities A, B and C. Priority A contains images without which the case cannot tell its story. B provides depth. C is extra material when time, weather and access permit.

In addition, create a short exclusion list. Consider house numbers, security systems, confidential plans, serial numbers, license plates or adjacent properties that should not be recognizable. Check that list on location, not during post-processing.

## Plan the right project phases

Not every project needs multiple photo opportunities. For a complex renovation, a limited series of shell construction, techniques and finishing can tell much more than just the end result. Choose stages based on what will later become invisible or inaccessible.

Do not automatically use progress images as advertising images. A technically useful photo can look messy, temporary or security-sensitive. Select per goal and provide context so that an intermediate phase is not read as final quality.

## Site access and safety always come first

A photographer is not a free visitor on an active site. Coordinate access with project or site management and follow the safety and health plan, signage and instructions on location. The FPS Employment describes the legal framework for [tijdelijke of mobiele bouwplaatsen](https://werk.belgie.be/nl/themas/welzijn-op-het-werk/arbeidsplaatsen/tijdelijke-mobiele-bouwplaatsen), including coordination and, for certain construction sites, attendance registration.

Ask in advance what personal protective equipment is required, where material is allowed and which areas are prohibited. Record who receives and accompanies the photographer. A camera is not a reason to move a barrier, silently approach a machine or ask someone to repeat an unsafe action.

Work at height only via the access and working method permitted by the responsible organization. The information from the FPS about [tijdelijke werkzaamheden op hoogten](https://werk.belgie.be/nl/themas/welzijn-op-het-werk/arbeidsmiddelen/tijdelijke-werkzaamheden-op-hoogten) emphasizes, among other things, that the means of access must be appropriate to the situation and that weather conditions must not endanger safety. So do not improvise a position on a ladder, scaffolding or roof. Discuss a safe alternative, a different time or possibly a separate drone recording.

[Project drone footage](/en/services/drone-fpv/project-drone-footage/) can add scale and site context. It does not replace ground photography: aerial views show size and surroundings, while ground-level images reveal finishes, materials and human scale.

## Plan around weather, sun and ambient light

Light determines whether shapes, textures and material transitions are legible. A facade in hard frontal sun shows different information than the same facade with grazing light. Interiors with large windows can have difficulty displaying outdoor views and indoor space at the same time. Artificial light can cause color differences or flickering.

Therefore, check orientation, sun position, opening hours and expected weather conditions. For outdoor work, plan a realistic fallback moment. A cloudy day often provides calm, even light; Directed sunlight can enhance depth and relief. Neither is always better. The right light results from the material and the desired appearance.

Prepare the location for photos before the shoot. Remove construction waste, cables, protective film and vehicles that do not belong to the story. Check lamps, windows, doors, plumbing, cushions and loose objects. At the same time, leave relevant usage marks or tools where they show a real process. Photo ready means well cared for, not artificially lifeless.

## Show craftsmanship without building a play

An action works strongly when it is real, safe and understandable. Don't ask a professional to point aimlessly at tools. Choose a task that makes it visually clear what is being checked, installed or completed. First explain the normal working order to the photographer and determine together which moment is useful.

Vary distance: a wide shot shows work context, a middle shot connects person and action, a detail shows tools or finishing. Keep protective equipment, correct working posture and a realistic working environment intact. A posed unsafe situation undermines exactly the trust that the photo is intended to build.

## Arrange permission, privacy and location agreements

Clear agreements are required for recognisable employees, residents, customers or bystanders. In its guidance on [the principles of image rights](https://www.gegevensbeschermingsautoriteit.be/index.php/burger/thema-s/recht-op-afbeelding/principes), the Belgian Data Protection Authority distinguishes permission to take an image from permission to publish or distribute it. Inform those involved about the purpose and intended channels, and keep a record of the appropriate agreements.

A photograph centred on a building or property can still contain personal data if it makes someone readily identifiable. The Belgian Data Protection Authority explains this in its guidance on the [Act of 30 July 2018 and images](https://www.gegevensbeschermingsautoriteit.be/burger/thema-s/recht-op-afbeelding/wet-van-30-juli-2018). Think beyond faces: a name on a drawing, number plate, unique address or personal interior can reveal sensitive information.

In addition, request permission from the client, owner or resident for access and commercial publication where necessary. Agree whether the address, architect, suppliers and other executors may or must be mentioned. In the case of sensitive projects, minors, secure locations or in doubt, have the specific situation assessed by a competent privacy or legal advisor. This article is not legal advice.

## Link the photos to a substantive case

A photo gallery without explanation leaves a lot of interpretation open. Build a case around a clear project question, the preconditions, your contribution, important choices and the result. Only mention figures and achievements that you can substantiate. Give relevant partners proper credit.

## Agree on selection, delivery and usage license

Before the shoot, determine how many finished images are provided, who makes the selection, what level of retouching is included and when the delivery will take place. Request formats for the agreed applications, for example high resolution for print and optimized variants for web. Also discuss vertical and wide cuts if you need them.

Receiving photo files does not automatically transfer all copyright. The [FPS Economy copyright FAQ](https://economie.fgov.be/sites/default/files/Files/Publications/files/FAQ_Auteursrecht.pdf) provides general information about protected work and lawful use. State exactly what the licence covers: your website, organic social media, paid advertising, print, press, partners, geographic reach, duration and any edits.

Also save the project name, date, creator, credits, permission status and usage restrictions with the image bank. This way, a colleague can later see which image is suitable for which channel. Have contractual questions assessed tailor-made when rights transfer, exclusivity or broad distribution are important.

## Publish images accessible and findable

Place each photograph close to the text it supports. Use a descriptive filename and write alt text for the image's function in context. [Google's image SEO guidance](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/google-images) recommends relevant surrounding text, useful alt text and a technically accessible implementation. Do not stuff alt text with keywords.

[WCAG 2.2](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/) requires a text alternative for meaningful non-text content. "Seamless join between a wooden custom cabinet and a sloping roof wall" is more useful than "project photography contractor Limburg". If an image is purely decorative and adds no information, an empty alt value may be appropriate.

Compress web variants and deliver responsive dimensions. Store the finished original separately. This way, a telephone does not have to load a large print file and sufficient quality remains for other applications.

## From a beautiful final image to demonstrable craftsmanship

Realization photography becomes valuable when it is planned early enough. Choose which project phases contain evidence, translate applications into a priority shot list and let security, privacy and access prevail. Then link the final selection to honest case copy, correct credits and clear usage agreements.

In this way, project after project, you build a portfolio that not only looks nice, but also shows what problem your team solved, how the work was carried out and what quality a future customer can actually assess.
