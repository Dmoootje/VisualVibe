# New website checklist: from brief to handover

<LeadIntro>
A **new website checklist** helps prevent important decisions from surfacing only just before launch. Design and technology are not the only things that need to be right. Goals, copy, photography, SEO, forms, privacy, measurement, ownership and follow-up all need an owner before the relevant milestone.
</LeadIntro>

This checklist is for SMEs commissioning a website and wanting to keep the project under control. Use it for the initial brief, internal progress reviews and final acceptance. It is not a list for one person to tick off on the last day. Some decisions belong to the business owner, while others sit with marketing, the web partner, IT or a legal adviser.

To understand why these elements belong together, start with our [complete guide to commissioning a website](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/complete-guide-to-building-a-website-in-limburg/). If you are still deciding how large the site should be, use our guide to choosing a [one-page or full website](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/one-page-or-full-website/). Once the scope is agreed, this page helps prevent work from falling between different areas of responsibility.

## Make ownership visible first

A task without an owner is more likely to be late or incomplete. For every item, agree who decides, who carries it out, who checks it and when feedback is due. One person can hold several roles, provided that responsibility is explicit.

  highlightFirstColumn
  headers={["Role", "Typical responsibility", "Decision or deliverable"]}
  rows={[
    ["Business owner or client", "Goals, budget, priorities and final approval", "What must the website achieve for the business?"],
    ["Internal project lead", "Planning, collecting materials and consolidating feedback", "Who supplies what, and by when?"],
    ["Web partner", "Structure, UX, design, development and quality control", "How will the objective become a working website?"],
    ["Content and SEO", "Search intent, copy, metadata and internal links", "Which page answers which question?"],
    ["Photography and video team", "Shot list, production, selection, rights and optimisation", "Which visual evidence supports the story?"],
    ["IT or domain administrator", "DNS, mailboxes, accounts, security and access", "Who controls the technical keys?"],
    ["Legal or privacy adviser", "Situation-specific review of information and processing", "Which obligations apply to this company and its website functions?"],
  ]}
  caption="Decide who will actually fulfil each role on your project. This table is not a universal division of contractual responsibility."
/>

Create one feedback route too. When five colleagues send comments separately, conflicting decisions and endless revisions follow. Ask the internal project lead to collect, prioritise and deliver feedback as one round.

## The seven phases of a website project

  steps={[

    { label: "3", title: "Content and media", description: "Produce copy, photography, video, evidence and metadata." },
    { label: "4", title: "Design and build", description: "Develop components, responsiveness, technology and accessibility." },
    { label: "5", title: "Quality control", description: "Systematically test content, functions, SEO, privacy and devices." },
    { label: "6", title: "Launch", description: "Monitor the domain, redirects, indexing, measurement and forms." },

The phases may overlap, but their order protects you from expensive rework. Approving a design before the structure and real content are clear, for example, often produces attractive sections that contain the wrong information.

## Phase 1: project framework and brief

Start with business decisions, not colours or animation. A website intended to build brand confidence requires different evidence from one expected to generate quotation requests every day. Define one primary outcome and no more than a few supporting goals.

<FeatureGrid columns={2}>
  <ChecklistBlock title="Business framework" className="my-0 h-full" items={[
    "The primary objective and desired conversion are documented",
    "The main audiences and geographic areas are defined",
    "The offer, priority services and differentiators are confirmed",
    "The budget includes content, media, licences and aftercare",
    "Success is linked to measurable signals",
    "The project owner and final decision-maker are named",
  ]} />
  <ChecklistBlock title="Practical agreements" className="my-0 h-full" items={[
    "The scope and explicit exclusions are in writing",
    "Milestones, feedback dates and desired launch date are realistic",
    "The number of revision rounds and feedback process are agreed",
    "Existing domains, hosting and accounts are inventoried",
    "Ownership of design, code, copy and media is clear",
    "Internal time for interviews and approval is scheduled",
  ]} />
</FeatureGrid>

Do not compare quotations on price alone. Our guide to [website development costs](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/website-development-costs/) explains which deliverables, recurring costs and responsibilities may account for the difference.

## Phase 2: sitemap, user journeys and functions

The sitemap is not an administrative list. It determines where visitors find answers and which pages search engines can understand. Give every page an owner, objective, primary audience and intended next step. Remove pages that exist only because competitors have them.

Test the structure against real situations. Can a new visitor go straight to the relevant service? Can an existing customer find support quickly? Can an applicant discover vacancies and company culture? Is contact always accessible without repeating the same button in every section?

<ChecklistBlock title="Structure and functional scope" items={[
  "Every URL has a unique purpose and content owner",
  "The main navigation uses words customers understand",
  "Services, case studies, sectors and knowledge content link together logically",
  "Mobile navigation contains the same important routes as desktop navigation",
  "Forms request only information that is genuinely needed",
  "Booking, payment, multilingual content and external integrations are functionally specified",
  "An error page, search function and empty states are provided where relevant",
  "Existing URLs that will change are included in a redirect list",
]} />

An online shop also needs product data, payment methods, delivery, returns, transactional emails and consumer information. Use our dedicated guide to [online shop development for SMEs](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/online-shop-development-for-smes/) alongside this checklist.

## Phase 3: content, SEO and media

Do not wait for the design to be finished before gathering copy and visuals. Real content determines the space that headings, evidence, tables, case studies and calls to action require. Write around customer questions and explain specialist terminology. Avoid copy that talks mainly about the company without making the problem it solves clear.

<FeatureGrid columns={2}>
  <ChecklistBlock title="Copy and SEO" className="my-0 h-full" items={[
    "Search intent and the main question are defined for every page",
    "The H1, subheadings and opening paragraph provide clarity quickly",
    "Claims are supported by case studies, figures or sources",
    "Page titles and descriptions are unique and accurate",
    "Internal links use descriptive anchor text",
    "Calls to action suit the visitor's stage in the journey",
    "Addresses, opening hours, prices and names are checked centrally",
  ]} />
  <ChecklistBlock title="Photography and video" className="my-0 h-full" items={[
    "The shot list follows the planned pages and image ratios",
    "The team, process, location and completed work provide genuine evidence",
    "Consent and usage rights are documented",
    "File names and alt text describe relevant content",
    "Images are cropped for both mobile and desktop",
    "Video has a poster image, captions and a lightweight, performant embed",
    "Source files and selections are stored securely",
  ]} />
</FeatureGrid>

[Google recommends descriptive, crawlable links](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/crawling-indexing/links-crawlable), helping people and search engines understand the destination. Link to [how website speed and SEO work together](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/why-a-fast-website-can-rank-better/), for example, rather than writing 'read more'. Plan [photography](/en/services/photography/) and [video production](/en/services/videography/) alongside the page structure so every visual has a specific purpose.

## Phase 4: design, development and accessibility

Do not check only whether the design looks attractive and matches the visual identity. Visitors must be able to read text, recognise buttons and operate forms on different screens and with different input methods. The [W3C Web Content Accessibility Guidelines 2.2](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/) cover text alternatives, contrast, keyboard control, focus and programmatically identifiable interface elements, among other areas.

Not every SME is subject to exactly the same statutory accessibility requirements. Even so, accessible design improves usability for many people. Before making a conformance claim, ask a specialist which formal standard applies to your organisation or service.

<DoDontGrid
  doTitle="Test through real interaction"
  dontTitle="This is not an adequate test"
  dos={[
    "Navigate with a keyboard and visible focus",
    "Understand and correct form errors",
    "Enlarge text and allow the page to reflow",
    "Operate buttons and links on a phone",
    "Understand images and video without missing essential context",
    "Try pages on a slower mobile connection",
  ]}
  donts={[
    "Approve only one desktop screenshot",
    "Rely solely on an automated score",
    "Use colour as the only carrier of meaning",
    "Treat placeholder text as a field label",
    "Load heavy media without alternatives",
    "Test mobile only after everything else is approved",
  ]}
/>

The technical scope should include HTTPS, current dependencies, backups, error logging, spam protection and secure forms. Test the current Core Web Vitals too, but do not treat one laboratory score as complete proof of quality. [Google explains](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/core-web-vitals) that Core Web Vitals are part of page experience and that good scores alone do not guarantee top rankings.

## Phase 5: legal information and privacy

Legal review depends on your legal structure, sector, audience, data processing and sales model. The points below are therefore a working checklist, not legal advice.

The [GDPR](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32016R0679) requires, among other things, a clear purpose, an appropriate legal basis, data minimisation and limited retention. Translate these principles into concrete decisions for each form and integration, and seek legal review where necessary.

The Belgian [FPS Economy](https://news.economie.fgov.be/203683-deze-info-moet-u-zeker-vermelden-op-uw-bedrijfswebsite/) states that a Belgian business website must display identifying information even when nothing is sold online. This includes the company name, registered address, rapid contact details and company number. Additional rules may apply to regulated professions and online sales.

<NoticeBox variant="warning" title="Consent before non-essential cookies">
According to the [Belgian Data Protection Authority](https://www.gegevensbeschermingsautoriteit.be/cookies-en-andere-traceringsmiddelen), non-essential cookies and similar trackers should generally be placed or read only after the visitor has received clear information and given valid consent. Test this technically: an attractive cookie banner does not solve the problem if analytics loads beforehand.
</NoticeBox>

<ChecklistBlock title="Legal and privacy review" items={[
  "The company name, address, email and company number are displayed correctly",
  "VAT details and any professional information have been checked",
  "The privacy notice matches the forms, tools and recipients",
  "Purposes and retention periods for collected data are defined",
  "Non-essential cookies genuinely wait for consent",
  "Visitors can change their cookie choice later",
  "External embeds, pixels, fonts and chat tools are inventoried",
  "Sales, payment, delivery and returns terms are reviewed for ecommerce",
  "The legal basis, image rights and usage agreements for people, testimonials and case studies are documented",
]} />

Have the final copy and settings reviewed by someone familiar with your particular situation. Do not copy privacy notices or terms from another website: its tools, processes and responsibilities are not yours.

## Phase 6: testing and launch

Test against a fixed acceptance list and record the severity, owner and status of every issue. Ask someone who has not seen the project every day to test it too. They are more likely to notice unclear labels, missing context and dead ends.

<FeatureGrid columns={2}>
  <ChecklistBlock title="Content and functions" className="my-0 h-full" items={[
    "All links, buttons, filters and downloads work",
    "Forms show confirmation and deliver email or data correctly",
    "Phone numbers, email addresses, addresses and opening hours are correct",
    "Spelling, placeholder copy and missing images have been checked",
    "An agreed device and browser matrix has been tested based on audience, analytics and functional risks",
    "The 404 page and error messages help visitors move forward",
  ]} />
  <ChecklistBlock title="SEO and technology" className="my-0 h-full" items={[
    "Indexable pages have correct canonical and robots settings",
    "Redirects from old URLs to the most relevant new URLs work",
    "The XML sitemap contains only intended canonical URLs",
    "Structured data is valid and matches visible content",
    "Analytics and conversion tracking respect the agreed cookie and privacy choices",
    "Backups, monitoring, security and recovery procedures are active",
  ]} />
</FeatureGrid>

[Google explains](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/crawling-indexing/sitemaps/build-sitemap) that a sitemap helps identify important URLs but does not guarantee crawling or indexing. Submit it through Search Console after launch, then monitor indexing, performance reports and errors. Use the [technical SEO checklist](/en/kennisbank/seo-geo/technical-seo-checklist/) for this stage too. Protect a staging site with authentication or a network restriction during development. `noindex` is not a security measure. Do, however, confirm that temporary indexing blocks have been removed when the public site launches.

A website redesign needs particular care with redirects. Sending an old service page to the homepage without good reason removes the visitor's context. Map every valuable old URL to the most relevant new destination. Read [when to redesign your website](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/when-to-redesign-your-website/) if the migration, technology and content are significantly out of date.

## Phase 7: handover, maintenance and review

A website has not been handed over simply because the homepage is visible. The client must know who owns the accounts, how content is updated, what happens during an outage and which costs recur.

<ChecklistBlock title="Handover pack" items={[
  "The domain, DNS, hosting and important accounts are registered to the appropriate party",
  "Administrators use personal accounts and suitable two-factor authentication",
  "Passwords are shared securely and not stored in an ordinary email chain",
  "Documentation or training covers everyday management tasks",
  "Source files, licences, fonts and media rights are documented",
  "Maintenance, updates, backups, monitoring and support have an owner",
  "The client can access analytics, Search Console and conversion measurement",
  "Outstanding improvements and future extensions are in a backlog",
  "Review dates after several weeks and several months are scheduled",
]} />

After launch, do not measure visitor numbers alone. Check whether people find the right services, complete forms, call and submit qualified enquiries. Look at where they abandon the journey too. If traffic produces mainly unsuitable enquiries, investigate the content, proposition, targeting, campaigns and form design. A higher speed score will not resolve that diagnosis.

For structured technical follow-up, [website maintenance](/en/services/web-design/website-maintenance/) can define updates, backups and checks. Content, case studies and SEO also need their own rhythm. Decide who will collect new work, review outdated information and update pages when the offer changes.

## Common website project mistakes

Many project problems arise not from one major technical fault, but from decisions that remain implicit for too long.

## A good launch begins long before launch day

Use this checklist as a shared working document. Add deadlines, owners and evidence of each check. 'Done' then becomes a series of verifiable agreements rather than a feeling. That does not make the project bureaucratic. It gives designers, developers and the client more room to do good work without surprises at the end.
