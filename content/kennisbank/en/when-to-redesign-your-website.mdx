# Redesigning a website: when should you optimise or rebuild?

<LeadIntro>
You should **redesign your website** when the current site obstructs your business goals. Sometimes better copy, images and calls to action solve the real problem. Sometimes a focused technical improvement or a new component system makes the difference. In other cases, the structure has become so restrictive that a redesign or complete rebuild is the more rational choice.
</LeadIntro>

The difficult part is deciding what to preserve, improve and replace. An unnecessary rebuild can disrupt value you have already created. Continuing to patch an unsuitable foundation can hold your team back just as much.

This guide gives SMEs a diagnostic framework covering business signals, content, data, technology, accessibility and SEO. It then outlines a migration process that carries existing URLs and measurement forward carefully. That reduces avoidable risks, but cannot guarantee that traffic or rankings will remain unchanged.

## Start with the problem, not the new design

"The website feels old" is not yet a project brief. Ask what is actually going wrong. Are visitors unable to find an answer? Are you receiving the wrong enquiries? Can the team not update content? Has the service offering changed while the navigation stayed the same?

Define one or more business goals, such as attracting more relevant quotation requests, explaining a service more clearly, reducing repetitive support questions, publishing vacancies more easily or serving a new market segment. Connect each goal to observable behaviour on the site. This allows you to assess whether the update is moving in the right direction without treating one conversion rate as absolute truth.

<NoticeBox variant="tip" title="Make the diagnosis measurable">
For each problem, record who experiences it, where it occurs, what evidence you have and what a noticeable improvement would look like. This keeps the redesign from becoming a discussion about taste alone.
</NoticeBox>

## When targeted optimisation may be enough

Optimisation is appropriate when the technical foundation is healthy, the URL structure remains useful and the content management system does not block your plans. You preserve existing value and can learn more quickly which intervention has an effect.

Typical examples include an unclear homepage, weak service copy, outdated photography or forms with too many fields. Local performance issues may also be solvable without rebuilding the entire website.

## When a redesign or rebuild becomes more sensible

A broader update makes sense when problems reinforce one another: the site no longer reflects the offer, important content is trapped in awkward templates and every small change needs a technical workaround. Outdated dependencies can also make continued patching unwise.

A visual redesign mainly changes presentation and interaction within a largely workable structure. A rebuild also replaces templates, technology or the platform. In practice, these projects can overlap. Let the audit determine the decision, not the label used in a quotation.

If the technology still works but your content scope has changed, compare a [one-page website with a full website](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/one-page-or-full-website/). Sometimes fewer pages and tighter editing are best; sometimes distinct routes are necessary. Use the [website cost guide](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/website-development-costs/) to compare quotations by scope. For an ecommerce site, include product data, payments and operations with the [SME ecommerce guide](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/online-shop-development-for-smes/).

## Establish a baseline before changing anything

Without a baseline, you cannot tell whether a post-launch difference comes from the new design, seasonality, a campaign or a measurement error. Gather data over a representative period and note unusual events.

Look beyond total traffic. Which landing pages attract relevant visitors? Which queries and external links lead to the site? Where do people start and abandon forms? Which devices do they use? Which pages do support teams share because they already explain something well?

Analytics never tells the whole story. Combine quantitative data with insights from sales, support and website managers.

## Inventory content before drawing the new sitemap

A redesign is an opportunity to tidy up, but removing content blindly can discard useful answers, external links and organic entry points. Inventory pages, downloads, forms, media, metadata, structured data and important internal links.

Assign each item a decision: preserve, update, merge, replace, archive or remove. Record its owner, audience, traffic and dependencies. A page may be reached through advertising, downloads and knowledge-base articles, so that context matters.

New content takes time. Plan interviews, approval and image production before development stalls on empty templates. Do not discard proven content simply because it looks dated; it may work again in a better structure.

## URL mapping and redirects protect established routes

Keep useful URLs unchanged when only design or technology changes. Every unnecessary URL change adds migration work and risk. When URLs do change, create an old-to-new map. Each old page usually needs its own relevant successor; several pages can point to one destination when their content has genuinely been merged there.

Google recommends accurate URL mapping, thorough testing and redirects for a site move. For a permanent move, [Google Search Central recommends server-side 301 or 308 redirects](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/crawling-indexing/301-redirects). Update internal links directly and, following the [site move guidance](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/crawling-indexing/site-move-with-url-changes), retain redirects for at least a year in most cases. Keeping them longer can still help visitors. Sending unrelated old pages to the homepage is not a content map.

Google notes that visibility can fluctuate temporarily during a site move and that processing takes time. Plan expectations, monitoring and timing accordingly.

## Preserve SEO without promising guarantees

SEO preservation is a risk-reduction process. Alongside URLs, record titles, headings, indexation rules, canonicals, hreflang, structured data, internal links and important content. The [technical SEO checklist](/en/kennisbank/seo-geo/technical-seo-checklist/) helps make the inventory complete. Check that the new version answers the same search intent at least as clearly.

A cleaner design must not hide useful answers. Make core answers easy to scan and details logically accessible. [Building SEO into the website from the start](/en/diensten/web-design/seo-ready-website/) makes these choices part of design.

No agency can guarantee unchanged rankings. Results also change because of competitors, algorithms, demand and the content itself. A sound migration plan helps search engines understand the relationship between old and new, and makes mistakes easier to detect.

## Align content, media and brand again

A technically modern site can still feel dated because of old copy and generic imagery. Review claims, evidence and visual language. Are services, team information, regions and case studies current? Do the photographs represent the quality you deliver today?

Plan images around the new page goals and agree on alt text, licensing and file management. Answer real customer questions directly and place CTAs where visitors have enough context. A new website does not need to be louder. It needs to explain more quickly who you serve, what problem you solve and which next step is appropriate.

## Accessibility is not a final check

A redesign changes colour, typography, navigation, forms and interactions, precisely the elements that determine accessibility. Include semantic headings, sufficient contrast, visible focus, keyboard operation, clear errors and understandable labels from the design stage.

The [Web Content Accessibility Guidelines 2.2](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/) organise testable criteria around perceivability, operability, understandability and robustness. Seek legal advice where necessary about obligations that apply to your organisation. Automated scanners do not find everything, so test core journeys with a keyboard, different screen sizes, zoom and real users.

## Test performance on templates and real pages

A new technology stack is not automatically fast. Large hero images, animations, trackers, external fonts and poorly configured scripts can slow a modern site. Set a performance budget and test with realistic content, not an empty component library.

Google uses Core Web Vitals to describe loading, responsiveness and visual stability. Its [Core Web Vitals guidance](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/core-web-vitals) also makes clear that good scores alone do not guarantee high rankings. Performance is a quality goal, not an isolated SEO trick. Read [why a fast website can rank better](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/why-a-fast-website-can-rank-better/) for the wider context.

## The first weeks after launch

After launch, check server errors, 404s, redirects, indexation signals, form delivery and core journeys. Compare them with the baseline, but do not draw sweeping conclusions after one day.

Prioritise issues that block access, contact or indexation, then friction in important journeys, and only then cosmetic refinements. Keep a change log. Once intensive aftercare ends, establish a [website maintenance](/en/diensten/web-design/website-maintenance/) routine for updates, backups, monitoring, content reviews and form tests.

## Common mistakes when redesigning a website

One mistake is treating a redesign as a superficial visual makeover. New colours and larger images do not fix an unclear offer, disorganised content or unworkable management. The opposite mistake is replacing everything even though valuable URLs, copy and processes work well.

Use real content, let staff test the CMS and check redirects before launch. Do not postpone analytics and privacy configuration.

<NoticeBox variant="warning" title="Never remove old pages blindly">
A visually unimpressive page may still attract relevant visitors, hold external links or answer an important customer question. Assess its function and data before deciding to preserve, improve, merge or deliberately remove it.
</NoticeBox>

## Replace what obstructs progress and protect what works

A good website redesign starts with respect for existing value. Data, customer questions, strong pages and workable processes deserve a place in the new whole. At the same time, you need to replace what is structurally counterproductive.
