# Turn training into video content: from course day to modular knowledge product

<LeadIntro>
**Turn training into video content** by redistributing its learning objectives, explanations, demonstrations and exercises across small modules that work independently. Do not automatically film the complete training day. First establish what learners must understand or perform without a trainer, then choose the right medium for each element and plan publication, measurement and updates from the outset.
</LeadIntro>

An existing training course is a valuable source. The trainer knows which practical questions arise, has relevant examples and understands where participants usually struggle. Yet a live course is not a ready-made video script. In the room, the trainer can adjust the pace, explain a term again and notice when an exercise is not working. An online learner does not have that immediate correction.

Digitisation therefore starts with selection and redesign. Some elements become short explanatory videos, while others become demonstrations, downloads, exercises or live follow-up sessions. The result may be a complete online course, an internal video library, an onboarding pathway or an addition to classroom training.

<QuoteBlock>
Preserve the knowledge and practical experience in your training, but redesign the learning path for someone who is not sitting beside the trainer.
</QuoteBlock>

## What does turning training into video content involve?

It means restructuring existing teaching materials into purposeful digital elements that learners can use independently or within a blended programme. The work includes more than recording and editing. You also define the sequence, prior knowledge, exercises, accessibility, publication and ownership of updates.

The right format follows the task. A stable foundational explanation may serve as a core module for years. A software demonstration may need rerecording more often. A conversation about practical cases may work better as a recurring live session. A complex skill requires practice and feedback; watching alone is not enough.

## Step 1: audit the existing training content

Collect slides, trainer notes, workbooks, assignments, assessments, sample files and recurring learner questions. Record explanations that currently exist only in spoken form. For each element, identify the learning objective it supports, the prior knowledge it requires and how stable the content is.

Do not remove repetition blindly. In a live session, repetition may reconnect the group after a break. Online, a short recap, chapter marker or exercise may serve the same purpose better. Conversely, an informal trainer remark may contain exactly the practical context missing from the slides.

Label content as core, advanced, example, exercise, support or temporary. Add an expected shelf life: stable, annual review, or update when a product, interface or rule changes. This combination determines both module structure and the maintenance plan.

## Step 2: write learning objectives that direct the module

A useful objective describes what learners can recognise, choose, explain, perform or check after the module. "Gain insight into safety" is too broad to guide a script or exercise. "Identify three risks in a sample situation and select a suitable control measure" provides direction.

Connect each objective to evidence. That might be a decision in a scenario, a brief explanation in the learner's own words, a completed action or a submitted piece of work. Not every objective needs automatic assessment in the learning platform. Do distinguish between information transfer and the points where practice, human feedback or facilitation remain necessary.

The [CAST UDL Guidelines](https://udlguidelines.cast.org/) provide a framework for reducing barriers and considering several forms of engagement, representation and action. This does not require a separate course for every learner. It helps prevent the assumption that one spoken video is enough for every person and every learning goal.

## Step 3: divide the training into replaceable modules

A module covers one coherent step with a recognisable beginning and end. It explains why the subject matters, which prior knowledge is needed, what the learner must do and where to go next. The task determines an appropriate length; there is no universal number of minutes that works for every lesson.

Give modules stable codes and names. In video, refer to "the Risk Analysis module" rather than "the previous video", because the order may change later. Separate rapidly changing details from stable principles. This lets you replace a software demonstration without reproducing the introduction, practical case and exercise.

<NoticeBox variant="tip" title="Design for maintenance before recording">
Split a module when its elements have different owners, update frequencies or audiences. A product setting that changes quarterly does not necessarily belong in the same video as a decision framework that remains stable for years.
</NoticeBox>

A practical module card records at least the objective, core question, format, estimated duration, required assets, exercise, owner and review date. It also becomes the bridge between subject expert, video team and LMS administrator.

## Step 4: choose video, text, exercises or live contact by module

Video works well for demonstrations, timing, voice, presence and processes that need to be seen. Text is often better for exact reference information, links, definitions and steps people need to scan quickly while working. A downloadable checklist can sit beside a video. A skill usually needs an assignment and feedback.

Keep live contact where nuance, coaching or exchange is central. A blended programme can provide stable basic knowledge beforehand and use contact time for cases, questions and application. If uncertain, compare an [online course and live training](/en/knowledge-base/masterclasses/online-course-vs-live-training/).

An existing workshop serves another purpose. If you mainly want to document the live moment, speaker and several applications, [filming a workshop professionally](/en/knowledge-base/masterclasses/filming-a-workshop-professionally/) is a better fit than completely rebuilding it as an independent course.

## Step 5: rewrite trainer materials as a script and visual plan

Slides are prompts for a live trainer, not an automatic video script. For each module, write an opening, central explanation, example, summary and next step. Also record what needs to appear: the speaker, an object, a slide, a screen, a detail shot or a simple graphic. The principles in [structuring a corporate video script](/en/knowledge-base/videography/how-to-structure-a-corporate-video-script/) are useful here, although the goal is learning rather than persuasion.

Use a complete script when definitions, legal wording, safety or translation require precision. An outline may sound more natural for an experienced trainer. Demonstrations need a separate action plan with materials, camera position, screen state, error scenario and checkpoint.

Remove room-specific phrases such as "you can see on the slide" or "discuss this with the person beside you" when the digital learner lacks that context. Replace them with a specific instruction: where to look, what to record and how to check the answer.

## Step 6: test one pilot module with real users

Before full production, create one representative module. Do not choose a simple welcome video, but a lesson combining explanation, imagery, an exercise and LMS display. Ask several people from the target audience to complete it without additional guidance. Observe where they stop, rewind, misunderstand a term or perform the task differently from what was intended.

A pilot also answers production questions. How long does content approval take? Are slides legible on mobile? Do captions represent technical terms correctly? Does the download work? How much editing does one module require? Use these answers to refine the template, script and schedule before repeating an error across the full series.

## Step 7: build accessibility into production

Accessibility begins in the script. Name essential visual actions rather than saying only "click here". Use sufficient contrast, readable text and meaning that does not depend on colour alone. Structure downloads logically and make sure the video player supports keyboard operation.

The W3C guide to [accessible audio and video](https://www.w3.org/WAI/media/av/) covers captions, transcripts and audio description. The exact provision depends on the content and use. At minimum, provide accurate captions for spoken information and have automatic transcription checked for specialist terms, proper names, numbers and negations.

[WCAG 2.2](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/) contains testable web accessibility criteria. The course experience includes more than its video page, however. Test login, navigation, quizzes, downloads, error messages and completion too. An accessible video file inside an inaccessible learning environment does not solve the complete user journey.

## Step 8: publish in an LMS that fits the learning path

Choose a learning management system around access, roles, sequence, exercises, feedback, reporting, privacy, integrations and maintenance. Before final exports, confirm supported video formats, caption files, file sizes and player functions.

Test with a genuine learner account, not only as an administrator. Review enrolment, resuming, mobile display, downloads, captions, assignment submission, support and completion. Administrators often see features and content hidden from learners.

Moodle describes [course completion](https://docs.moodle.org/502/en/Course_completion) as a status based on criteria configured in the course. These might include viewing an activity or achieving a score. Its meaning therefore follows your configuration. "Completed" does not automatically prove that someone has retained the skill.

<NoticeBox variant="warning" title="Define what completed means">
Record whether a module is complete after it is opened, viewed in full, followed by a task or followed by feedback. Use the status only for the meaning that you have genuinely configured and checked.
</NoticeBox>

## Step 9: manage rights, privacy and version control

Review rights for slides, images, video excerpts, music, software screens, guest contributions and workbooks. The [FPS Economy copyright FAQ](https://economie.fgov.be/sites/default/files/Files/Publications/files/FAQ_Auteursrecht.pdf) gives general copyright information. For the project, record where content may appear, for how long, in which languages and whether promotional excerpts are included.

An existing recording may show learners, customer names, chats, screen notifications or confidential cases. The Belgian Data Protection Authority's information on [image rights](https://www.gegevensbeschermingsautoriteit.be/burger/thema-s/recht-op-afbeelding/principes) distinguishes recording from further use. Where possible, use test data, camera-free zones and framing that avoids unnecessary personal data. Seek legal advice where the specific context requires it.

Give every export a module code and version number. Store the approved script, caption source, assets and publication date together. This reveals which version is in the LMS and which learners may need notification after a substantive change.

## Step 10: measure use and application without overpromising

Before publication, decide which question the data should answer. Viewing behaviour can show where learners stop or return. A knowledge question can expose a misconception. A practical task can show how someone performs a step. None of these signals proves by itself that the training caused revenue, safety or performance outcomes.

Moodle's [course reports](https://docs.moodle.org/en/Course_reports) documentation provides examples covering activity, participation and completion. Other platforms use different definitions. Record internally what each metric means, which data quality is required and who acts on the signals.

## Turn one course into several useful content formats

Modular production enables responsible reuse. A core explanation can sit in the LMS, a short derivative clip can answer one question and a transcript can provide reference material. Preserve context and rights: a standalone excerpt must make sense independently and must not omit a crucial warning or condition.

Plan derivative content before recording. Record openings and endings separately where useful, leave room for vertical crops and retain source files for graphics. This is more efficient than trying to cut a long lesson randomly into social clips afterwards. [Short-form versus long-form video](/en/knowledge-base/videography/short-form-vs-long-form-video/) helps select those formats.

## Maintain the course as a product, not an archive

Assign a content owner and next review date to every module. Add triggers for interim review, such as a software update, new regulation, changed product or recurring support question. An annual calendar is not enough for material that can change sooner.

Maintain a changelog listing the module, old and new version, reason, impact and publication date. Decide when a minor correction is sufficient, when a video needs rerecording and when learners must be notified. Archive old versions carefully so they do not continue circulating through forgotten links.

This is where modularisation creates its greatest benefit: a changed interface or procedure does not require the entire course to be filmed again. That advantage depends on orderly management of scripts, files, ownership and publication from the beginning.

## From existing training to a managed video library

Turning training into video content succeeds when learners independently understand where to begin, what to do and where to find support. The organisation must also know who maintains the content, how use is measured and when a module needs updating.

Do not start by booking as many production days as possible. Start with a content audit, module card and pilot. This small, complete chain reveals the design decisions, technology and facilitation the programme needs. You can then expand production in a controlled way and reuse the material responsibly later.
