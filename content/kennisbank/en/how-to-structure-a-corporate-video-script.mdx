# How to structure a corporate video script: from core message to shoot-ready plan

<LeadIntro>
A workable **corporate video script** starts with one viewer question, one intended next step and concrete evidence. For each scene, specify what viewers see, hear and need to understand. Use natural spoken language, plan interviews as focused conversations and connect every important point to a visible process, person, product or result. Your script then becomes a practical plan for visuals, sound and editing, rather than a long piece of advertising copy.
</LeadIntro>

A corporate video rarely begins with a blank document. Website copy, presentations, sales pitches and ideas from several colleagues already exist. The challenge is not fitting everything into the video. It is deciding what the right viewer needs from this particular video. Without that choice, the script becomes an inventory of services, values and slogans. It may sound comprehensive, yet nobody knows what to show or what the viewer should do next.

This guide builds on [why invest in a corporate video](/en/kennisbank/videografie/why-invest-in-a-corporate-video/). Here, we take the practical next step from objective and evidence to structure, scenes, interviews, accessibility and approval. If you prefer to develop the process with a crew, explore our [corporate video production](/en/services/videography/corporate-video/) service.

<QuoteBlock>
A script becomes cinematic when viewers can grasp its core message from what happens on screen, not only from what a voice claims.
</QuoteBlock>

## What should a good corporate video script achieve?

A script must serve three groups at once. The organisation needs to verify the message, facts and tone. The production crew needs to know which people, locations, actions and shots are required. Viewers need to understand why the story matters without prior knowledge. Beautifully written copy that cannot be filmed therefore fulfils only one third of the task.

Do not work from a spoken-copy column alone. For each section, record at least its purpose, expected visual, audio and source of evidence. If someone says quality comes first, which inspection process will you show? If a client saves time, which step demonstrably becomes simpler? If the team offers a personal service, which genuine interaction makes that credible?

## Start with one viewer question and one action

First, write down the question someone has just before pressing play. A prospective client may want to know whether you can handle complex projects. A candidate may want to meet the team and understand the workplace. A buyer may be looking for evidence of a reliable working relationship. A short narrative cannot answer all those questions equally well.

Now write one sentence in this form: "After this video, [audience] understands that [core insight], because we show [evidence], and can take [next step]." This is not voice-over copy. It is a filter for every suggestion. A scene that adds nothing to the insight, evidence or action receives a lower priority.

The action does not always have to be a request for a quotation. Viewers might open a case study, view a vacancy, book a meeting or visit a specific product page. Choose the action that fits their stage and the publication context.

## What is the difference between a concept, script, storyboard and production brief?

These documents are often confused. The concept describes the central creative idea: from which perspective will you tell the story, and why does it fit the brand? The script turns that idea into a sequence of visuals, spoken content, text and sound. A storyboard visualises key frames or transitions. The production brief organises times, locations, people and logistics.

Not every SME production needs a storyboard for every shot. An interview-led documentary may need only a scene table, visual references and a prioritised shot list. A tightly directed promotional video involving actors, effects or product movement is more likely to require planned frames and timing. Let production risk determine the documentation instead of treating one template as compulsory.

<NoticeBox variant="tip" title="Work broadly first, then add precision">
Approve the concept and story arc before developing every word and shot. Otherwise, the team will debate commas while the fundamental choices about audience or evidence remain unresolved.
</NoticeBox>

## How do you collect evidence for the core message?

A business knows its own offer so well that abstract language can feel self-explanatory. To an outsider, words such as "quality", "complete service" and "tailored" mean little without context. Interview internal experts, salespeople, project leads and those who work directly with clients first. Ask not only what goes well, but which concerns clients have beforehand and which step pleasantly surprises them later.

Create an evidence inventory. It might include a visible quality check, a working prototype, a specific method, specialist knowledge, a before-and-after situation or a client speaking from experience. Avoid figures that cannot be checked internally and kept current. A credible process shot is stronger than a spectacular claim with no traceable source.

## Which story structure works for a corporate video?

There is no universal format, but viewers do need a clear path. A practical structure begins with recognition, establishes what matters, shows how the organisation acts and ends with an appropriate next step. It may unfold chronologically, through a client case or across one working day. The sequence should follow the viewer's question, not the organisational chart.

A compact model has five functions. Open with a relevant situation or tension. Provide enough context to understand the subject. Introduce the approach without reading out a service list. Prove that approach through people, actions or results. Finish with the next logical moment. Each function does not require its own line of voice-over. One strong image can perform several jobs.

## How do you write an opening without a hollow attention trick?

The first scene does not need to be loud, but it must be relevant. Start with a recognisable detail of the problem, an unexpected view of a process, a pointed client question or a statement that immediately gains context. "Welcome to our company" spends valuable attention on information the logo and page title often provide already. A substantive opening shows why watching further is worthwhile.

Write two or three openings and test them outside the project team. Does someone understand within a few sentences who the video is for? Does the image raise a question that the story answers honestly? Does the tone fit the rest? Avoid a spectacular promise that the video cannot support. The opening and the evidence must belong to the same story.

Plan for silent or low-volume viewing at the same time. On social channels, someone may encounter the opening seconds without usable audio. Make sure the image and brief on-screen copy offer enough context without squeezing the entire story into small text. Our related guide to [social media video for SMEs](/en/kennisbank/videografie/social-media-video-for-smes/) explains these channel choices in more detail.

## How do you write spoken copy that sounds natural?

Copy that reads well on paper can sound awkward aloud. Write in short units of thought, use words the speaker would choose and read every version aloud. Remove subordinate clauses that belong in a brochure. Give key terms room and avoid forcing a complete service list into one breath.

Let speakers communicate meaning rather than chase a flawless delivery. A business owner does not need to become an advertising voice. A teleprompter can help when precise legal, technical or safety information must be reproduced word for word. Allow time for rehearsal, a calm reading pace and natural pauses. A focused interview often works better when personal conviction matters.

## When should you choose interviews, and when should you use voice-over?

Use interviews when the person is part of the evidence. A technician can explain why an inspection matters, an employee can make the working culture tangible and a client can speak from personal experience. Voice-over works well for clarifying time, place or a transition, but it should not replace all human content.

Do not script a complete interview answer. Decide which content block you need from each person and use open questions that invite examples. Ask, "What did the client notice before the change?" or "Which decision made the difference here?" Then follow up on context, action and result. Avoid questions that produce only "yes" or the company slogan.

A client story requires additional agreements about selection, claims and use. Our separate guide to [creating a testimonial video](/en/kennisbank/videografie/creating-a-testimonial-video/) covers that preparation in more detail.

## How do you turn the story into a scene table?

Give each scene four columns: function, visuals, audio and production. The function records why the scene exists. Under visuals, describe an action or observable moment, not merely "office atmosphere". Under audio, record an interview line, voice-over, ambient sound or silence. Production covers the person, location, props, timing and constraints.

For example, the function might be "prove a reliable handover". The scene shows a project lead completing the final checks with the client. The audio combines a brief explanation of what is being inspected with the real sound of the space. Production records which client will attend, which documents must not reveal personal data and when the location is available.

Mark each scene as essential, desirable or reserve. On a busy filming day, the director can then make choices without losing the story's backbone. Include transition shots too: hands preparing materials, a detail of the finish, a vehicle arriving or an on-screen action without confidential data. These give the edit room to breathe.

## How do you plan one shoot for several video versions?

A main video and its shorter derivatives share building blocks, but do not automatically share the same framing or opening. Record in advance which statements must work independently, which scenes need a vertical frame and where captions or titles need space. Capture concise, complete answers that do not depend on an unheard question.

Give each planned version a small objective. A short social clip can address one objection. A sales extract can explain one process step. A vacancy page can introduce an employee. If every extract tries to summarise the entire business, you end up with several weak copies of the main video.

Choosing between running time and detail deserves its own assessment. Read [short-form versus long-form video](/en/kennisbank/videografie/short-form-vs-long-form-video/) for that decision. Above all, the script should be modular enough to select the right content for each context.

<NoticeBox variant="success" title="Write answers that can be edited">
Ask interviewees to name the subject in their answer. "During the handover, we check three points together" remains useful on its own. "That is how we do it" loses its meaning when the question is removed.
</NoticeBox>

## How do you build accessibility into the script?

Accessibility costs less and works better when planned before filming. The [WCAG explanation of captions for prerecorded media](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/Understanding/captions-prerecorded) describes captions as text for spoken and relevant non-speech audio. Note who is speaking, which sounds carry meaning and how specialist terms are spelled. After editing, provide a checked caption file or a suitable burned-in version for the publication channel.

Automatic transcription is a starting point, not the final check. [YouTube advises creators](https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/6373554?hl=en) to review automatically generated captions because pronunciation, accents and background noise can cause errors. Names, technical terms, locations and product names are particularly vulnerable in SME videos.

Where possible, work important visual information naturally into the spoken track. W3C describes this as integrated description in its guidance on [description of visual information](https://www.w3.org/WAI/media/av/description/). "We measure this joint before installation" provides context even without the image. When crucial information remains visual only, a descriptive transcript or audio description may be necessary, depending on the content and intended level of accessibility.

## Which rights and permissions should the script flag?

A scene table is a useful place to identify rights risks. Mark recognisable employees, clients, visitors, artworks, screens, music, locations and third-party brands. The Belgian Data Protection Authority stresses in its guidance on [image rights](https://gegevensbeschermingsautoriteit.be/burger/thema-s/recht-op-afbeelding/principes) that permission to record someone does not automatically include permission to publish or distribute the recording. Document the purpose, channels, duration and responsible party, and obtain legal advice for specific exceptions.

For music, archive footage, illustrations and existing material, "found online" does not amount to a right of use. The [Copyright FAQ from FPS Economy](https://economie.fgov.be/sites/default/files/Files/Publications/files/FAQ-auteursrecht.pdf) provides general information on protected material and licences, including for promotional video. Agree who buys the licences, which channels and territories they cover, and what happens if a campaign is extended later.

## How do you organise feedback without losing control of the script?

Appoint one content owner to consolidate feedback. Review strategy and structure first, then facts and phrasing, and only then the production version and on-screen copy. If management, marketing, HR and sales each rewrite the same document independently, internal preferences will obscure the primary viewer.

Ask every reviewer to connect comments to a reason: factually incorrect, unsuitable for the audience, a legal risk, impractical to produce or inconsistent with the brand voice. "This sentence does not feel right" is not enough to start a new direction. Track version numbers and keep one approved source version for the crew and editor.

## How do you use the script during filming and editing?

On filming day, the script is a compass, not a theatre script. The crew checks that the essential content and footage have been captured, while remaining alert to a stronger real action or turn of phrase. Note when an answer departs from the script and proves more effective. The editor can then see which take fulfils the original function.

Assign someone to monitor continuity in facts, clothing, props and screens. Gather extra footage once the essential scenes are safe. Record ambient sound and calm transition shots. For interviews, a content log helps identify strong answers and claims that still need checking.

During editing, do not ask whether every scripted word survived. Ask whether the viewer can follow the promised line. A look, action or client statement may replace an entire voice-over passage. Make sure, however, that selecting only the most energetic quotes does not remove essential context.

## How do you measure and improve a corporate video script?

Tie evaluation to the video's objective. For a website video, check whether the right visitors find the relevant next step and ask well-informed questions. In recruitment, suitable applications and recurring candidate questions are more useful than views alone. In sales, the team can record which objections the video resolves in advance and where further explanation is still needed.

Do not infer causation from one metric. A video works alongside the page, offer, traffic, placement and follow-up. Compare versions only when their contexts are sufficiently similar, and record qualitative signals. If viewers repeatedly leave before the evidence appears, the opening may be too broad. If clients still ask the same basic question afterwards, the video may lack context or introduce the call to action too soon.

When publishing a video on its own page, give it relevant written context. Google's [video SEO best practices](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/video) emphasise discoverable embeds, valid thumbnails, stable URLs and unique information, among other requirements. A script can prepare a clear title, description, transcript and chapter structure, but it cannot guarantee a video result or ranking.

## From corporate video script to a production that works

A good script does not restrict creativity. It ensures that creative choices solve a clear problem. Begin with one viewer's question, identify the available evidence and build a storyline in which visuals, voice and sound complement one another. Turn that storyline into scenes that are practical to organise, safe to film and easy to edit into the required versions later.

Keep applying one test throughout the process: what does the viewer understand here that would remain unclear without this scene? If there is no answer, the scene can probably be shorter, more specific or removed altogether. That leaves room for people, rhythm and genuine moments without losing essential content.

A strong corporate video script does not dictate every image. It clarifies which evidence the camera needs to find and why it matters to the viewer.
