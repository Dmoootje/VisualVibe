# Drone video for construction projects: progress, case studies and safe planning

<LeadIntro>
**Drone video works best when each flight records a defined story or comparison point.** Use repeatable routes for progress, separate cinematic shots for marketing, and ground footage for people, materials and detail. Coordinate every visit with site management, let the operator assess current flight conditions, and never treat marketing footage as a substitute for a technical inspection or formal site report.
</LeadIntro>

From the air, site layout, volumes, logistics and the relationship between different parts of a project become easier to understand. A random monthly flight rarely creates a useful timeline, however. Changes in altitude, direction, lens and light make two recording dates difficult to compare. A spectacular move may also be unusable if it crosses an active work zone or serves no clear purpose in the edit.

This guide brings communication, production and safety together for contractors, developers and project teams. Its regulatory information is for orientation only. It is not legal advice, an operational assessment or approval for a particular flight.

A construction drone video becomes valuable when viewers not only see that work is happening, but understand the progress, scale and expertise the images demonstrate.
</QuoteBlock>

## Decide whether you need progress, marketing or documentation

The phrase "construction site video" can conceal three different assignments. Periodic progress communication compares recognisable project phases. A marketing film tells a persuasive story about craftsmanship, collaboration and the result. Internal documentation records a condition at a particular time so that it can be found again later. One flight can produce material for several purposes, but only when the brief names those purposes separately.

If the assignment requires technical inspection, surveying, volume calculations or formal evidence, it needs a separate method and suitably qualified expertise. A polished 4K edit does not automatically make its source material fit for those purposes.

## When does a drone add value on a construction site?

A drone is especially useful when scale and context are hard to read from ground level. Examples include multi-block housing, an industrial site, infrastructure, a roof renovation or phased landscaping. A fluid movement can show how access, the working area and a new volume relate to one another.

For a small interior renovation, installation detail or finishing work, a ground camera is normally more valuable. On a densely built site, the desired flight path may add little information or be operationally unsuitable. The right answer may be a tripod, a controlled elevated ground position or focused [project photography](/en/services/photography/project-photography/).

## Design a repeatable image set for progress updates

Repeatability takes discipline. For every reference shot, record the position, altitude, direction, focal length, movement, start frame and end frame. Note the intended time and project phase as well. Automated flight options do not release the operator from checking the site, geozone and safety conditions afresh at every session.

A useful fixed set normally contains a broad establishing shot, several oblique views of important areas and one or two movements that explain spatial relationships. On each visit, add only temporary phase shots that will remain meaningful later, such as foundations, shell construction, facade closure, technical services, landscaping or commissioning.

Keep a reference frame with the next briefing. "Fly along the north side again" is too vague if the starting point or lens changes. Store the recording date in the archive metadata; it does not necessarily need to appear prominently throughout the marketing edit.

<NoticeBox variant="info" title="Consistent does not mean identical">
A site changes continually. Cranes, scaffolding, barriers and safe launch points may make an earlier route impossible. Preserve the comparison goal and document why the viewpoint or movement changed.
</NoticeBox>

## Use a different shot list for marketing

Progress footage records; marketing footage tells a story. A case study needs a beginning, development and end. A drone can introduce the location and scale, but craftsmanship often becomes visible only in a close-up of the work, an interview with the project lead or a finished detail.

<ChecklistBlock title="Shot list for a construction case study" items={[

A [corporate video](/en/services/videography/corporate-video/) can carry the complete case study. Aerial footage is one perspective within it, not a separate chapter that must keep returning.

## Link recording dates to irreversible construction phases

A fixed monthly appointment is easy to administer, but it is not always meaningful. A critical roof closure, lifting operation or facade installation may fall between two calendar visits. Set a baseline rhythm and a list of project milestones that site management and production can coordinate in good time.

Choose only milestones that can be demonstrated visually and recorded responsibly. A complex or hazardous operation does not have to be followed live from the air to feature in the case study. Preparation, a safe moment before or after the operation and an expert explanation may show the process more honestly and clearly.

## Coordinate every flight with site and safety management

A construction site is not a film set. Routes, deliveries, lifting zones, machinery, scaffolding and access rules change. The operator needs current information but remains responsible for deciding how the flight can be conducted safely within the applicable framework. Site management controls site operations and can rule out areas or time windows.

Appoint one contact who knows the day's programme. Discuss the launch and landing point, uninvolved people, planned crane movements, radio or communication arrangements and stop criteria. Never ask workers to repeat an unsafe action "for the camera", and never remove collective protection to improve a composition.

## Match the aircraft and movement to the communication goal

A larger camera may offer different creative options, but weight, aircraft class, location and nearby people affect what is operationally possible. "Use the best drone" is not a useful technical requirement. Define the result, then let the operator select a suitable and permitted approach.

For progress updates, calm repeatable movements matter more than maximum speed. A reveal, lateral movement or controlled approach can work for a cinematic case study, provided the beginning and end are usable in the edit. FPV is a separate form with different route and safety questions, so do not order a dynamic route through a site as though it were ordinary drone video.

Record enough footage before and after the key movement. An editor needs breathing space for transitions, text and different pacing. Where responsible, repeat essential shots, but do not let variations displace the agreed must-haves.

## Respect the European and Belgian drone framework

European rules classify operations by risk rather than by recreational or commercial purpose. EASA's [Open category guide](https://www.easa.europa.eu/en/domains/drones-air-mobility/operating-drone/open-category-low-risk-civil-drones) explains how A1, A2 and A3 differ according to factors including the aircraft, pilot competency and distance from people.

For Open operations, [FPS Mobility](https://mobilit.belgium.be/nl/luchtvaart/vliegen-met/drones-uas/categorieen/categorie-open) lists core conditions including no flight over an assembly of people, a maximum of 120 metres above ground level, direct visual line of sight, no dropping of material and compliance with geozones. A site fence does not automatically make everyone beneath the route involved, or make every Open subcategory suitable.

The current [Belgian summary table](https://mobilit.belgium.be/nl/luchtvaart/vliegen-met/drones-uas/categorieen/categorie-open/overzichtstabel) connects subcategory, aircraft class, mass, training, registration, Remote ID and geo-awareness. If the planned operation does not fit Open, a different approach or a process within Specific may be required. Only an assessment of the actual operation and the required official steps can settle that question.

<NoticeBox variant="danger" title="This guide does not approve a construction-site flight">
A site safety plan and the client's permission do not replace aviation rules. Conversely, a flight that is acceptable under aviation rules does not replace site, privacy or location agreements. Bring both frameworks together before every recording session. This is not legal advice.
</NoticeBox>

## Check registration, insurance and Droneguide

The [general information from FPS Mobility](https://mobilit.belgium.be/nl/luchtvaart/vliegen-met/drones-uas/algemene-informatie/algemene-informatie) describes operator registration, insurance, training, aircraft checks and geozones as practical preparation. Registration is generally required for a non-toy camera drone, even if it weighs less than 250 grams. The applicable insurance must suit the intended operation.

Check [Droneguide](https://map.droneguide.be/) again for every project visit. A long-running construction commission is not permanent confirmation that geographical UAS zones or access conditions will remain the same. Where zones overlap, their additional requirements apply cumulatively.

Start this check before communicating a recording date externally. Applications, geozone coordination or a different operational approach take time. Keep a ground-based alternative ready so that a stakeholder update need not stop when the drone flight cannot proceed.

## Protect people, neighbours and project information

An overview can record workers, number plates, neighbouring gardens, safety documents, installations and confidential project details. Restrict the route and focal length to the communication purpose. Do not let raw footage circulate unmanaged among every project partner.

The [EASA Privacy Handbook](https://www.easa.europa.eu/en/domains/civil-drones/privacy/privacy-handbook) addresses privacy and data protection specifically for professional drone operations. Agree who determines the purpose, access, selection, retention and publication. Inform identifiable participants where appropriate and check people captured incidentally outside the site as well.

A commercial case study can also reveal contractually sensitive information, such as client names, techniques, delivery flows or parts of a design before public release. Ask the client, project lead and communications owner to review the facts and approve publication. A privacy check is not the same as permission to disclose project information.

## Combine aerial video with ground footage, interviews and sound

Air footage shows where something happens; ground footage and explanation show how and why. A case study can open with a recognisable overview, let the project lead define the challenge, show genuine work at ground level and return to a comparable aerial viewpoint for the result.

Record interviews in a quieter, safe area. A noisy site makes speech harder to understand and can interfere with safety communication. Captions help both silent social viewing and accessibility, but a poor source recording remains poor source material.

Music and fast editing must not distort the project chronology. Use a timeline label or voice-over when footage from different months appears in one sequence, so viewers understand that they are not watching a single continuous working day.

## Create a separate edit for each audience

A management update, project case study and social teaser need different pacing and information density. Build one organised source archive and create purposeful exports, rather than one generic edit that works approximately everywhere. The master can carry context and explanation; shorter versions choose one message and direct viewers to the full case study.

Agree revision rounds, colour finishing, music licensing, usage term and partner distribution in advance. Loose raw footage is not automatically colour-corrected, privacy-checked or cleared for public distribution.

## Publish accessible, technically controlled video

Video that slows a page down or conveys information through sound alone excludes visitors. Provide a poster image, compressed source and suitable resolutions. Do not autoplay a decorative hero video with loud sound, and provide controls when the video carries information.

Under the [WCAG 2.2 explanation of captions for prerecorded media](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/Understanding/captions-prerecorded), synchronised media should provide a text alternative for speech and meaningful non-speech audio, unless it is clearly an alternative to existing text. Captions can therefore require relevant sounds and speaker identification, not merely a transcript of the interview.

Place the key facts in ordinary web copy beside the video. A search engine, screen reader or visitor with limited data should not need to play the complete edit to understand the project type, contribution and outcome. Video does not take alt text; use a descriptive title, surrounding context, captions and, where needed, a transcript or audio description.

## Build a usable project archive

After several visits, folders quickly fill with nearly identical names. Use a fixed structure covering project code, date, phase, camera, status and version. Keep approved exports separate from raw files, and record which shots have privacy or contractual restrictions.

A shot log can be simple. For each reference shot, a table can record direction, altitude, lens, description, changes and publication status. Link every final edit to its source selection so that later revisions are not assembled from arbitrary files.

Set a retention period and owner as well. "Keep everything forever" creates cost and unclear risk. A practical archive retains what is required for agreed reuse or proof of the communication production, then removes duplicates and unnecessary privacy-sensitive takes in line with the agreement.

## What determines budget and production scope?

Budget reflects more than flight time. A periodic commission includes the intake, definition of fixed shots, repeated site and geozone checks, site communication, travel, recording, data organisation and possibly an edit after each phase. A one-off final case study may require more ground camera work, interviews, sound and extensive post-production.

Compare quotations by outputs and responsibilities. How many visits, reference shots, phase shots, edit versions, revision rounds and usage rights are included? Who triggers a milestone visit? What happens after weather postponement or a change on site? Are travel and application costs itemised?

A restrained production can be highly useful when priorities are clear. Four fixed reference shots and a quarterly edit may create more insight than a large volume of unordered footage after every visit.
