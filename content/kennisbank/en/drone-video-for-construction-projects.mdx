# Drone video for construction projects: progress, case studies and safe planning

<LeadIntro>
**Drone video works best when each flight records a defined story or comparison point.** Use repeatable routes for progress, separate cinematic shots for marketing, and ground footage for people, materials and detail. Coordinate every visit with site management, let the operator assess current flight conditions, and never treat marketing footage as a substitute for a technical inspection or formal site report.
</LeadIntro>

From the air, site layout, volumes, logistics and the relationship between different parts of a project become easier to understand. A random monthly flight rarely creates a useful timeline, however. Changes in altitude, direction, lens and light make two recording dates difficult to compare. A spectacular move may also be unusable if it crosses an active work zone or serves no clear purpose in the edit.

This guide brings communication, production and safety together for contractors, developers and project teams. Its regulatory information is for orientation only. It is not legal advice, an operational assessment or approval for a particular flight.

## Decide whether you need progress, marketing or documentation

Start with the job the footage must do. Progress footage makes changes between agreed milestones visible. Marketing footage presents scale, craftsmanship and the finished result. Operational documentation may support internal communication, but ordinary camera footage is not a survey, technical inspection or formal site record.

## When does a drone add value on a construction site?

A drone is especially useful when scale, access, roof areas, site organisation or the relationship with the surroundings cannot be shown clearly from ground level. It can reveal the building footprint, route through a site and transition from shell to finished project in a few seconds. It adds less when the important work happens indoors or when safe separation from people and machinery cannot be maintained.

## Design a repeatable image set for progress updates

Choose a small number of reference shots before the first flight. Record the position, altitude, direction, lens, movement and approximate time of day. Repeatability matters more than novelty when the goal is comparison. Keep one wide establishing view, several views tied to major project zones, and closer details that explain the current phase.

## Use a different shot list for marketing

Progress comparison calls for consistency. Marketing calls for rhythm and a clear narrative. Begin with context, move towards the activity or finished building, and include details that prove quality. Capture horizontal master shots as well as carefully planned vertical alternatives when social content is part of the brief. Do not assume that cropping one format will preserve the same composition in another.

## Link recording dates to irreversible construction phases

Plan around milestones that will not be visible later: groundworks, structure, roofing, facade installation, technical systems and final landscaping. A fixed monthly date can help with continuity, but project milestones usually create a more meaningful story. Keep weather flexibility because wind, rain, visibility and site activity can make a planned flight unsuitable.

## Coordinate every flight with site and safety management

The operator needs a current site contact, a safe take-off and landing area, an agreed flight window and clear information about cranes, lifting operations, temporary structures, access routes and people on site. A toolbox briefing or site induction may be required. Permission from the client or site manager does not replace aviation, privacy or location requirements.

<NoticeBox variant="warning" title="A live construction site changes constantly">
An earlier risk assessment is not enough on its own. The operator must reassess the site, geozone, weather, people, machinery and safe escape options for every session.
</NoticeBox>

## Match the aircraft and movement to the communication goal

A stable camera drone suits wide views, repeatable routes and controlled cinematic movement. FPV can create an immersive route through or around a project, but requires a different plan and is not automatically appropriate for an active site. The smallest aircraft is not always the safest choice, and the largest does not automatically produce the best image. Select the system after assessing image quality, environment, legal category and risk.

## Respect the European and Belgian drone framework

Many lower-risk flights fall within the European Open category when all applicable conditions are met. The commonly stated basics include a maximum height of 120 metres above the closest point of the earth's surface and keeping the aircraft in direct visual line of sight. The subcategory, aircraft class, distance from uninvolved people and local restrictions still matter.

Rules and geozones change. Consult the current information from the Belgian Directorate-General Civil Aviation, EASA and Droneguide before each operation. This guide cannot determine whether a particular flight is permitted.

## Check registration, insurance and Droneguide

Confirm the operator and pilot requirements, aircraft identification, any training or competency requirements, insurance and the current geographical UAS zone. Droneguide may show conditions or coordination requirements for the location. A green-looking site map should never replace a complete operational check.

## Protect people, neighbours and project information

Drone images can reveal workers, neighbouring homes, vehicle registrations, security arrangements and commercially sensitive construction details. Define who may appear, what must remain confidential and where the camera may point. Inform relevant people, avoid unnecessary capture, restrict access to raw files and agree retention periods. EASA's privacy handbook offers practical guidance, but project-specific legal questions require appropriate advice.

## Combine aerial video with ground footage, interviews and sound

Air footage explains location and scale. Ground footage shows hands, materials, faces and finish. A short interview can explain a design choice or project challenge, while recorded site sound makes the result feel tangible. Together, these elements create a stronger case study than a continuous aerial montage.

## Create a separate edit for each audience

One recording plan can support several outputs: a short progress update, a presentation for stakeholders, a website case study, social clips and a final project film. Define duration, aspect ratio, captions, branding, music rights and call to action in advance. A concise edit for a client meeting should not simply be the first minute of the campaign film.

## Publish accessible, technically controlled video

Provide captions for prerecorded speech and meaningful sound where required. Add a descriptive title and summary, use an accurate thumbnail and place the video on a page that explains its context. Compress delivery files appropriately and retain a high-quality master. Captions also help people watching without sound and make important project terms explicit.

## Build a usable project archive

Store masters, selected shots and exports with consistent project, date, phase and version names. Keep flight notes and permissions with the production record. Limit access to sensitive raw footage and document the agreed retention period. A well-kept archive makes later case studies far easier to assemble.

## What determines budget and production scope?

The number of visits, site complexity, travel, permissions, aircraft choice, crew, ground production, interviews, edit versions, captions and archive requirements all influence scope. Weather contingencies and restricted operating windows can also matter. A useful quotation starts with project phases and intended outputs, not with flight time alone.

## From isolated site flights to a convincing project timeline

Useful construction drone video begins with communication goals, repeatable reference shots and safe coordination. It then combines aerial context with ground-level evidence and turns the material into versions that suit each audience. Every session still needs a fresh operational and regulatory check.
