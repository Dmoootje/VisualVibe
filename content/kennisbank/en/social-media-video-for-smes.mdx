# Social media video for SMEs: from content plan to measurable series

<LeadIntro>
A **social media video for your SME** works when it answers one recognisable audience question in a format that suits the channel. Before recording, decide whom you want to reach, what that person needs to understand and which next step makes sense. Then shoot modularly: a strong core message, several opening lines, enough supporting footage and focused endings. One well-prepared production day will then yield a publishable series that you can measure and improve, rather than a folder of disconnected clips.
</LeadIntro>

This guide replaces improvisation with a workable process. You will learn which formats an SME can sustain, how to accommodate platform differences, what to check technically and how to interpret results without confusing views with business outcomes. If you first need to decide how video fits into your wider communications, start with our pillar on [why to invest in a corporate video](/en/knowledge-base/videography/why-invest-in-a-corporate-video/).

<QuoteBlock>
A social clip is not a shortened corporate film. It is one complete idea, designed for a specific viewing moment.
</QuoteBlock>

## What is social media video for an SME?

Social media video is video content created or adapted for a social feed, company page or vertical video channel. It might be a 40-second explanation, an answer to a customer question, a short demonstration, a project update, an interview excerpt or a sequence from an event. The channel shapes part of the format, but the business purpose determines the substance.

For an SME, there is an important difference between merely posting video and producing purposeful video content. A random atmosphere clip may gain reach without clarifying anything. A straightforward expert video built around a specific question may prompt relevant comments or conversations. Professional quality does not automatically mean a large set. Above all, it means intelligible sound, reliable images, a clear message, suitable rights and a version that can be published without guesswork.

## Start with one business goal and one audience action

"More visibility" is too broad a target for a video. Decide what visibility should support in this series. Do you want existing customers to discover an additional service, candidates to experience the workplace or prospects to compare a frequently misunderstood process more effectively? Each choice calls for different evidence and a different next step.

Set one primary objective per series and one audience action per video. That action need not be an immediate quotation request. Someone might read the full explanation, tag a colleague, open a vacancy, view a case study or ask a follow-up question. The CTA should match the person's distance from a purchase. A new viewer who has just encountered a useful tip is not automatically ready for a sales conversation.

## Build recurring formats your team can sustain

A format is a repeatable agreement about the subject, speaker, structure and appearance. It reduces the number of decisions without making every story identical. Examples include "one minute with the specialist", "the mistake we often prevent on site", "three questions for a new colleague" or "from brief to result". Viewers recognise the series and your team knows what it requires.

Choose two or three formats instead of ten content categories. You might combine an expertise format, an evidence format and a human format. Keep collecting questions in one list, then add an owner, example and desired CTA to each one. A production day then begins with material that has already been tested against genuine customer conversations.

## Give each channel a role instead of posting the same file everywhere

LinkedIn, Instagram, YouTube and TikTok all support video, but people do not open them with precisely the same expectations. On LinkedIn, a substantive post can provide considerable context alongside the video. Instagram Reels and TikTok are strongly vertical, and interface elements cover parts of the frame. YouTube Shorts can lead viewers towards a channel or a longer explanation. Your own website offers more control and can retain the full context, transcript and CTA.

This does not mean every channel needs an entirely new production. It does mean you should create a distribution matrix. For each channel, note whom you reach there, which crop is required, how the opening seconds work, what the accompanying post should say and where the viewer goes next. Publish a clip only where it has a clear role.

For production, you can combine [professional social media video production](/en/services/videography/social-media-video/) with a [broader corporate video production](/en/services/videography/corporate-video/). The main story and its derivatives can then be planned together, instead of being cut afterwards from one wide master edit.

## Check current aspect ratios and upload limits before export

Platform limits change. Check the official help information and the actual publishing screen for every campaign. On 12 July 2026, Meta stated that Instagram Reels could be uploaded at ratios between 1.91:1 and 9:16, at a minimum resolution of 720 pixels and 30 frames per second. The [official Reels specifications](https://www.facebook.com/help/instagram/1038071743007909?locale=en_GB) also clarify that the cover image and video frame do not use the same ratio.

[LinkedIn Help](https://www.linkedin.com/help/linkedin/answer/a548372/) lists a ratio of 1:2.4 to 2.4:1, a resolution range of 256 x 144 to 4096 x 2304 and a maximum duration of 15 minutes for standard uploads. On YouTube, square or vertical uploads of up to three minutes have been categorised as Shorts since 15 October 2024. Check the exceptions and music rights in [YouTube Help on three-minute Shorts](https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/15424877?hl=en).

TikTok for Business describes 9:16, 1:1 and 16:9 as supported optimisation ratios for TikTok ad placements. This is advertising documentation, not a universal promise for every organic feature. Always check the specification for your chosen placement and objective in the [TikTok Business Help Center](https://ads.tiktok.com/help/article/video-aspect-ratio-intelligent-optimization-tool?lang=en&redirected=2).

<NoticeBox variant="warning" title="Keep a flexible master">
Treat platform limits as a current technical check, not a permanent content rule. Keep a high-quality master without embedded interface elements, along with the project files and separate captions. You can then create a new crop if a platform changes its specifications, safe zones or display.
</NoticeBox>

## Write the opening as an honest promise

The first line should quickly explain why the video matters without resorting to shouting. "Three mistakes everyone makes" is weak if the video merely repeats generic tips. "This gap looks small, but creates a visible misalignment after installation" tells the right viewer exactly what is at stake.

Write several openings for the same core message. A question works when viewers genuinely ask it. A demonstration works when the difference is immediately visible. A result can lead when you then explain how it was achieved. Avoid a long logo animation, general greeting or company history before the actual value begins.

The rest of the video must deliver on the opening. Present context, evidence and conclusion in a logical order. To prepare that structure systematically, read our guide to [structuring a corporate video script](/en/knowledge-base/videography/how-to-structure-a-corporate-video-script/). A good social script is often short on paper, but precise about visuals, spoken text and the CTA.

## Shoot vertically without ruling out future reuse

A 9:16 vertical master makes sense for full-screen mobile feeds, but not every final use is vertical. Your website, presentation or YouTube watch page may require a wide frame. Decide which version leads before the shoot. For important campaigns, you can record vertically and horizontally at the same time, shoot two takes, or use enough resolution and framing space to accommodate both.

Keep faces, products and text away from the edges. Interface buttons, captions and descriptions can cover important information. LinkedIn explicitly warns users to keep essential elements away from the edges in its upload specifications. Create a test export for each platform and view it on a real device, not just in the editing window.

## Make every video understandable without sound

Captions are not decorative headlines. They present spoken content and relevant sounds in sync with the video. The [W3C guidance on accessible audio and video](https://www.w3.org/WAI/media/av/) recommends captions, transcripts and, where needed, descriptions of important visual information. Automatic recognition can provide a starting point, but names, dialect, specialist terminology and figures must be checked.

Write short, readable caption lines, leave enough time to read them and keep them clear of busy interface areas. Do not make essential information depend solely on music, colour or a rapid flash of text. If a person demonstrates an action without speaking, a short description may be needed to make the outcome clear.

Keep a clean file and a separate subtitle file alongside versions with embedded social captions. The clean version remains useful when a platform adds native captions or when you later create another language version. A transcript on your website also makes a substantive video easy to scan for people who prefer reading.

## Organise one production day as a modular content system

An efficient content day is not about recording as many minutes as possible. Group topics that use the same location, speaker or demonstration. Schedule the essential answers and actions first, then record variants and extra supporting footage. Alternate topics so a speaker does not have to deliver 20 nearly identical introductions in a row.

## Focus feedback on accuracy and audience understanding

Too many reviewers rarely improve a short video. Agree who is responsible for factual accuracy, the brand, privacy and final approval. Combine comments into one round and refer to time codes. "It does not feel dynamic enough" is difficult to act on. "The use for existing customers only becomes clear after 35 seconds" identifies a specific issue.

First assess whether the video answers the audience question correctly. Then review pace, visuals, text, audio and brand style. Also record which claims are time-sensitive and who will check them after publication.

## Publish with context instead of expecting the video to work alone

The accompanying text can clarify the audience and nuance or lead to a more detailed page. It should not simply repeat the transcript. Make sure the title, cover, opening and landing page create the same expectation. Respond to comments, because strong follow-up questions are valuable material for the next video.

## Measure against the goal and compare similar videos

A view is not a uniform measure. Platforms count starts, repeats and engaged views differently. YouTube, for example, changed the way Shorts views are counted while retaining "engaged views" as a separate comparison metric in Analytics. Understand each platform's definitions before placing figures side by side.

Then look at signals that match the objective. For an explanation, average viewing time, relevant comments and clicks to further information matter more than reach alone. For recruitment, vacancy visits and suitable applications may count. For an offer video, look at landing-page visits, enquiries and the quality of conversations.

The [YouTube explanation of audience retention](https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/9314415?hl=en) distinguishes drops, spikes and rewatched moments, among other signals. Treat such a graph as a clue, not an automatic conclusion. A spike may indicate enthusiasm or confusion. Above all, compare videos with similar subjects, channels, audiences and durations.

## Repurpose the source without copying the same edit everywhere

Repurposing begins with the source material, not the final file. One strong interview can yield a compact social explanation, a longer website video, a quotation, FAQ copy and a sales clip. Each version selects a different complete idea. Simply cropping a horizontal corporate video to vertical can remove faces, demonstrations or text.

Different durations can coexist. A short clip introduces a question; a longer version answers it fully. Our guide to [short video versus long video](/en/knowledge-base/videography/short-video-vs-long-video/) helps you decide when greater depth is better than further trimming.

## A sustainable series makes expertise visible regularly

An SME does not need to become a daily content machine. A small number of strong formats, genuine questions and well-prepared production days can already create a reliable flow. Quality lies in the connections: accurate substance, images that provide evidence, versions suited to their channels and a next step that does not pressure the viewer.

Begin with one series of several episodes. Record what you expect to learn, publish consistently and afterwards discuss which questions, openings and destinations proved useful. Every subsequent production will become more focused. Social media video then grows from disconnected visibility into a managed part of sales, recruitment, service or brand communications.
