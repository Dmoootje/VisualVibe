# Recruitment video for employers: show the work, culture and expectations

<LeadIntro>
A **recruitment video for employers** should help candidates make a better-informed choice. It can show the workplace, everyday tasks, colleagues and expectations in a way a vacancy text cannot. Credibility depends on what you reveal, not on making every moment look spectacular. Start with the vacancy and the questions a suitable candidate will ask, then build the interviews, scenes and channel versions around honest answers.
</LeadIntro>

Recruitment video sits between vacancy communication and employer branding. It can make a role tangible and introduce the organisation, but it cannot replace complete, current employment information. The video, written vacancy, recruitment process and real employee experience must tell the same story.

This guide explains how to plan a recruitment video with real employees, inclusive choices, suitable rights agreements and measurable distribution. If you want production support, explore [recruitment video production](/en/services/videography/recruitment-video/) and [corporate video production](/en/services/videography/corporate-video/).

<QuoteBlock>
A recruitment video is credible when candidates recognise the reality of the role after they start, not merely when the film looks attractive before they apply.
</QuoteBlock>

## What is a recruitment video?

A recruitment video is produced content that helps potential applicants understand an employer, vacancy or field of work. It can combine employee interviews, observed working moments, details of the environment and practical information. A broad employer-brand film introduces the organisation, while a vacancy video answers questions about a particular role. One production may support both, but each edit needs a clear purpose.

The video should give candidates evidence they can interpret: what the work looks like, who they collaborate with, which surroundings and tools are involved and what the organisation expects. Slogans about passion or a close-knit team are not evidence on their own.

## Start with the vacancy and the candidate's question

Read the complete vacancy before developing a concept. Mark the tasks, essential requirements, workplace, schedule, reporting line, selection process and conditions that shape a candidate's decision. Then list questions the written copy does not answer well. What does a normal shift involve? How physical is the role? How does the team hand work over? Which training is available? What makes the environment demanding?

Decide what the video must clarify and what belongs in text. Salary, contract terms, accessibility information and current application instructions should remain easy to find without playing a video. Use the film to add context, people and recognisable situations, then direct viewers to the authoritative vacancy page.

<NoticeBox variant="tip" title="Write one candidate question above every planned scene">
If a scene does not answer a relevant question or establish useful context, it is probably atmosphere rather than recruitment information. Atmosphere has a place, but it should not crowd out the reality of the role.
</NoticeBox>

## What should a candidate genuinely be able to see?

Show representative tasks rather than a sequence of people walking through corridors and smiling at meetings. For a technician, that might include preparation, safety checks, equipment and collaboration on site. For an office role, it may include customer contact, focused work, team reviews and the tools used. Choose moments that are real, safe to film and understandable without disclosing confidential information.

Include the working environment honestly. Noise, protective clothing, outdoor conditions, travel or shift work may be relevant to the decision. Do not hide demanding aspects behind a glossy montage. Equally, do not stage dangerous or inefficient behaviour simply because it looks dynamic. Safety and normal operations take priority over the shot list.

## How do you avoid a generic employer-brand film?

Specificity is the best defence against generic copy. Replace "no two days are the same" with an example of what changes. Replace "a great team" with how colleagues divide responsibility or support a new starter. Ask employees for a recent moment that illustrates a point, rather than asking them to recite values.

Keep the organisation recognisable. The same fashionable office, fast-cutting edit and broad claims could fit hundreds of employers. Real terminology, locations, tools and decisions make the story distinctive. Use brand style to frame reality, not to overwrite it.

## Who should speak, and how do you select roles?

Choose people who can explain the role from relevant perspectives. A future colleague can describe daily work, a team lead can clarify expectations and a recent starter can discuss onboarding. Senior management may establish direction, but should not dominate a film intended to show everyday experience.

Participation should be voluntary and informed. Do not select employees solely for camera confidence or visual diversity. Give people a real professional role in the story and avoid presenting anyone as decoration. Check availability, language, comfort, factual knowledge and the consequences if someone later changes role or leaves the organisation.

### Which interview questions produce credible answers?

Ask for experiences, examples and decisions. "What happened during your first week?", "Which part of the job requires the most concentration?" and "When do you ask a colleague for help?" invite concrete answers. "Why are we the best employer?" invites a slogan.

Share themes in advance but avoid scripting personal testimony. Confirm facts such as schedules, training, equipment and conditions with the appropriate owner. If precise wording is legally or operationally important, record it separately and make clear that it is organisational information, not a spontaneous personal opinion.

## How do you build the script, scenario and shot list?

Start with a simple narrative: the candidate's question, evidence from employees and work, the expectations attached to the role and the next step. A script can set the order, facts, on-screen text and transitions without dictating every interview sentence.

Turn that narrative into a shot list tied to real operations. Record the location, people, task, required permission, safety limitation, sound need and likely channel for every scene. Schedule rare or time-sensitive work first. Plan cutaways that genuinely illustrate interview statements, plus clean portraits and vertical-safe frames where shorter versions are required.

## How do you safeguard equal treatment and inclusion?

Review the entire recruitment journey, not just who appears on screen. Job requirements, language, casting, scene roles, captions, distribution and the application route can all include or exclude. Show people as professionals doing meaningful work, and avoid stereotypes in who leads, operates equipment or provides support.

Belgian guidance from the [FPS Employment](https://werk.belgie.be/nl/news/nationale-arbeidsraad-nar-nieuwe-praktische-gids-diversiteit-en-gelijke-behandeling-bij) and [National Labour Council](https://cnt-nar.be/nl/pers/nieuws/actualisering-van-de-brochure-diversiteit-en-gelijke-behandeling-bij-personeelswerving) addresses diversity and equal treatment in recruitment. Collective agreement no. 38 also covers recruitment and selection. Have employment-law and anti-discrimination questions assessed for your actual process. This article is not legal advice.

Use inclusive, role-relevant language and only state requirements that the vacancy genuinely needs. Make captions readable, ensure the application route works without audio or video and provide an accessible contact method for reasonable adjustments.

## How do you agree image rights with employees?

Explain the production purpose, channels, expected duration, paid advertising, possible crops and reuse before filming. In an employment relationship, the freedom of consent requires particular care. The Belgian Data Protection Authority's guidance on [image rights](https://www.gegevensbeschermingsautoriteit.be/professioneel/thema-s/recht-op-afbeelding) should be considered alongside the appropriate legal basis and internal policy.

Record agreements in clear language. Decide what happens when someone changes role or leaves, how long raw footage and edits are kept, who can access them and whether older campaign versions remain online. Consent to filming does not automatically cover every publication or future campaign.

Also clear visible customers, visitors, screens, documents, brands, music and locations. Use controlled scenes when normal work would expose confidential data, but do not turn the result into a false representation of the job.

## How do you make recruitment video accessible?

Provide accurate captions that identify relevant speakers and meaningful sound. Ensure important facts are not conveyed only through music, colour or fast on-screen text. The W3C overview of [accessible audio and video](https://www.w3.org/WAI/media/av/) explains captions, transcripts and other media alternatives.

Check contrast, text size, reading time and safe areas on the smallest intended screen. Supply a transcript or a full written vacancy containing the essential information. Captions also improve use in silent social feeds, but accessibility is broader than adding automatically generated subtitles. Review names, technical vocabulary and punctuation manually.

## What does an efficient production day look like?

Confirm the work schedule, participants, permissions and safety briefing before the crew arrives. Film representative operations when they really happen instead of repeatedly interrupting the workplace to invent them. A site contact should resolve access and operational questions, while the director protects the editorial plan.

Begin with a location and safety check. Record interviews where sound and background are controllable, then capture supporting scenes around the actual workflow. Allow people time to settle and answer in their own words. Check picture and sound before releasing anyone, and create a verified backup before clearing the production.

## What length and versions do you need?

There is no universal ideal duration. A film on a detailed vacancy page can take enough time to establish the role and evidence. A social clip should answer one question quickly and lead to the full vacancy. A broader employer-brand film may introduce the organisation before role-specific pieces take over.

Create purposeful versions rather than one compromise edit. A vertical crop may need a different opening, larger captions and tighter framing. A muted website loop should not carry essential information. A six-second advertisement, a one-minute social explanation and a three-minute vacancy film are different editorial products, even when they share footage.

## Where should you publish the recruitment video?

Put the principal film beside complete, current information on the relevant vacancy page. Social platforms can introduce one task, colleague or candidate question, then send viewers to that page. Employer profiles, recruitment campaigns, presentations and job events may need their own version and rights scope.

Keep the destination current. If a role closes, do not leave paid traffic pointing to an expired page without a useful alternative. Name the role and location accurately in titles and descriptions. Avoid posting identical captions and formats everywhere, since viewing behaviour and technical constraints differ by channel.

## How do you measure whether video helps recruitment?

Start with the recruitment objective. Measure relevant visits to the vacancy, completion of the intended next step, suitable applications, candidate questions and feedback about whether expectations were accurate. Platform views and completion rates show media behaviour, not hiring quality by themselves.

Record the publication date, channels, targeting and other changes to the vacancy or campaign. Salary, labour-market conditions, application friction, media spend and selection speed can all influence results. Do not attribute every change to video. Compare like with like and combine quantitative signals with recruiter and candidate feedback.

## What determines budget and production scope?

Scope depends on research and concept work, number of roles and locations, contributors, safety and access, filming days, crew, equipment, travel, graphics, music, captions, version count and review rounds. A single controlled workplace and two interviews differ greatly from several live sites across shifts.

Ask a production partner to state assumptions and deliverables. Does the quotation include interview preparation, location recce, image agreements, scripting, subtitles, vertical edits, paid-media usage, translations and raw-footage retention? A low filming-day price can conceal work that still needs to happen before publication.

## A recruitment video makes the choice more concrete

A good recruitment video does not begin with how attractive the company can look. It begins with what candidates need to choose consciously for or against a role. That requires real work, clear context, credible employees and information that matches the vacancy.

When the script, equal treatment, image agreements, accessibility, distribution and measurement are planned together, the video becomes more than a campaign asset. It supports a more honest conversation between employer and candidate and gives recruiters material that answers practical questions.
