# Event photography checklist: from run sheet to rapid publication

<LeadIntro>
An **event photography checklist** connects your event objective to a workable run sheet, a focused shot list and clear arrangements for access, privacy, selection and publication. The photographer then knows not only where to be, but which moments genuinely matter to communications teams, partners and participants.
</LeadIntro>

An event cannot be restaged. The handshake after a keynote, the reaction to an award and the opening of a new venue all happen in their own time. Without preparation, a photographer may create attractive atmosphere shots and still miss the photograph the organiser needs the following morning.

This guide helps you prepare a company celebration, opening, conference, networking event, trade fair, sporting occasion or launch. For the wider choice between portraits, reports, products and projects, start with [what corporate photography involves](/en/kennisbank/fotografie/what-is-corporate-photography/). Here, we focus on the event production itself, from setting objectives to building the image library.

<QuoteBlock>
An event photographer cannot be everywhere at once. A good run sheet shows where the story is unfolding at every point in the programme.
</QuoteBlock>

## Decide what the event photographs need to achieve

Before discussing hours or image counts, ask how the report will be used. Do you need to send a press photograph that evening, publish a review the following morning or build a campaign for the next edition later? Does a sponsor require agreed visibility? Should speakers receive a selection? Every purpose affects timing, framing and delivery deadlines.

Distinguish documentation from promotion. A documentary report shows honestly who and what was present, and where. Promotional photography selects images that support the desired character of the next edition. Both can come from the same report, but a busy wide shot does not prove attendance figures, and an enthusiastic close-up does not automatically show the scale of the event.

Photography can support communication, but it does not guarantee attendance, reach or sales. The programme, distribution, reputation, timing and media budget remain separate factors.

## Turn the programme into photographic priorities

An hour-by-hour programme is not yet a photography run sheet. For every item, mark what will happen, who is central, where the photographer may stand and which image cannot be missed. Allow time to move between spaces. Two consecutive moments in different rooms are not a workable schedule for one photographer.

Use three priority levels. A moments are unrepeatable and essential, such as the official opening, award presentation or group photograph. B moments add depth to the story, including networking, demonstrations and reactions. C moments are useful additions when time and circumstances allow.

The event manager should monitor programme changes and send them directly. A group chat with several stakeholders can help, but appoint one person who has authority to change priorities. The photographer will not then receive conflicting instructions during a one-off moment.

## Give the photographer a concise brief

The brief need not be a weighty document, but it must answer the questions that cost time on location. Share the objective, programme, floor plan, names and roles of key people, visual identity, partner commitments, publication deadlines and emergency contacts.

Also state which situations must not be photographed. These might include confidential presentations, prototypes, minors, quiet areas, medical information, badges containing personal data or guests with specific arrangements. An exclusion list matters just as much as the shot list.

## Arrange access, accreditation and room to move

Do not make the photographer join the ordinary guest queue when they need to capture the setup, empty room or preparations for reception. Arrange early access, parking or a loading area and a badge covering the necessary zones. Inform security, the venue manager and stage manager.

Walk the important routes before the doors open. Where can the photographer approach the stage without blocking sight lines? May they switch sides during a keynote? Which passage must remain open for catering and emergency services? Where can batteries, cards or a laptop be stored safely?

<NoticeBox variant="tip" title="Plan a short venue walkthrough">
Fifteen minutes with the event manager or stage manager prevents a great deal of improvisation. Check stage height, lighting, branding, the group photo location, backstage entrance and the route between parallel rooms before guests arrive.
</NoticeBox>

Trade fairs and external venues sometimes impose their own accreditation, safety and publication rules. Request these directly from the organiser or venue and share them with the entire crew.

## Build a shot list that tells a story

An event report needs rhythm. Begin with the venue and preparations, show arrival and recognition, build towards the content and interaction, then close with the result or final atmosphere. Close-ups alone make the event hard to follow; wide shots alone make it feel distant.

Avoid a checklist of dozens of names without priorities. Specify who needs to appear with whom and in what context. "CEO" is less useful than "CEO welcomes the keynote speaker by the stage steps at 7:55 pm".

## Decide how many photographers you need

One photographer can cover a great deal when the programme is linear and locations are close together. Parallel sessions, a simultaneous VIP reception, a photo wall and a stage programme may require a second photographer or assistant. This is not a fixed rule. Base the decision on overlap, distance, risk and the coverage required.

Clarify who will take the team photographs if part of the crew is busy with other work. The organisers' group photograph is surprisingly often postponed until someone has already left.

## Coordinate the stage, speakers and VIPs

Ask speakers in advance whether photography is permitted during their session and whether any moments are sensitive. Share names, roles, pronunciation and reference photographs. Agree with the stage manager where the photographer may stand and whether shutter noise, flash or movement could be disruptive.

Do not schedule an official photograph when the people involved must leave immediately for another appointment. Choose a fixed location, check the background and light, and appoint someone to gather the group. Capture the essential combination first, then make variations.

VIPs, public figures and performers may have their own management, press or usage arrangements. The organiser should collect these in writing and provide them before the assignment. Do not assume that being well known means accepting unrestricted commercial image use.

## Prepare for challenging event lighting

Venues often combine dark ambient light, bright LED screens, coloured spotlights and rapidly changing scenes. Ask for the technical specification or at least photographs from previous editions. If possible, test stage lighting during the venue walkthrough. A speaker lit only from behind may look atmospheric to the audience but remain difficult to recognise in a photograph.

Flash is not always welcome or permitted. Agree where discreet additional lighting is acceptable and where it is not. Check smoke, lasers, projections and screen refresh rates too. A photo wall needs a clear queue, fixed floor mark and consistent lighting, otherwise it may block reception.

Retain the atmosphere in editing. An evening celebration need not look like a brightly lit office. At the same time, skin tones, sponsor logos and important actions must remain legible. Give the photographer a few brand references, while allowing for the event's actual lighting conditions.

## Privacy and image rights at events

Recognisable event photographs can involve both image rights and data protection. In its guidance on [image rights](https://www.gegevensbeschermingsautoriteit.be/professioneel/thema-s/recht-op-afbeelding), the Belgian Data Protection Authority explains that taking and using a targeted photograph are separate questions, and that permission does not work in exactly the same way in every situation. Do not automatically treat a ticket or general attendance as unlimited permission for every commercial portrait.

In its discussion of [specific situations](https://www.gegevensbeschermingsautoriteit.be/burger/thema-s/recht-op-afbeelding/bijzondere-gevallen), the authority covers public places, people who are incidental to the scene and crowd images, among other examples. Those contexts are not equivalent to a recognisable close-up, testimonial or advertising image. Have the specific use, location and audience assessed instead of applying one broad rule to every photograph.

The [General Data Protection Regulation](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32016R0679) includes principles covering purpose limitation, data minimisation, retention and transparent information. Put these into practice: inform guests clearly, provide a workable route for questions or objections, restrict access to files, and determine who selects, retains and publishes them.

Specialist review is sensible for minors, private gatherings, health information, political contexts or large-scale commercial campaigns. This checklist provides practical production information, not legal advice.

## Organise rapid selection without sacrificing quality

"As soon as possible" is not a delivery agreement. Decide how many images are needed, by what time, for which channel and who will approve them. A set of ten press or social photographs during the event needs a different workflow from a complete report delivered several days later.

A rapid selection requires a quiet workspace, power, reliable data transfer and one recipient. Tell the editor or photographer which A moments come first. Use an agreed file name and delivery folder so the communications team does not have to search through arbitrary files for the keynote.

<NoticeBox variant="success" title="Use two deliveries">
First provide a small, checked selection for the immediate communication need. Then finish the complete report consistently. This keeps a rapid turnaround manageable without treating the entire set as rushed work.
</NoticeBox>

Check the rapid set for focus, expression, visible badges, screen information, unwanted gestures and arrangements concerning people or brands. A mistake published immediately will spread faster than a later correction.

## Define delivery and usage rights

Agree the image count, selection method, editing, file formats, delivery times and archiving in advance. Request web versions and high-resolution files when both are needed. Record whether crops, black-and-white versions or separate sponsor selections are included.

Possessing a file does not automatically mean owning its copyright or holding an unlimited licence. The [FPS Economy copyright FAQ](https://economie.fgov.be/sites/default/files/Files/Publications/files/FAQ_Auteursrecht.pdf) provides general information about protected images and lawful use. Define whether publication is permitted on the organisation's website, organic social media, paid advertising and in the press, and whether sponsors, speakers and visitors may use the images, including any conditions.

## Publish the report as a story

Do not select chronologically without editing. Open with a strong contextual image, then show content, interaction and the brand experience before ending with a recognisable close. Alternate wide views, medium shots and details. Include names and roles only after checking them.

On a website, place images near relevant text. Google's [image SEO guidance](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/google-images) recommends relevant context, suitable alt text and technically accessible image elements, among other practices. Do not write "event photography Limburg" on every image. Describe what that particular image contributes in context.

[WCAG 2.2](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/) requires text alternatives for meaningful non-text content. "Panellists answer questions during the SME conference" is more informative than "event photo 27". When a graph, award text or content-bearing screen contains information readers need, provide that information in ordinary text too.

Publish optimised formats and responsive sizes. A complete high-resolution set in one web gallery can make the mobile experience unnecessarily heavy. Keep originals in the managed archive and use the agreed web exports online.

## Combine photography, video and drone footage purposefully

Photography and video may follow the same programme, but their best moment is not always the same. A photographer can capture several perspectives during applause, while a videographer protects continuity and sound. Agree priorities and positions so crews do not obstruct one another.

A [corporate event aftermovie](/en/diensten/videography/corporate-event-aftermovie/) conveys movement, voices, music and the build-up. [Event drone footage](/en/diensten/drone-fpv/event-drone-footage/) can show scale and surroundings when the venue, safety, rules and conditions allow. Choose media for the story rather than trying to tick off every product in one day. Our pillar on [why to invest in a corporate video](/en/kennisbank/videografie/why-invest-in-a-corporate-video/) helps determine when time, sound and movement are essential to the content.

If the event opens a completed building or project, schedule a separate calm photography slot too. The guide to [project photography for contractors](/en/kennisbank/fotografie/project-photography-for-contractors/) explains why architecture, materials and finish require different timing and viewpoints from guests and the programme.

## From a busy programme to a useful image library

Good event photography begins with choices. Decide which communication is needed immediately, which moments cannot be repeated and which people or partners must be represented correctly. Then arrange access, privacy, team roles and a delivery workflow that fits the real deadline.

The result is a report that documents the event honestly while remaining useful to the press, partners, a retrospective and the next edition. It offers no guarantee of reach or attendance, but gives you control over the images, context and arrangements you can prepare.
