# How to prepare a corporate photo shoot: a practical plan

<LeadIntro>
To **prepare a corporate photo shoot**, start by deciding which images the organisation genuinely needs. Before production day, record their intended uses, priorities, participating employees, locations, permissions and required formats. Turn those decisions into a shot list and a realistic schedule. The photographer retains creative freedom, while essential images for your website, campaigns and vacancies do not get overlooked.
</LeadIntro>

An on-location shoot can sound simple: gather the team, tidy the rooms and let the photographer walk around. In practice, diaries, light, operations, safety, styling and communication all converge. Decisions left until the morning consume valuable time and often leave one crucial wide shot, detail or employee missing from the final selection.

This is an operational companion to our guide explaining [what corporate photography is](/en/knowledge-base/photography/what-is-corporate-photography/). It covers the brief, shot list, call sheet, consent, logistics, selection and file management. You can also explore [on-location corporate photography](/en/services/photography/corporate-photography/).

<QuoteBlock>A productive shoot day does not start with taking pictures faster. It starts with less uncertainty about which pictures must come back.</QuoteBlock>

## Start with communication goals, not a list of attractive pictures

A useful brief connects every requested image to an application. “Professional atmosphere shots” is too vague. “A wide homepage image with clear space on the left, three process images for the service page and vertical pictures for two vacancies” establishes subjects, composition and priority.

Review what the business expects to publish during the next twelve months: web pages, sales material, vacancies, presentations, advertising, press requests and social posts. For each channel, ask what proof is missing. Does a customer need to meet the team, understand a process, assess the finish or see the scale of a location?

Mark images as essential, desirable or reserve. If the day runs late, protect the essential set first.

## Write a brief that means the same thing to everyone

Provide context without prescribing every photograph. Describe the business, audience, visual character, applications and what you do not want. Add brand guidelines, page layouts or references and explain which feature matters. Appoint one content decision-maker to consolidate input from management, marketing and the web partner.

## Build the shot list around pages, stories and moments

For each scene, note the subject, purpose, location, people, orientation, priority and practical constraints. Start with anchor images that summarise the organisation: location, team, core activity and main result. Add mid shots of collaboration and execution, then details of hands, materials, tools, interfaces or finishing. This variety gives pages rhythm.

Use our [corporate portrait tips](/en/knowledge-base/photography/corporate-portrait-tips/) for portrait styling. Here, record who needs a portrait, which roles should be grouped and the time available per person.

<NoticeBox variant="tip" title="Plan transition images too">Details, quiet spaces and close-ups can connect page sections, presentations and social crops. Put them on the reserve list so they never displace essential scenes.</NoticeBox>

## Turn the shot list into a realistic schedule

Plan around fixed constraints: staff availability, operating hours, customer access and favourable daylight. Group scenes by room and setup to avoid repeatedly moving lights and equipment. Include setup, test images, participant briefings, travel and interruptions. Five five-minute portraits take more than 25 minutes once arrivals, clothing checks and posing are included.

A call sheet should include the address, arrival time, parking, contacts, schedule, participants, clothing, safety and bad-weather scenario. Share it with reception and team leaders too.

## Check location, logistics and safety in advance

Use a recce or recent photographs and video to confirm dimensions, light, power, access and distractions. Check daylight at the scheduled time. Remove rubbish, tangled cables, confidential documents and customer data; inspect screens, whiteboards, number plates and third-party brands. Prepare representative products and materials, reserve a quiet portrait area and mark prohibited zones.

Confirm access, badges, keys, parking, a safe loading route, power, protective equipment, weather contingencies and an authorised internal guide. Do not make the location sterile: retain details that explain the real work, but ensure temporary items do not tell the wrong story.

## Inform employees and arrange consent beforehand

People participate more naturally when they know the purpose, timing and intended channels. Announce the shoot early, provide one contact for practical or privacy questions and do not surprise anyone with a portrait request.

The Belgian Data Protection Authority distinguishes consent to take an image from consent to publish or distribute it in its guidance on [image rights](https://www.gegevensbeschermingsautoriteit.be/burger/thema-s/recht-op-afbeelding/principes). Record the channels and purposes in advance and assign responsibility for evidence and later changes. Customers, suppliers, models, visitors and identifiable passers-by need attention too. Specific circumstances require their own assessment; this article is not legal advice.

<NoticeBox variant="warning" title="Consent is not the photographer's box to tick alone">The organisation usually determines the publication purposes and knows the people involved. Appoint an internal owner for information, consent and post-publication questions, and obtain legal advice where needed.</NoticeBox>

## Match clothing, styling and props to the real brand

Clothing should fit the person's role and setting. Workwear may look used when craftsmanship is central, but it should be clean, safe and current. Agree which logos, colours and patterns suit the series. Ask for alternatives when portraits and report images are combined. Fine checks can shimmer on camera, while very bright colours may dominate or reflect onto skin.

Props need a purpose. A laptop, plan, sample, product or instrument can explain an action; an arbitrary mug or unused tool looks staged. Ask people to do something relevant to their real role.

## Combine photography and video only around shared priorities

One production day can reuse the location, people and styling, but photography and video are not identical. Video requires movement, sound, repeatable actions and longer takes; photography may need a different moment or lighting setup. Give each discipline its own essential list, identify shared scenes and allow time to reposition people and equipment. If moving pictures are included, first read [why businesses invest in corporate video](/en/knowledge-base/videography/why-invest-in-a-corporate-video/).

## Manage the day without eliminating spontaneity

Begin with a walk-through and confirm absences, weather, operations and changed rooms. The internal guide manages people, timing and factual detail; the photographer manages light, composition, direction and technical quality. Keep those roles distinct. At every block, verify the action, safety, privacy, orientations, background, names and completion of the essential shot before changing setups. Preserve buffer for delays, safe repetition, travel, weather and valuable unplanned moments.

## Select for use, not file count

Agree who makes the first selection, how many feedback rounds are included and when choices are due. Group proofs by scene, let one person consolidate feedback and assess message and intended channel before expression, background, technique, crop and consistency. A compact image library is more useful than five nearly identical photographs. Verify names and confidential details, and distinguish careful retouching from a misleading change to product colour, workmanship or location condition.

## Agree formats, copyright and management explicitly

Define uses rather than merely asking for “high and low resolution”: print masters, optimised web versions, colour profiles and fixed campaign crops. Keep an untouched master. Receiving a file does not automatically transfer unrestricted copyright. Use the [FPS Economy copyright FAQ](https://economie.fgov.be/sites/default/files/Files/Publications/files/FAQ-auteursrecht.pdf) as general guidance, and record channels, duration, partners, media and advertising uses in the licence.

For websites, W3C's [Images Tutorial](https://www.w3.org/WAI/tutorials/images/) says text alternatives should reflect an image's purpose. Meaningful process photography needs useful alt text, while a decorative image may need an empty alt attribute. Google's [image SEO guidance](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/google-images) also covers surrounding context, descriptive filenames, standard image elements and responsive versions. Alt text is not a place to stack services and locations.

Store the subject, date, location, photographer, rights status and restrictions with every final file, using a folder structure colleagues can understand.

## A sound production plan leaves room for better images

Preparation clarifies goals, people and constraints before the clock starts. The photographer can focus on light, direction and unexpected opportunities while the essential set remains protected. The result is not a full memory card, but a findable and responsibly managed series for the website, sales, recruitment and campaigns.
