# GEO and AEO for SMEs: appearing in Google, ChatGPT and AI search results

<LeadIntro>
Search is changing. People still use Google, but they also ask questions of AI systems, chatbots and search engines that summarise answers themselves. Instead of searching only for "web design Limburg", they may ask which web designer in Limburg also provides photography and SEO, or what a professional SME website needs.
</LeadIntro>

**SMEs improve their chances of appearing in AI search results by publishing crawlable, well-structured and factually consistent content that answers real questions, identifies the business clearly and supports claims with evidence.** GEO and AEO build on sound SEO. No setting, schema type or writing formula can guarantee a citation.

That is why terms such as **GEO**, **AEO** and **AI SEO** are appearing more often. They sound technical, but the principle is straightforward: your website and content must be clear, reliable and structured enough for people, search engines and AI systems to understand when your business is relevant.

For SMEs, this is a meaningful development, not a promise of automatic visibility. Clear content, strong service pages, local SEO, appropriate structured data, genuine case studies and demonstrable expertise make information more useful to visitors and easier for search and answer systems to interpret.

<NoticeBox variant="warning" title="Important update from July 2026">
Google treats AEO and GEO for its generative Search features as part of SEO. Its official guidance states that there is no special AI schema, no mandatory `llms.txt` file and no ideal page length for AI. Unique experience, crawlability, sound technology, strong media and useful content remain the basis. Read Google's current [guidance for generative Search features](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/ai-optimization-guide).
</NoticeBox>

This guide explains what GEO and AEO mean, how they differ from conventional SEO and how to prepare a website for a future in which Google, ChatGPT and other AI systems provide more answers directly.

<QuoteBlock>
AI visibility does not start with tricks. It starts with clear, reliable and well-structured information.
</QuoteBlock>

## GEO in one minute

**GEO** stands for **Generative Engine Optimization**: improving digital content so that generative search and answer systems can find and understand it accurately and may use it as a source. The term was developed in the original [GEO: Generative Engine Optimization](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.09735) research, but there is no universal switch that forces a mention.

For an SME, GEO is about clear entities, substantiated claims, sufficient context and coherent relationships between services, case studies and knowledge articles. For its own generative Search features, Google explicitly places this work within the existing SEO foundation.

## AEO in one minute

**AEO** stands for **Answer Engine Optimization**. It organises a page so that a specific question first receives a clear, self-contained answer, followed by the necessary nuance, explanation and evidence. This helps readers who need quick orientation and makes the answer structure more explicit to search engines.

AEO is broader than adding an FAQ at the foot of a page. It begins with question research, descriptive headings, factually complete answer blocks and a logical next step.

## How do SEO, AEO and GEO fit together?

SEO provides crawlability, indexability, internal structure and relevance. AEO improves answer formats. GEO makes claims, entities and evidence easier to interpret in generative contexts. Google explicitly treats work for its generative Search features as part of SEO. It does not prescribe a separate AI schema, an ideal AI word count or a mandatory `llms.txt` file.

## Why does this matter for SMEs?

Many SMEs assume that AI visibility matters only to large brands. It does not. Local businesses in particular can benefit from clear, specific and reliable content.

An AI system needs context. If a website is vague, the company is hard to understand. If it explains who the business is, what it does, whom it serves, where it operates and which case studies support that story, it becomes much easier to interpret.

Vague copy says: "We provide end-to-end solutions for your digital growth." It sounds polished but reveals little. Compare it with: "VisualVibe helps SMEs in Limburg, Belgium, with web design, SEO, corporate photography, video, drone footage, 3D tours and podcasting. We combine strategy, media and technology to improve online visibility and generate more enquiries."

## What is GEO?

The term became widely known through research into Generative Engine Optimization, but industry use is broader and still developing. In practice, GEO combines editorial clarity, technical access, evidence and entity consistency. Treat claims about platform behaviour cautiously and check current first-party documentation.

## Entities: state clearly who you are and what you do

An entity is something identifiable: a brand, person, service, region, sector, product or topic. Use the same business name, service terms, contact details and geographic descriptions across the website.

"VisualVibe is a creative media agency in Limburg, Belgium, helping SMEs with web design, SEO, photography, video, drone footage, 3D tours and podcasting" is far more precise.

## Structured data and schema

Structured data can give search engines explicit information about a page. It is not a ranking switch and, under [Google's general structured data guidelines](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/structured-data/sd-policies), it must match what visitors can actually see.

<FeatureGrid columns={3}>
  <FeatureCard title="Organisation and local business">Use `Organization` or `LocalBusiness` for accurate organisation details and local business context.</FeatureCard>
  <FeatureCard title="Service, article and navigation">Where appropriate, use `Service`, `BlogPosting`, `BreadcrumbList` or `CreativeWork` for the visible page type.</FeatureCard>
  <FeatureCard title="Media and reviews">Use `ImageObject`, `VideoObject`, and only honest, visible `Review` or `AggregateRating` data.</FeatureCard>
</FeatureGrid>

A knowledge article like this may use `BlogPosting` and `BreadcrumbList`; a service page may use `Service`; a contact page may support `LocalBusiness` or `Organization`; and a case study may use `CreativeWork` or suitable project information.

Structured data must never contain hidden invention. It does not create an entitlement to an AI citation, and Google requires no special structured data for generative results.

## Crawler access: Search is not the same as AI training

Check `robots.txt`, page robots directives, canonical tags, authentication and firewall rules separately. For Google's generative Search features, pages must be indexed and eligible for a search result snippet. Google lists no additional technical admission requirement that guarantees inclusion.

OpenAI makes an important distinction in its [official crawler documentation](https://developers.openai.com/api/docs/bots):

<FeatureGrid columns={2}>
  <FeatureCard title="OAI-SearchBot for search">This crawler supports discovery for ChatGPT search features. Manage access separately in `robots.txt` and at infrastructure level.</FeatureCard>
  <FeatureCard title="GPTBot for model training">This crawler concerns content that may be used to train generative models. Allowing or blocking it is separate from the decision for OAI-SearchBot.</FeatureCard>
</FeatureGrid>

This distinction prevents a common mistake: treating every AI crawler as one setting. Document the business purpose and privacy decision behind each crawler. OpenAI also explains the choice in its [FAQ for publishers and developers](https://help.openai.com/en/articles/12627856-publishers-and-developers-faq).

<NoticeBox variant="info" title="Access is necessary, never sufficient">
A crawlable page may be eligible to be found or cited. No crawler setting guarantees a position, citation, click or lead. Measure technical access, content quality and outcomes separately.
</NoticeBox>

## How do you measure visibility in generative results?

Do not turn an invented GEO score into the end goal. Combine visibility, source referrals and business outcomes. Available reports differ by platform and may still change.

As of July 2026, Google is rolling out its dedicated [generative AI performance report](https://support.google.com/webmasters/answer/16984139) gradually to some site owners. For other properties, AI features remain within general Search Console reporting. Microsoft describes citations, cited pages and grounding queries in [Bing AI Performance](https://blogs.bing.com/webmaster/February-2026/Introducing-AI-Performance-in-Bing-Webmaster-Tools-Public-Preview) as early visibility signals. OpenAI states that ChatGPT Search referrals include `utm_source=chatgpt.com`. Compare trends over a longer period and always connect them with landing pages and genuine conversions.

FAQs can resolve real follow-up questions quickly, but they must add substance rather than append five commercial prompts to every page. Google stopped showing FAQ rich results on 7 May 2026, so a good FAQ is an editorial choice, not a trick for occupying extra search-result space.

## Content that AI can understand more easily

AI-friendly copy is not vague. Follow the same [people-first quality principles](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/creating-helpful-content) that apply to ordinary search results: show who created the information, what supports its claims and whom it genuinely helps.

GEO is not solely about text. Original company photography, project footage, video and case studies add context and evidence that generic stock media cannot provide.

AI systems may not extract every visual detail as a person would. The surrounding context therefore matters. A photograph of a corporate shoot communicates more when the adjacent text explains who is shown, what is happening, where it took place and why it matters.

## Internal links connect your expertise

A useful knowledge base is not a set of isolated articles. Natural internal links show how an explanation relates to a service, region, case study or deeper guide. Avoid generic anchors such as "click here" and do not create links to pages that do not exist in the same locale.

## Practical GEO and AEO checklist

Check the direct answer, heading hierarchy, examples, terminology, authorship, organisation details, metadata, internal links, structured data, mobile performance and evidence. Keep names and claims consistent across pages. Use local context only where it is genuinely relevant.

## What can SMEs do now?

Make services concrete, state the real service area, answer questions customers actually ask, show evidence through case studies and media, build meaningful internal links, and add structured data that matches visible content. You do not need a perfect AI strategy before improving these fundamentals.

GEO and AEO sound new, but the foundation is practical. Explain who you are, what you do, whom you serve, where you work and what supports your claims. Combine useful content and genuine evidence with crawlability, internal structure and accurate metadata.

AI changes how people search, but not what they need: clarity, trust and proof.
