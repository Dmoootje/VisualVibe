# Online course or live training: which format fits your learning goal?

<LeadIntro>
Choose an **online course or live training** based on the task participants need to complete afterwards. Online works well for repeatable explanations, demonstrations and independent practice. Live guidance is more valuable when observation, direct feedback, collaboration or a safe practice situation is central. A hybrid process only combines both when each part has a clear function of its own.
</LeadIntro>

So the choice is not simply about digital versus human. An online course can also include personal guidance and a classroom course can use digital preparation. The real question is where explanation, practice, feedback and application best take place.

Anyone who only looks at reach sometimes digitizes exactly the part that was valuable live. If you keep everything live, participants always follow the same basic explanation and make scaling up unnecessarily difficult. Therefore, start with learning function, context and risk. Only then choose recording format, platform and schedule.

<QuoteBlock>
Digitize what participants can process independently. Save live time for what needs observation, conversation, practice and targeted feedback.
</QuoteBlock>

## What is the difference between an online course and a live training?

An online course is a planned learning route that participants complete completely or partially at their own pace. Video can provide explanations and demonstrations, but ideally it forms one component alongside text, assignments, downloads, feedback and progress. A live training brings participants and supervisor together at the same time, physically or via an online session. This allows the trainer to immediately respond to questions and behavior.

A recording of a live lesson is not yet a designed online course. The timing, audience questions and references to participants present are made for the original moment. An independent viewer misses that context. Conversely, a series of polished videos is not a complete training without practice, guidance and application.

## Start with what the participant should be able to do

A topic is not yet a learning objective. “Work safely”, “sell better” or “know our software” does not indicate which behavior needs to change. Describe a recognizable task: an employee can shut down a machine in the correct order, an advisor can analyze a customer question or a user can correctly create a file.

Then ask what it takes to perform that task reliably. Should someone only recognize a fixed order? Then a short demonstration may be sufficient. Does the participant need to judge subtle signals, argue or adjust physical technique? Then practice and feedback are probably more important than extra explanation videos.

## When is a hybrid course the strongest choice?

Hybrid learning is not automatically an online course plus a class day. It only works when the two parts are given different, connected tasks. For example, let participants view concepts and a demonstration in advance. Then use the live session for diagnosis, role play, equipment, feedback and exceptional situations. Afterwards, a compact video library can serve as a memory aid.

Make that transition explicit. In the online part, tell what someone needs to complete before the session and why. Do not start live with the same presentation, but build on the preparation. Have the follow-up refer back to exact modules instead of “the videos”. This way the participant experiences one process instead of three separate channels.

## Can you simply record an existing live training?

Yes, when the purpose is registration, review or documentation. No, if you expect an independent online course without further processing. Live training often includes welcomes, breaks, group assignments, side conversations and questions without repeated context. These elements can be valuable for attendees, but make a long registration difficult to navigate.

Decide what the final product should be in advance. A complete recording preserves the event. A summary shows key moments. Modular lessons are reassembled around defined questions. A new online course uses the existing training primarily as a content source and redesigns the learning journey. The guide to [short versus long video](/en/kennisbank/videografie/short-form-vs-long-form-video/) helps link length to purpose. For the production itself, see our [training video production service](/en/diensten/masterclasses/training-video-production/).

For targeted registration, the guide on a [recording masterclass for companies](/en/kennisbank/masterclasses/record-a-masterclass-for-business/) will help. If it is an interactive session with participants, read how to professionally record a [workshop](/en/kennisbank/masterclasses/filming-a-workshop-professionally/).

'We will decide what to do with the footage later' leaves framing, permissions, audio and editing unnecessarily uncertain. Decide in advance whether you need an archive, course module, promotional video, short knowledge clip or a combination.
</NoticeBox>

## How do you make online learning accessible and usable?

Accessibility starts in design. The W3C guideline [Making Audio and Video Media Accessible](https://www.w3.org/WAI/media/av/) describes, among other things, subtitles, transcript, description of meaningful visual information and an accessible media player. The facilities required depend on the material, the audience and the applicable requirements.

Therefore, do not just let a speaker say “click here” or “as you see on this graph”. Also mention the button, trend or meaning out loud. Use sufficient legible text, limit distracting background music and check subtitles for technical terms and names. Don't deliver important instructions exclusively through one sense.

Accessibility also helps in everyday situations: participants watch without sound, work in a noisy environment, search for a single term or process information in another language. Plan transcript, captions and source files during pre-production; repairs afterwards usually require more editing and control.

## Which platform do you need?

Don't choose a learning platform based on the video player alone. First determine how participants gain access, in what order they work, where assignments are located, who answers questions and what data is actually needed. A normal website may be suitable for a public knowledge library. An internal or commercial process may need accounts, roles, progress, evaluation or certification.

For example, Moodle documents separate capabilities for [course completion](https://docs.moodle.org/502/en/Course_completion) and [course reports](https://docs.moodle.org/en/Course_reports). Such functions provide signals about activity and completion, but do not automatically prove that someone has mastered the skill. Combine platform data with appropriate assignments, observations or results in the work environment.

## How do you budget production and supervision fairly?

Don't just compare the number of video minutes or recording days. The actual scope consists of content analysis, learning path, scripts, presentation coaching, recording, editing, graphics, subtitling, quality control, publication and subsequent updates. An online process also shifts part of the recurring teaching time to production and management.

Live learning has other recurring costs: trainer time, location, scheduling, travel, and participants who need to be available at the same time. Hybrid can save resources when basic explanations are not repeated each time, but becomes more complex when two separate paths are maintained side by side.

Therefore, ask in a quote which deliverables, revision rounds, source files, formats, captions, usage rights and publication support are included. Also describe what the organization itself provides: substantive ownership, approval, platform, participant support and update decisions.

## How do you measure which form works?

First measure behavior that fits the learning objective. A watched video or completed module can show that someone has opened or completed a step according to platform rules. It does not by itself prove understanding or application. Therefore, include appropriate evidence: a decision in a case, a demonstration, a controlled task, feedback from a supervisor or change in relevant work errors.

Use questions from live sessions and support as a content signal. When several participants get stuck at the same step, the explanation, exercise or prior knowledge may be unclear. If a module is hardly used, it may be redundant, difficult to find or installed at the wrong time. Only draw conclusions after you have examined the context.

## How do you deal with participants, privacy and usage rights?

Visualizing a trainer and filming a room of participants are different situations. The Data Protection Authority explains in the [Principles of the Right to Image](https://www.gegevensbeschermingsautoriteit.be/index.php/burger/thema-s/recht-op-afbeelding/principes) that consent for the creation and further use of images must be considered separately. Determine the purpose, channels, duration and types of reuse in advance and seek legal advice when the specific situation requires it.

Also design a workable camera-free zone or recording angle when participants do not need to be clearly visible. Remove personal data from screen recordings and examples. Check permissions on slides, photos, excerpts, music, templates and guest material. An internally intended recording may not tacitly become an advertisement or paid course.

## Only choose the form after the learning route is clear

The choice between online course and live training becomes easier once the goal, participant, exercise and evidence are concrete. Repeatable explanations and demonstrations can often be done digitally. Complex application, collaboration and direct feedback deserve a guided moment. Hybrid works when both parts demonstrably prepare and reinforce each other.

If you want to restructure existing teaching material step by step, read how to convert a [training course into video content](/en/kennisbank/masterclasses/turn-training-into-video-content/). In this way, the choice of form does not become a separate media decision, but part of a maintainable knowledge product.
