<LeadIntro>
**Drone footage for businesses reveals what is difficult to see from the ground:** the scale of a site, the setting of a property, progress on a construction project or the energy of an event. Its value does not come from the drone alone, but from a purposeful viewpoint, a clear story and a flight that can be carried out safely and lawfully. Choose between photography, conventional drone video and FPV first. Only then should you settle on the aircraft, route and filming day.
</LeadIntro>

A high viewpoint is not automatically a strong viewpoint. Looking straight down at a roof, car park or business site can be informative, but may lose the human scale. A fast FPV flight can look spectacular, yet feel wrong for a particular location or audience. Professional production starts with a question: what should viewers understand, feel or do after seeing the image?

This pillar guide helps you make that choice. It covers uses, formats, briefing, flight planning, privacy, delivery and current rules. For every actual flight, the operator remains responsible for checking the conditions that apply at that location and time.

## When does drone footage genuinely add value?

Drone footage is strongest when height, route or spatial relationships add information. One view can show a building, its entrance, parking and surroundings together. On a construction project, it can explain how different zones relate. In video, a movement from outside to inside can literally guide viewers through a location.

## The current European and Belgian framework

EU rules classify drone operations by risk, not by whether filming is commercial. The legal basis includes [Implementing Regulation (EU) 2019/947](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg_impl/2019/947/oj). Open, Specific and Certified operations have different conditions.

Many lower-risk operations can take place in Open when all conditions are satisfied. EASA covers A1, A2 and A3 subcategories, aircraft class, pilot competency, distances from people and a height below 120 metres. Always consult the current [EASA Open category guidance](https://www.easa.europa.eu/en/domains/drones-air-mobility/operating-drone/open-category-low-risk-civil-drones).

<ComparisonTable headers={["Category", "General risk profile", "Practical meaning"]} rows={[

  ["Certified", "High risk", "A strict framework closer to manned aviation and not to an ordinary corporate shoot"],

The Belgian [Open category summary table](https://mobilit.belgium.be/nl/luchtvaart/vliegen-met/drones-uas/categorieen/categorie-open/overzichtstabel) brings together current combinations of subcategory, aircraft, mass, training, registration, Remote ID and geo-awareness. General Open conditions include no flight over an assembly of people, a maximum of 120 metres above ground, direct visual line of sight and compliance with geozones.

<NoticeBox variant="danger" title="This article does not grant flight permission">
Rules, geozones and local conditions can change and depend on the aircraft, route, people, location and operation. Only current official information and required approvals determine whether a specific flight can proceed. Ask a competent authority or qualified operator when in doubt.
</NoticeBox>

## Registration, training, insurance and geozones in Belgium

[General drone information from FPS Mobility](https://mobilit.belgium.be/nl/luchtvaart/vliegen-met/drones-uas/algemene-informatie/algemene-informatie) describes five practical preparation steps: operator registration where required, suitable insurance, training for the operation, an aircraft mass and class check, and a geozone check before every flight.

Operator registration is generally relevant for a non-toy camera drone, including below 250 grams. Official instructions require the operator number on the aircraft and, where applicable, in Remote ID. Pilot and operator may be the same person, but their responsibilities differ.

Belgian registration asks for insurance information. The [FPS drone FAQ](https://mobilit.belgium.be/nl/luchtvaart/vliegen-met/drones-uas/veelgestelde-vragen) discusses third-party liability insurance and recommends checking policy terms carefully. A general business policy does not automatically cover a drone operation.

The official Belgian [Droneguide map](https://map.droneguide.be) shows geographical UAS zones and their access conditions. A location that technically fits Open may still fall within a zone with additional requirements or a prohibition. Where zones overlap, FPS Mobility states that the strictest condition applies. Some zones require an advance application through the DAA planner to the geozone manager.

## FPV needs its own safety approach

An FPV pilot watches a live video feed, but Open operations still require VLOS. The Belgian FPS FAQ states that a visual observer is mandatory during genuine FPV flight. The observer helps monitor the aircraft and its surroundings directly. The applicable A1 or A3 conditions also depend on factors including weight, speed and aircraft.

EASA explains in its [FPV guidance on VLOS and visual observers](https://www.easa.europa.eu/en/node/140038) why goggles do not replace direct visual contact. Our complete guide to [FPV video for business](/en/kennisbank/drone/fpv-video-for-business/) applies that framework to route selection, rehearsals, production and useful corporate content.

Scout every indoor or outdoor FPV route in advance. Loose materials, glass walls, cables, audiences, animals and moving machinery can make a planned line unsafe. Rehearse in a controlled way. A spectacular take never matters more than the ability to stop safely.

The location may need to be temporarily cleared during filming. Staff and visitors need clear instructions. A route through an active warehouse or event is not automatically feasible merely because it sounds creatively attractive.

## Privacy and data protection

An aerial camera may capture people, number plates, gardens, windows, screens and other identifiable information. Aviation permission is not permission to publish every image.

The [EASA Privacy Handbook](https://www.easa.europa.eu/en/domains/civil-drones/privacy/privacy-handbook) helps operators consider data protection during planning. It addresses purpose limitation, necessity, transparency, retention and circumstances in which a data protection impact assessment may be relevant.

## Light, weather and continuity determine image quality

Wind affects both safety and battery life as well as movement. Rain may prevent flight. Low sun creates long shadows, while midday can make a site look flat. A production plan therefore includes a preferred time, weather limit and alternative.

Repeatability matters more than a fresh spectacular angle for periodic construction footage. Record position, direction, height, focal length, time and crop. You can then compare progress without implying that two entirely different viewpoints show the same thing.

Colour grading can unify footage from different cameras, but cannot completely repair poor light or soft source images. Repeat essential movements when safety, battery and schedule allow.

## Delivery for websites, social media and archives

Do not ask merely for “the footage”. Define outputs. Photography may require a high-resolution selection and web variants. Video may need a master, short edits, vertical crops, captions and selected individual shots for future use.

Websites need compressed files, suitable dimensions and stable aspect ratios. Large video files should not block the first screen. Our guide to [website development in Limburg](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/complete-guide-to-building-a-website-in-limburg/) explains how media, structure and conversion planning work together.

Use descriptive file names and alt text that explains the image. “Business drone photo” is less useful than “business site with production hall, loading area and visitor parking in Limburg”. Describe only relevant visible content.

## How do you compare quotations for professional drone footage?

Quotations are comparable only when scope is concrete. A day rate says little: one assignment may include location research, flight preparation, editing and several formats, while another covers recording only at a pre-approved location. Ask what preparation, production time and delivery are included, and which assumptions still need checking.

Start with the result. How many finished photos or video versions are required? Must one production supply a landscape website video, vertical social edit and archive footage? Are music, grading, captions or editing with ground footage included? Agree revision rounds and when additional editing becomes extra work.

Review operational planning next. A sound quotation identifies location, proposed routes, aircraft, risk category, geozones, possible applications and responsible operator. It cannot guarantee every outcome in advance. Geozone-manager conditions, site changes or unsafe weather may still affect filming. Include a reserve date, alternative viewpoints and travel-cost arrangements.

Ask about insurance, relevant pilot training and the treatment of people, number plates and sensitive company information. On construction or industrial sites, a contact person, access rules, personal protective equipment and coordination with the safety lead can matter as much as the camera. FPV quotes should account for route scouting, an observer and extra safety margins.

Finally, settle usage rights and retention. Record channels, territories and duration, whether music licences cover them, and whether partners may reuse footage. Agree whether raw files are supplied, how long source material is retained and who archives final files. The best choice is not automatically the cheapest flight, but the provider that joins creative quality, feasibility and responsibilities most clearly.
