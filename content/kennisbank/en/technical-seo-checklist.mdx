# Technical SEO checklist for SME websites

<LeadIntro>
A **technical SEO checklist** checks whether search engines can reach your important pages, process them and link them to the correct URL. Therefore, do not start with a general tool score. First, check status codes, crawlability, indexing signals, canonicals, internal links, and mobile content. Only then will structured data, speed and further optimization have their proper place.
</LeadIntro>

Technical SEO does not automatically make a page relevant or convincing. It does prevent good content from becoming impossible to find, duplicated or difficult to use due to a technical error. A perfect sitemap does not compensate for an empty service story. Conversely, an excellent guide may not generate much search traffic if the canonical points to a different URL or a temporary 'noindex' remains after going live.

Use this checklist for a new website, a migration or a periodic audit. It is written for SMEs and their web partners, but consciously remains platform neutral. The exact implementation differs between Next.js, WordPress, Shopify and customization. If you want to have the check carried out by a specialist, see [technical SEO](/en/diensten/seo/technische-seo/).

<NoticeBox variant="warning" title="An audit is not a ranking promise">
The technical basis determines whether a page is eligible for processing and serving, but Google does not guarantee indexing or ranking. Content, search intent, competition, reputation and many other systems continue to play a role.
</NoticeBox>

## Work with priorities, evidence and owners

Not every report is equally important. A blocked sales page requires action faster than a missing description on an old news item. Therefore, register per finding which URLs are affected, what the observable effect is, who solves it and how you check the solution.

Keep a small log with each release. Make a note of changed templates, redirects, robot rules, structured data and measurement codes. When traffic changes later, you can rule out or confirm technical causes more quickly.

## Check 1: Can the server deliver a usable page?

Google lists three minimum technical conditions to be eligible for indexing: Googlebot is not blocked, the page shows a successful HTTP status and the content is indexable. This is stated in the official [Google Search Technical Requirements](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/essentials/technical). Meeting these conditions does not guarantee inclusion in the index.

Don't just test the homepage. Check at least a service page, knowledge article, category, case, form and any product detail. Technical errors are often in a template, resulting in dozens of URLs sharing the same problem.

## Check 2: robots.txt, noindex and access rules

`robots.txt` determines which crawl requests a crawler is allowed to execute. It is not a reliable method to remove a URL from search results. A blocked URL may still be known as a URL under certain circumstances, while the crawler cannot retrieve the page to read a 'noindex'.

Google's [robots.txt documentation](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/crawling-indexing/robots/intro) makes that distinction clear. Use access security for confidential environments. Use a supported indexing directive when a publicly accessible page should not appear in search results.

Make a list of pages that are deliberately not indexable, such as internal search results or certain account screens. This way, a 'noindex' does not become an unexplained exception that is subject to discussion during every audit.

## Check 3: XML sitemap and internal discovery

An XML sitemap is a list of URLs that you care about. Google emphasizes that a sitemap is just a hint and does not guarantee crawling or indexing. Follow the official guide to build and submit a [sitemap](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/crawling-indexing/sitemaps/build-sitemap).

Only include canonical URLs that give a successful status and are intended for indexing. Remove redirecting, erroneous, duplicate and 'noindex' URLs. Use a proper `lastmod` only when that date reflects a meaningful substantive update.

A sitemap does not replace internal links. Google discovers many pages through links and recommends plain `<a>` elements with an `href` and descriptive anchor text. The full guideline can be found at [crawlable links and anchor texts](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/crawling-indexing/links-crawlable).

## Check 4: canonicals and duplicate URLs

A canonical is the preferred signal for a page when the same or very similar content is reachable via multiple URLs. It is not an automatic redirect for visitors or a way to merge random pages.

Google treats a rel="canonical" as a strong signal, but may choose a different canonical when signals contradict each other. The [canonical URLs](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/crawling-indexing/consolidate-duplicate-urls) documentation recommends, among other things, absolute URLs, consistent internal links, and only canonical URLs in the sitemap.

Check:

- every indexable page has a self-referring or deliberately chosen canonical;
- the canonical returns 200 and is not blocked or `noindex`;
- HTTP, HTTPS, www, non-www and slash variants are consistent;
- tracking parameters do not unnecessarily create standalone indexable versions;
- language and region versions do not erroneously canonicalize to one language;
- pagination and filters have a deliberate indexing strategy;
- structured data, sitemap and internal links call the same URL.

Don't canonicalize a local page to a global page when the local page has truly unique content and independent intent. Conversely, a copied city page is not a unique destination because the city name is different. The pillar about [local SEO in Limburg](/en/kennisbank/seo-geo/local-seo-for-smes-in-limburg/) helps determine that substantive boundary.

## Check 5: redirects and website refresh

Redirects send visitors and crawlers from an old URL to a new destination. If you are moving permanently, use a permanent server redirect and choose the most appropriate end page in terms of content. Sending an entire old website to the homepage makes the route less usable for visitors.

The Google guide on [redirects and Search](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/crawling-indexing/301-redirects) describes supported methods and their interpretation. Keep redirects active long enough after a migration, update internal links and prevent chains from old to older to new.

## Control 6: Mobile, Rendering, and JavaScript

Google mainly uses the mobile version of the content for indexing and ranking. Mobile should therefore not only be visually more compact; important text, links, images, metadata and structured data must remain equivalent in content. Read the current [best practices for mobile-first indexing](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/crawling-indexing/mobile/mobile-sites-mobile-first-indexing).

Test on a real phone and with a slower connection. Check that navigation opens, accordions are operable, text isn't truncated, forms have clear labels, and important links don't only exist on desktop.

JavaScript is not necessarily an SEO problem. It becomes a risk when the main content only appears after a failed client action, links don't have a real 'href', or error handling leaves an empty page. Server rendering or static generation can provide a robust foundation for public content, but that approach must also be properly tested.

## Check 7: structured data and visible facts

Structured data helps systems interpret information on a page, but does not validate invisible or incorrect claims. Google requires markup to correctly display the visible main content and warns that valid markup does not guarantee a rich result. See the [general structured data guidelines](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/structured-data/sd-policies).

Only use types that really fit, for example `Organization`, `LocalBusiness`, `Service`, `Article`, `BreadcrumbList`, `Product` or `VideoObject`. Check required and recommended properties, valid URLs and consistent names. Don't add a review score that the visitor can't see on the page and don't confuse FAQ display with a guaranteed FAQ result.

Structured data is also not a special AI ticket. In [what AEO means](/en/kennisbank/seo-geo/what-is-aeo/) and [what GEO means](/en/kennisbank/seo-geo/what-is-generative-engine-optimisation/) you can read how direct answers, entities and source-worthy content compare to regular SEO. The pillar about [AI search results and GEO](/en/kennisbank/seo-geo/how-to-appear-in-ai-search-results-geo-aeo/) connects that content with technology.

## Check 8: Core Web Vitals and Accessibility

Measure performance per key template and with multiple data sources. Lab tests aid in diagnosis; field data shows what real Chrome users experience under their circumstances. Google recommends good Core Web Vitals, but explicitly says that good metrics alone do not guarantee high rankings.

The current good limits are LCP within 2.5 seconds, INP within 200 milliseconds and CLS maximum 0.1, rated at the 75th percentile. Read the definitions and nuance in [Google about Core Web Vitals](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/core-web-vitals) and the practical explanation in [why a fast website can rank better](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/why-a-fast-website-can-rank-better/).

First, look for causes that affect multiple pages: slow server response, oversized hero images, blocking scripts, unstable dimensions, heavy fonts, and external widgets. Do not remove essential content to turn a score green.

Accessibility is not a ranking hack, but it is a quality condition for many users. The [WCAG 2.2](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/) covers observability, operability, understandability and robustness. Test keyboard navigation, focus, labels, error messages, contrast, zoom, reflow and text alternatives, among other things. An automatic scanner finds only some of the problems.

## Check 9: Local, content, and AI signals remain consistent

Technology must support visible reality. Company name, address, services and regions may not differ per template. Let website, structured data and Google Business Profile use the same verifiable facts. The guide [Optimize Google Business Profile](/en/kennisbank/seo-geo/optimise-google-business-profile/) covers that external local layer; [local SEO](/en/diensten/seo/lokale-seo/) provides guidance on consistency.

Also check authorship, update information, and sources on knowledge articles. A technically indexable page with vague, generic text remains substantively weak. Use [SEO Content for Service Providers](/en/kennisbank/seo-geo/seo-content-for-service-businesses/) to fill technical templates with real expertise.

According to Google, the same basic principles continue to apply to generative search experiences: crawlable pages, useful content, good page experience and supported structured data. Separate AI tricks do not replace technical SEO. Within [AI SEO, AEO and GEO](/en/diensten/seo/ai-seo-aeo-geo/) technology, facts and content can be assessed together.

## Control 10: Go live and monitoring

An audit is only valuable when solutions are checked. Test in a representative environment before go-live and repeat critical checks on production. DNS, caching, environment variables and redirects may behave differently there.

<ChecklistBlock
  title="Acceptance before and after go-live"
  items={[
    "Robots.txt and noindex have been checked for production",
    "Important templates provide the expected status codes",
    "Canonicals, hreflang and structured data use production URLs",
    "Redirectmapping has been tested automatically and manually",
    "XML sitemap has been reached and submitted",
    "Forms and conversion measurement work with appropriate consent",
    "Search Console ownership and alerts are available",
    "404, 5xx and performance monitoring have an owner",
    "Backup and restore procedure have been tested",
    "An evaluation moment after days and weeks is planned,"
  ]}
/>

After going live, fluctuations are not automatically an error. First check whether important pages remain accessible and indexed and whether old URLs are forwarded correctly. Then compare template groups, queries and conversions. Document what you changed before performing a second major procedure.

## Technical SEO makes good content accessible

The best technical audit does not end with a hundred reports. It identifies which blockers to remove first, which templates need structural improvement and how to verify that the result works. Search intent, content and local credibility can then do their work.
