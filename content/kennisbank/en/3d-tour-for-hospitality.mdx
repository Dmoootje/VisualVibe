# 3D tour for hospitality: from space preparation to booking

<LeadIntro>
**A 3D tour for hospitality allows future guests to navigate through a restaurant, hotel, café, banquet hall or event venue themselves before making reservations or requesting information.** The tour especially helps when layout, atmosphere and route are part of the choice. It must match the current guest experience, cannot hide essential information, and needs a clear management plan for privacy, hosting, accessibility, and changes to the space.
</LeadIntro>

A photo can show a strong table setting or hotel room at a glance. Video can tell reception, service and atmosphere in motion. An interactive tour offers something different: the visitor determines how rooms are connected and can move from entrance to hall, terrace, room or meeting room at their own pace.

That does not make the tour an automatic booking machine. Availability, prices, menus, packages and terms still belong in managed copy and booking systems. The walkthrough supports expectations and orientation, after which visitors need a conventional, accessible route to continue.

A hospitality tour does not sell an empty space. It helps guests understand how the atmosphere, layout and intended use relate to a real visit.
</QuoteBlock>

## What is a 3D tour for hospitality?

A hospitality 3D tour is an interactive digital representation of a physical guest location. Visitors navigate between captured viewpoints and view the space around them. Depending on platform and capture, overview views, labels, links, media, measurement functions or a guided route may be available.

A restaurant tour can connect dining room, bar and terrace. A hotel can show room types, lobby, breakfast area and meeting rooms. An event venue tour explains the relationship between reception, main room, outdoor space and practical facilities. The tour is a snapshot: it shows the setup and condition during capture.

The pillar guide [what is a 3D tour](/en/kennisbank/3d-vr/what-is-a-3d-tour/) explains the difference between linked 360 panoramas, spatial digital twins, photography and video. For a specific production, see our [virtual hospitality tour service](/en/services/3d-vr-ar/hospitality-virtual-tour/).

## For which hospitality choice does a tour add value?

The tour is strongest when space answers a recurring question. A guest wants to know whether a restaurant feels intimate or open. An organizer wants to see how reception, dinner and dance floor can connect. A hotel guest compares room type, lobby and common areas. A business customer reviews meeting setup and route for participants.

A kitchen, staff room or storage is not automatically included in the tour. It can be interesting for transparency or rental, but raises operational, security and privacy questions. Only include a zone when the purpose is clear and publication can be done responsibly.

## Which guest and which action drives the tour?

A romantic dinner, family weekend, business meeting and wedding require different information. Choose one primary target group per page or tour view. Formulate what the visitor can assess better after the experience and what action logically follows: reserve a table, choose a room, download a venue brochure, ask for availability or book an on-site tour.

<NoticeBox variant="tip" title="Write the guest question first">
Complete: this visitor uses the tour to assess whether… If the route does not match that answer, additional scan points or effects are probably not a solution. First simplify scope, information and next steps.
</NoticeBox>

Let the CTA align with what the tour can prove. A spatial tour does not show current availability and does not confirm a customized arrangement. Therefore, link to the correct booking or contact page and make it clear which information applies there.

For multiple audiences, separate starting positions, guided routes or pages can be better than one crowded experience. Matterport describes on its page with [digital twin functions](https://matterport.com/digital-twin-features) guided tours, tags and different views, among other things; availability depends on platform and plan.

## Which spaces belong in the scope and route?

Map the guest journey from arrival to destination. Note the entrance, reception, main space, adjoining rooms, route to the toilets, lift or stairs, terrace, garden, parking context and relevant passageways. That does not mean everything has to be visible. The map helps determine what is essential, optional or excluded.

A hotel does not have to scan every identical room. Choose representative room types and clearly state that individual rooms may differ if so. A party room can have multiple setups; capture one true configuration and supplement it with regular photos or maps of alternatives. Don't present a digital adjustment as if it were the standard setup.

Test whether a new visitor understands the transition between rooms. An operator knows every back door and staircase; a guest does not. The virtual route must follow public logic and not suggest unintended backstage paths.

## How do you prepare location and timing for capture?

Schedule capture outside service, check-in, cleaning or setup. Moving people can appear incompletely or in multiple places in a composite image. An empty, stable location gives more control over privacy, light, table arrangement and passageways.

Prepare the room as if the next guest can quietly examine every detail. Align tables and chairs, replace damaged cards, hide cables, check glass and mirrors, remove cleaning materials and consciously open or close doors. Do not allow screens to show names, bookings or camera images.

Pay attention to daylight and seasonal decoration. A Christmas setting that remains visible all year round can quickly make the tour dated. Choose a timeless base unless a seasonal version is deliberately used temporarily and then replaced.

## How do you show the atmosphere and layout fairly?

A spatial viewer gives freedom, but camera and starting point choice still influence the impression. Avoid positions that suggest spaces are larger, brighter or quieter than a normal guest experiences them. Correct technical errors, but do not remove fixed features relevant to the choice.

Show table spacing, aisles and ceiling height in context without claiming that a marketing tour is a certified measurement document. Measurement functions can be platform and capture dependent. Official documentation and professional advice remain leading for capacity, fire safety, accessibility or installation plans.

An empty venue shows the architecture clearly but may lack atmosphere. Complement it with genuine photography or video from a representative occasion, rather than fabricated digital crowds. Describe alternative layouts in text and publish current capacities and terms on a managed venue page.

## Which tags and practical information are useful?

A tag can name a room type, open a venue brochure, clarify accessibility information or lead to a reservation page. Keep the interactive layer quiet. A visitor who sees a pop-up at every table loses spatial orientation.

Do not unnecessarily link rapidly changing information to the image. Menu, price, availability, package and opening time belong on pages that the hospitality team keeps up to date. Use the tour to reference that resource. Avoid hard-wired texts that become incorrect after one menu or price change.

Don't use tags as a substitute for navigation. The route itself must remain understandable without someone first reading instructional text in various pop-ups.

## How do you prevent privacy problems with guests and staff?

A hospitality environment quickly contains personal data: recognizable guests and employees, names on reservation screens, badges, planning, cash register data, camera monitors and personal photos. Schedule an capture without guests present and delete data before recording. Blurring afterwards is a safety net, not a complete preparation plan.

The GDPR at [EUR-Lex](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2016/679/oj) contains principles such as purpose limitation, data minimisation, accuracy, storage limitation and integrity. The final [EDPB guidelines on video devices](https://www.edpb.europa.eu/documents/guideline/guidelines-32019-on-processing-of-personal-data-through-video-devices_en) provide general guidance on image data, purposes and legal basis. Have the specific hospitality capture legally assessed when persons or other personal data may be visible.

Also check what the external tour service processes for visitors. Matterport publishes product and security information through the [Trust Center](https://matterport.com/trust); the owner also publishes a [CoStar Global Privacy Notice](https://www.costar.com/about/privacy-notice). Discuss roles, processing agreements, sub-processors, access management, cookies, retention periods and international transfers with your privacy officer.

For a hotel or residence, the boundary between public space and private context is extra important. Do not display occupied rooms, guest belongings or operational key information. Only make a representative, prepared space part of the public tour.

## How do you make the hospitality tour accessible?

A guest with a mobility, visual, cognitive or other disability may find an interactive 3D interface difficult to use. Provide essential information as plain text, photographs and, where useful, a simple floor plan too. Describe entrances, relevant routes and facilities factually, but do not allow a marketing tour to replace a formal accessibility audit.

The [WCAG 2.2](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/) covers text alternatives, keyboard controls, focus, contrast, reflow and animated content, among other things. Test the viewer on keyboard and mobile, check that focus doesn't freeze and give users control over automatic movement. Provide a clear way back to the regular page.

The [WAI Images Tutorial](https://www.w3.org/WAI/tutorials/images/) recommends tailoring alt text to function and context. “Restaurant interior” is often too general. For example, state that a photo shows the stairs between the foyer and the main room or the free passage to the terrace when that is the relevant information.

## How do you integrate tour, website, SEO and GEO?

Create your own page per relevant location or main application. Give the page a clear title, describe what the visitor sees in the tour and add current practical information. Only an iframe with a short sales slogan is thin content and hardly helps anyone who does not open the tour.

An interactive embed can be resource-intensive. Google explains in its guideline for [lazy-loaded website content](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/crawling-indexing/javascript/lazy-loading) that relevant content should load when it becomes visible and should not rely on interaction when Google needs to process that content. Keep important hospitality text, images and links on the page as plain HTML.

Use a recognizable preview and load external scripts purposefully. Test Core Web Vitals, mobile data usage, layout shifting and what happens when cookies or third-party content are rejected. The tour should not block the reservation path or hide the most important information under a slow interactive element.

For local SEO, correct name, address, opening hours, category, reviews and local evidence remain important. For GEO, a factual summary around the embed helps: which spaces, capacity types, facilities and contact routes exist? Don't guarantee that a search or AI system will run or quote the tour itself.

## How do you combine 3D tour, photography and video?

The three media have different tasks. A tour shows layout and route. Photography chooses strong moments for quick comparison and atmosphere. Video allows you to experience reception, preparation, service, movement, sound and human explanation. A menu and reservation page provides up-to-date decision information.

Use event photography or an aftermovie when the experience during a packed event is central. The [event photography checklist](/en/kennisbank/fotografie/event-photography-checklist/) helps plan key moments and rights. [an aftermovie of a company event](/en/kennisbank/videografie/corporate-event-aftermovie/) is included for movement and voice.

Schedule productions in separate time blocks. The 3D capture requires a stable, empty space; photography can use a styled table arrangement and video can just require guests, waiters or a chef. One unclear shooting day on which everything happens at the same time results in compromises for every medium.

## Who manages hosting, seasons and renovations?

Many interactive tours remain active via a cloud platform. For example, Matterport's current [subscription page](https://matterport.com/plans) distinguishes between active and archived spaces and between functions per plan. Always check current conditions, because subscriptions and options can change.

Record who manages the account and model, who pays recurring costs, who receives user rights and what happens when the collaboration or subscription ends. Ask which exports can be used independently and which experience remains platform-specific. Stills or a map are not the same as a transferable interactive tour.

Hospitality locations change due to seasonal decoration, renovation, new furniture, different room furnishings and adapted room types. Set triggers for control: renovation, new name, changed entrance, adjusted capacity or a non-representative setup. Quickly adjust links and text; plan recapture when the space itself changes significantly.

## How do you link the tour to reservations and information?

The tour must end in a logical route. A restaurant guest goes to the current reservation system. A hotel guest compares room type, conditions and availability. An event organizer requests a proposal with date, number of guests and desired setup. Do not use one general contact button for all situations if targeted forms exist.

Also place CTAs outside the viewer. A visitor does not have to find out how to make a reservation after an external interface opens. For each action, clearly indicate what follows: direct booking, request without confirmation, telephone discussion or venue viewing.

Keep tour and booking information separate but connected. The spatial recording may remain usable for a long time; current rates, menus and availability change more quickly. Therefore, link to the source that the team actually maintains and periodically check whether the route still works.

## How do you measure whether the tour is useful?

Link measurement to the original question. Did you want to better prepare room questions, clarify room types or give guests certainty about the atmosphere and route? Then look at relevant click-throughs, brochure downloads, contact questions and feedback, not just the number of starts of the viewer.

Ask employees if conversations change. Do organizers ask more specific questions? Do guests reference a space they saw on the tour? Do misunderstandings arise because a setup is outdated? That information helps more than an isolated visit counter.

Platform analytics have their own definitions for visits and interactions. Write them down and carefully compare periods. Season, price, availability, campaigns, reviews and website traffic may change at the same time. Do not claim a reservation effect without a measurement design that takes other explanations into account.

## What determines budget and lead time?

Surface area is only one factor. Number of floors, rooms, room types, outdoor zones, route complexity, preparation, travel time, post-processing, blurs, tags, different views, website integration, hosting and recapture together determine the scope.

A hotel with many similar rooms does not need to have each room as a separate public tour. An event venue with different setups may require additional photography and text rather than additional scans. Decide for each information request which delivery is useful.

In a proposal, request clarity about preparation, capture, correction, starting position, labels, links, privacy control, integration, subscription, ownership and maintenance. Compare total usage costs and responsibilities, not just the recording day rate.

## A hospitality tour only remains valuable if it is correct

A good 3D tour gives guests freedom to understand a real space, but maintains clear boundaries. It shows representative public areas, refers to current information and makes no greater promise than the location can deliver on site.

The capture becomes sustainable when someone remains responsible for hosting, links, privacy, changes and the booking journey. The virtual tour is not a separate technical object, but a managed part of the website, sales and guest communication.
