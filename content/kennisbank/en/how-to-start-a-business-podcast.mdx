# How to start a business podcast: from a strong format to measurable publication

<LeadIntro>
To **start a business podcast**, build a recurring editorial channel around questions your audience genuinely cares about. Do not begin with microphones or a well-known guest. Begin with a precise audience promise, a format your team can sustain and a clear reason to publish every episode. Recording, hosting and distribution only become efficient once that foundation is sound.
</LeadIntro>

A business podcast can explore specialist knowledge, guide customers, preserve conversations with peers or make complex subjects more human. That makes it valuable for SMEs, consultants and organisations with substantial expertise. It is not an automatic growth engine, however. Publishing episodes alone does not create reach, trust or enquiries. Relevance, consistency, quality, distribution and follow-up all need to work together.

This guide covers the entire process, from goals and format to pre-production, recording, hosting, distribution, rights, accessible transcripts and measurement. It will help you decide whether podcasting fits your communications, which choices need to be made upfront and when a [podcast production programme](/en/services/podcasting/podcast-production-programme/) makes sense.

<QuoteBlock>
A strong business podcast starts not with what you want to say, but with the question your audience will willingly return for.
</QuoteBlock>

## What exactly is a business podcast?

A business podcast is a recurring audio or video series produced by an organisation from a recognisable editorial perspective. Episodes may contain conversations, explanations, case studies, observations or stories. What sets it apart from a standalone promotional video is continuity: a podcast establishes an expected format within which several subjects fit naturally.

That does not mean every episode must sound the same. Listeners should, however, understand who the series is for, what kind of value it offers and why another episode could be relevant. A [business podcast](/en/services/podcasting/business-podcast/) is not a long advert. Your brand is the publisher, but the subject and the listener should drive the content.

## Define the business objective first

The question, “What could we talk about?” almost always produces too many ideas. Start with, “What change should this series support?” You may want to answer recurring customer questions, make internal expertise visible, encourage dialogue within your sector or strengthen relationships with guests. Choose one primary objective. Other benefits may emerge, but they should not all direct the format at once.

Translate that objective into behaviour you can observe. Should sales be able to send a specific episode in response to a question? Should a prospect be better able to compare approaches after listening? Does HR want to present real roles and professional knowledge? These uses are more concrete than “more visibility” and make later measurement more meaningful.

A podcast is less suitable when the subject offers little depth, nobody owns the content or you only need a short campaign peak. An article, webinar, case-study video or focused landing page may then be more efficient. Choosing a medium professionally also means being willing to decide that a series is not necessary yet.

## Write an audience promise that can sustain a series

A useful audience promise has three parts: who the podcast serves, which questions it addresses and from what recognisable perspective. “A podcast about business” is too broad. “Practical conversations for manufacturing businesses that want to explain technical knowledge more clearly to customers” gives direction to subjects, guests and tone.

Test the promise against at least ten possible episodes. If every topic is merely a variation of the same answer, the series may be too narrow. If almost anything fits beneath the title, it is too broad. Also consider whether the business has credible, lasting access to the required expertise, stories or guests. A promise without a sustainable source of content soon runs dry.

<NoticeBox variant="tip" title="Make the editorial boundary explicit">
Write down what falls outside the format as well as what belongs in it. This boundary prevents opportunistic episodes that may seem interesting in isolation but give the audience no clear reason to subscribe to the series.
</NoticeBox>

A personality-led format can work for experts and consultants, provided the content does not depend entirely on spontaneous inspiration. Our [podcast service for experts](/en/services/podcasting/podcast-for-experts/) therefore also starts from a repeatable perspective. It does not assume that expertise automatically produces a good episode. The guide to a [podcast for experts and consultants](/en/knowledge-base/podcasting/podcast-for-experts-and-consultants/) explores audience promises, evidence and season planning in more depth.

## Choose a format that suits both the content and the organisation

Format involves more than choosing between an interview and a solo episode. It includes the host, number of voices, recurring segments, usual depth, tone, publication rhythm and any video version. The best format is not necessarily the most spectacular. It is the one that remains strong within the time and people available.

A solo episode gives you control over structure and timing, but requires confidence and a robust script. An interview introduces other experiences, but also requires guest scheduling, preparation and consistent moderation. A panel can reveal nuance, while intelligibility and turn-taking become more complex. A narrative report can be very clear, but demands more editorial work and editing.

There is no universally ideal episode length or publishing frequency. A technical subject may deserve more time than a focused update. Remove repetition and internal pleasantries, but do not shorten an episode until its nuance disappears. One dependable episode a month can be stronger than a weekly series that ends after six weeks.

## Design a pilot before committing to a full year

A pilot makes assumptions tangible. Produce one representative episode as if it were going live, complete with a real introduction, content, edit, title, description, cover and CTA. Do not merely ask people in the target audience whether they “like” it. Ask what they understood, which section they would skip, what question remains and whether the title accurately reflects the content.

The pilot also tests the organisation. How much preparation did the guest need? Could the host ask useful follow-up questions? How long did editing and approval take? Who supplied the metadata and transcript? If a single episode already depends entirely on improvisation and overtime, a larger season will not solve the problem.

## Pre-production makes conversations more focused

A good conversation sounds spontaneous because preparation creates room to listen. Define the episode objective, likely listener, key questions, sensitive subjects, pronunciation of names and desired CTA in advance. Guests do not need to memorise answers, but they should know which context matters.

Use a running order: a concise sequence covering the opening, segments, possible follow-up questions, transitions and closing. Write the introduction and factual passages more precisely, while allowing experience-led stories to develop naturally. Verify figures, names and claims before recording so the edit does not depend on corrections that sound artificial afterwards.

Plan the logistics too. Confirm the date, location, arrival time, contact person, parking or access arrangements, quiet room, clothing for video and who authorises publication. For an on-location series, [professional podcast recording](/en/services/podcasting/podcast-recording/) is most valuable when editorial and technical preparation are aligned beforehand.

## Recording quality starts with the room and microphone technique

Expensive equipment cannot fix a hollow room, poor microphone distance or an interrupted conversation. Listen for ventilation, traffic, refrigerators, reverberation and activity next door. Soft materials and a smaller controlled space often help more than an extra camera. Choose microphones suited to the voices, movement and environment, and explain how close each participant needs to remain.

Monitor the recording through headphones. Check more than moving meters: speech should be clear, clothing should not rub and mobile signals or cables should not cause interference. Record each speaker separately where possible and make a safety recording or backup when the production warrants it. Note interruptions and strong moments to make editing more efficient.

Video adds decisions about camera angles, eyelines, lighting, background, glasses, screens and continuity. A [podcast video](/en/services/videography/video-podcast/) does not need frantic editing. Calm, consistent images keep attention on the conversation. Include vertical crops or short clips in scope only when they have their own narrative and a planned area for on-screen text from the outset.

## Hosting, RSS and listening apps are different layers

A podcast host stores media files and generally creates the RSS feed containing show and episode data. Listening apps read that feed and present the series to users. Your website can also provide its own episode page with a summary, transcript, sources and CTA. Each layer has a different function and should not be confused with the others.

Apple explains the RSS data, artwork and episode information required to submit a feed in its official [podcast requirements](https://podcasters.apple.com/support/823-podcast-requirements). Check the feed before launch and do not change its technical owner or URL without a migration plan. The RSS feed is a business asset, so keep access, account details and ownership agreements centrally documented.

Spotify's guidance on [finding and enabling an RSS feed](https://support.spotify.com/nl/creators/article/finding-and-enabling-your-rss-feed/) also explains that enabling it does not automatically send a podcast to every other platform. Record who submits, verifies and manages the series for each listening service. Use a monitored functional email address and consider which feed data becomes public.

<NoticeBox variant="info" title="Your host is not your complete distribution plan">
A working RSS feed enables technical distribution. Discoverability also requires a clear title, useful episode metadata, dedicated web pages, relevant internal links and active distribution through channels your audience already uses.
</NoticeBox>

## Give every episode its own web page

A listening app is convenient for subscribers, but your website can provide context and a logical next step. Create a unique page for each episode with a descriptive title, key answer, player, guest information, relevant sources, an accessible transcript and a related service or knowledge-base page. Readers can then scan the content without first searching in an app.

Connect the series to your wider content structure. An episode about a customer question may link to in-depth [SEO content for service businesses](/en/knowledge-base/seo-geo/seo-content-for-service-businesses/). Conversely, a knowledge-base article can offer a specific episode when the voice and example genuinely add insight. Write anchor text around the subject rather than repeatedly using “listen here”.

Publication then becomes a set of appropriate adaptations: a short email containing the key insight, a social clip with context, a quotation for a sector page and a direct reference that sales can use. Do not automatically cut twenty clips from every conversation. Select only extracts that make sense independently and represent the full episode fairly. The guide to [turning a podcast into social content](/en/knowledge-base/podcasting/turn-a-podcast-into-social-content/) provides a selection and publication matrix.

Text remains important for search and answer engines. A reviewed transcript makes statements searchable and citeable, but a raw speech-to-text export is not a finished article. Add structure, speaker labels, correct technical terms and links. This reflects the broader principles for [appearing in AI search results](/en/knowledge-base/seo-geo/how-to-appear-in-ai-search-results-geo-aeo/): provide clear answers, support facts and keep core information consistent on your own domain.

## Make transcripts and video accessible

For prerecorded audio, a transcript makes spoken information available as text. The W3C guidance on [accessible transcripts](https://www.w3.org/WAI/media/av/transcripts/) covers speaker identification and relevant non-speech sounds. Video requires accurate captions and may require further description if essential visual information is absent from the normal audio.

Accessibility starts in the script. State figures and concepts clearly, avoid having guests refer only to “this chart”, and describe meaningful images that listeners would otherwise miss. Check automatic captions for names, dialect, technical terms and punctuation. Ensure the player works with a keyboard and place the transcript and show notes logically beside the episode.

An accessible transcript also helps editors, sales teams and customers find a particular answer. Keep it readable without rewriting statements into a different meaning. Say when the text has been lightly edited and ask a content owner to approve sensitive corrections.

## Settle rights and consent before publication

A guest who agrees to be recorded has not automatically granted unlimited permission for every future edit, advert or video version. The Belgian Data Protection Authority distinguishes between permission to create an image and permission for its further use in its [principles of the right to image](https://www.gegevensbeschermingsautoriteit.be/index.php/burger/thema-s/recht-op-afbeelding/principes). Record the purpose, channels, duration, audio and video uses, short extracts and any paid promotion in writing.

Check music, sound effects, artwork, photographs, quotations and guest material too. The [copyright FAQ from FPS Economy](https://economie.fgov.be/sites/default/files/Files/Publications/files/FAQ_Auteursrecht.pdf) provides general information about copyright and use. Licences may vary by channel, territory, duration or commercial purpose. Keep proof of licences and state clearly in contracts who may adapt or republish source files and finished episodes.

Employees, customers, minors and sensitive topics may raise additional privacy or employment-law questions. This guide is not legal advice. Ask a suitable professional to assess any uncertainty in the context of the actual recording, relationship and intended publication.

## Measure the podcast against its chosen objective

Downloads and plays are not a uniform measure of attention. Apple's [listener analytics](https://podcasters.apple.com/support/5392-listener-analytics) show platform-specific signals such as listeners, engaged listeners and completion. Spotify's [analytics overview](https://support.spotify.com/us/creators/article/overview-analytics/) uses its own definitions for plays and other consumption signals. Do not compare figures blindly as though every environment measured the same thing.

The IAB Tech Lab [Podcast Measurement Technical Guidelines](https://iabtechlab.com/standards/podcast-measurement-guidelines/) offer a technical framework for download and audience measurement. Ask your host which version and certification it uses, but remember that a download does not prove understanding, brand preference or an enquiry.

## When should you choose a podcast rather than an online course?

A podcast suits recurring exploration, current topics, different perspectives and conversations that listeners need not follow in a fixed order. An online course requires learning outcomes, a modular path, practice and some form of progression. If participants need to develop a skill step by step, read the guide to [filming an online course](/en/knowledge-base/masterclasses/film-an-online-course/).

The formats can complement each other. A podcast can gather questions from the profession and encourage interest in a more in-depth programme. A course can use additional interviews as optional context. Keep the editorial purpose clear: a public episode is not automatically a lesson, and a course module need not work as a standalone podcast.

## From a good conversation to a sustainable publication process

A business podcast becomes valuable when content, production and distribution are designed together. The audience promise protects relevance. The pilot reveals whether the format and workload are realistic. Good pre-production protects the quality of the conversation. Hosting, owned web pages, transcripts, rights and measurement keep episodes useful after recording.

Start small enough to learn, but complete enough to test the entire chain. Then use content quality, organisational experience and audience signals to decide whether to continue, adjust or choose another medium. No microphone can guarantee growth. A clear system can, however, stop valuable expertise from being stranded in a collection of disconnected recordings.
