# What is a 3D tour? From capture to a useful virtual walkthrough

<LeadIntro>
**A 3D tour is an interactive digital representation of a real space that visitors navigate from one viewpoint to another.** Depending on the capture method and platform, it can include panoramic images, depth data, a spatial overview, labels, measurement tools or a guided route. It does not automatically replace photography, video, a construction plan or an in-person visit. Its value lies in orientation: people can understand a space's layout and atmosphere at their own pace.
</LeadIntro>

A property seeker wants to understand how rooms connect. A showroom customer wants to preview the available displays and styles. A hospitality guest wants to assess the atmosphere and accessibility of a space. In these situations, an interactive walkthrough provides different information from a carefully edited video.

This pillar guide explains when a 3D tour fits, how capture and publication work, and which arrangements are often made too late. Who manages the tour? How long does it remain online? What information appears in the images? Does the embed work on mobile? What accessible alternative is available to someone who cannot use the interactive environment?

## What makes a tour genuinely three-dimensional?

The term is used broadly. Some "3D tours" are collections of linked 360-degree panoramas. Other systems add depth and a spatial model, enabling a floor-plan or dollhouse view and measurement tools. Do not ask only whether a supplier produces a 3D tour. Ask what outcome and data you will actually receive.

A virtual marketing tour must not be used as a technical measurement document without verification. Accuracy depends on the device, capture, processing, space and exported file. Record whether the assignment covers presentation only or also a floor plan, point cloud, CAD, BIM or another technical file.

## 3D tours, VR and AR are not the same

A 3D tour is usually viewed in a standard web browser on a phone, tablet or computer. Some platforms support a VR view through a headset, but VR is not a requirement. Virtual reality places users inside an immersive digital environment. Augmented reality overlays digital elements on the real world.

For an SME, the terminology matters less than the use case. Should a prospect view a space without installing anything? A browser-based tour is a logical choice. Is spatial interaction part of training? A specialist VR solution may be needed. Do you want someone to place a virtual product in their real room? That points more towards AR.

<NoticeBox variant="info" title="Start with the task, not the label">Ask what visitors need to do, on which device and with what information. Only then choose between 360, 3D, VR, AR, video or a combination.</NoticeBox>

## A 3D tour starts before capture

The camera also records things that were never meant for publication: cables, dust, personal photographs, screens, documents, temporary signs and unfinished areas. Good preparation saves more time than trying to hide dozens of details afterwards.

## Prepare the space for consistent capture

A tour records the space from many directions. An object hidden from one viewpoint may be fully visible from another. Walk the entire route beforehand.

<FeatureGrid columns={2}>
  <ChecklistBlock title="Visual preparation" className="my-0 h-full" items={["Complete finishing, cleaning and styling", "Set doors deliberately open or closed for the route", "Keep lighting consistent within each room", "Remove temporary boxes, cables and maintenance equipment", "Check mirrors, windows and reflective surfaces", "Agree which outdoor views or adjoining rooms may be visible"]} />
  <ChecklistBlock title="Operational preparation" className="my-0 h-full" items={["Schedule a period without visitors or moving staff", "Appoint one location manager", "Confirm access, alarm, keys and safety instructions", "List prohibited or sensitive areas", "Provide power and network access where the system needs them", "Allow more time for large, complex or changing spaces"]} />
</FeatureGrid>

People moving between consecutive capture points can appear twice, partially or distorted. Avoid normal public operation during a complete capture unless the chosen method and privacy arrangements deliberately support it.

## Capture quality and measurement accuracy

Not every tour uses the same device. A smartphone, 360 camera, professional depth camera or laser scanner can produce different resolution, depth quality and workflows. The best choice depends on area, light, required output and budget.

For measurement or documentation purposes, ask for a stated accuracy, testing method and suitable export format. A measurement tool in a marketing viewer is not automatically intended for construction orders or safety-critical decisions. Have technical uses validated by the appropriate specialist.

Glass, mirrors, plain white surfaces, outdoor light, stairs and moving objects can make processing more difficult. An experienced capture plan uses enough viewpoints and checks the tour before delivery instead of relying solely on automated processing.

## Hosting, ownership and continuity

An interactive tour is often not a standalone file that remains on your web server forever. It may depend on a cloud platform, an active subscription and an embed. Hosting is therefore part of the purchase, not merely a technical detail.

Matterport is one well-known platform, but not the only option. Its current [Matterport plans](https://matterport.com/plans), for example, distinguish between active and archived Spaces. According to the provider, an active Space can be viewed, edited, published and shared. An archived Space cannot until it is reactivated. Features such as password protection, private embeds, blurring, analytics or technical exports vary by plan.

<ChecklistBlock title="Questions to include in the quotation" items={["Who owns and manages the platform account?", "Which annual or monthly hosting remains necessary?", "How long must the tour remain active and publicly available?", "Can the project be transferred to another account?", "Which source data and exports are actually delivered?", "What happens after archiving, cancellation or a price change?", "Who updates labels, links, privacy blurs and the starting position later?", "Which analytics are available and who can access them?"]} />

Agree an exit path too. A photo series, video, floor plan or raw capture data can be useful if interactive hosting later ends, but only when that output is explicitly included in the assignment and supported by the platform.

## Privacy: capture no more than necessary

A tour can show identifiable people, family photographs, number plates, computer screens, access badges, patient information, art collections or security details. Once images contain personal data, the [GDPR](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2016/679/oj) and the specific roles of client, supplier and platform become relevant.

The [EDPB guidance on video devices](https://www.edpb.europa.eu/system/files/2026-04/summary_edpb_guidelines_201903_video_devices_en.pdf) emphasises a clear legal basis, necessity, transparency and limiting processing to what the purpose requires. A commercial tour is not identical to video surveillance, but the same data-protection principles help teams plan capture responsibly.

  <ChecklistBlock title="Before capture" className="my-0 h-full" items={["Define purpose, legal basis and responsibilities", "Capture outside public opening hours where possible", "Remove personal and confidential objects", "Inform employees, residents or other people appropriately", "Exclude spaces that the purpose does not require"]} />
  <ChecklistBlock title="Before publication" className="my-0 h-full" items={["Check every room and navigation point", "Blur faces and other data where necessary", "Restrict public access when the purpose is internal", "Test that hidden or trimmed areas are genuinely inaccessible", "Record the retention period and deletion process"]} />
</FeatureGrid>

The [Matterport privacy policy](https://matterport.com/privacy-policy), for example, says that users are responsible for personal data in uploaded images and that this data may become publicly visible when a model is shared publicly. That is platform-specific, but it shows why a client must not assume that automated face blurring removes every privacy responsibility.

<NoticeBox variant="warning" title="Blur is a tool, not complete privacy control">Automated blurring may miss details and does not protect a floor plan, security route, document or personal interior. Prevention, manual review and access restrictions remain necessary.</NoticeBox>

## An accessible alternative belongs on the page

An interactive tour can be difficult or impossible to use with a screen reader, keyboard, magnification or alternative controls. It must not be the only way to reach essential information, contact details or a reservation.

[WCAG 2.2](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/) includes requirements for text alternatives to non-text content, keyboard operation, visible focus and avoiding keyboard traps. The exact criteria depend on the implementation, but the practical direction is clear.

<ChecklistBlock title="Always provide alongside the tour" items={["A text summary of the layout, functions and important spaces", "A logical photo sequence with suitable alt text", "A readable floor plan or overview where relevant", "Contact, reservation or enquiry buttons outside the embed", "Keyboard access and a clear way to leave the tour", "Information that is not explained through colour, position or movement alone"]} />

The [WAI Images Tutorial](https://www.w3.org/WAI/tutorials/images/) explains that a text alternative should serve the same purpose as the image. One alt attribute on an iframe cannot replace all the spatial information in a tour. Build a useful page around it.

## Put the tour on your website without sacrificing speed or clarity

An embed can be heavy. Load a tour below the fold only when it approaches the visible area and use a clear poster image or start button. Google's guidance on [lazy-loaded content](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/crawling-indexing/javascript/lazy-loading) identifies browser-level lazy loading for iframes as a valid method, provided relevant content becomes visible without Google having to click or swipe first.

Make the surrounding page understandable in its own right. Search engines and visitors need text about the location, audience, spaces, options and practical information. A bare title followed only by an iframe is a thin landing page, however impressive the tour looks.

## How should you assess the result?

A tour succeeds when it supports its chosen task, not when it simply contains many panoramic views or scan points.

## Combine a 3D tour with other media

A tour lets visitors explore independently. Photography gives you more control over composition and detail. Video determines pace, story and sound. Drone footage adds context around the building and its surroundings. Choose the right medium for each question.

A showroom campaign can lead with professional photography and then link to the tour. A property presentation combines exterior and interior photographs, a floor plan and an interactive walkthrough. A hospitality venue can place a promotional film and tour alongside practical reservation information.

## Conclusion: a tour is a managed digital space

A good 3D tour does not begin with the camera or end with the embed code. Purpose, route, capture, privacy, hosting, accessibility, management and website context together determine whether the tour remains useful. Make these decisions before capture and treat the tour as a digital asset that requires maintenance.
