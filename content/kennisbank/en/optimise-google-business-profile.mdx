# Optimise your Google Business Profile: practical checklist for SMEs

<LeadIntro>
A **Google Business Profile checklist** helps you verify that customers see the right information in Google Search and Maps, understand what you offer, and can call, request directions or visit your website without friction. Optimisation does not mean adding as many fields or keywords as possible. It means making every visible detail accurate, current, specific and demonstrably connected to your real business.
</LeadIntro>

This audit focuses exclusively on the business profile. To understand how it connects with your website, local landing pages, citations and regional content, begin with our pillar guide to [local SEO for SMEs in Limburg](/en/knowledge-base/seo-geo/local-seo-for-smes-in-limburg/). If your profile does not yet lead to a clear website, use the [complete guide to building a website](/en/knowledge-base/web-design/complete-guide-to-building-a-website-in-limburg/) to establish that foundation first.

Google explains local results in terms of **relevance, distance and prominence**. Complete information can help Google understand what you do, but you cannot optimise distance away, and nobody can buy or guarantee a better position. See Google's [official explanation of local ranking](https://support.google.com/business/answer/7091?hl=en). When in doubt, consult the central [overview of Google Business Profile policies](https://support.google.com/business/answer/13762416?hl=en), as the rules can differ between visible profile elements.

<NoticeBox variant="warning" title="Accuracy comes before optimisation">
Do not add place names, services or slogans to your business name unless they form part of the name by which the business is recognised in the real world. Google's [business representation guidelines](https://support.google.com/business/answer/3038177?hl=en) require the name, location, category and service area to represent the actual business accurately. An artificial name can lead to rejected edits or restrictions.
</NoticeBox>

## Audit the profile in four layers

A profile may look complete yet still perform poorly. Work in a fixed order. First, confirm that the business is eligible and identify who owns the profile. Next, correct the core details. Then add content and evidence. Only after those stages should you assess performance and maintenance.

## 1. Check eligibility, ownership and verification

A Business Profile is intended for a business that has in-person contact with customers during its stated hours, either at a customer-facing location or because employees travel to customers. A fully online business without face-to-face customer contact is not automatically eligible. Nor should you create an additional profile for every municipality you serve. In principle, Google allows a service business one profile for its central location, unless it has genuinely separate locations with their own staff and operations.

Next, check who is listed as owner and manager. Prefer company accounts, give employees only the role they need and maintain a recovery path that does not depend on one private email address. Remove outdated agencies or former employees only after the correct owner has secure access. Record the profile ID, verification status and responsible person in your internal website or marketing documentation.

Verification is not a one-off tick that automatically approves every future edit. Changing the name, category or location may trigger another review. Keep evidence of your genuine business name, location and operations, and avoid making a large set of speculative changes at once when the profile is already accurate.

## 2. Use your real business name and the right category

The naming rule is simple: use the name by which customers recognise your business on your website, communications, invoices and physical signage. Do not write `VisualVibe Web Design SEO Photography Limburg` when the actual name is `VisualVibe`. Services and regions have their own fields and do not belong in the name as hidden keywords.

The primary category describes what the business **is**, not everything it owns or offers. Choose the most specific available category that accurately covers your principal activity. Additional categories are useful for substantial secondary activities, but a long collection will not automatically make the profile more relevant. Google's [category guidance](https://support.google.com/business/answer/7249669?hl=en) confirms that categories affect local ranking and recommends one specific primary category with only a few suitable additional categories.

## 3. Configure your address and service area safely

Show a customer-facing location only when customers can genuinely visit during the stated hours and the premises are suitably equipped and identifiable. If you work from home and visit customers but do not receive them there, hide the address and use a service area. A virtual office or postal address does not become a valid customer-facing location.

Service areas tell customers where you deliver or work. Google lets you select cities, postcodes or other areas, not define your own radius. Under the [official service-area guidance](https://support.google.com/business/answer/9157481?hl=en), you can add up to twenty areas, and the combined area should generally remain within about two hours' drive of your base. Select only places where you genuinely accept work.

A service area does not replace strong website content. You still need a clear site structure for different services and regional questions. Our guide to [SEO for websites in Limburg](/en/knowledge-base/seo-geo/seo-for-websites-in-limburg/) explains how search intent, service pages, local evidence and conversion work together without creating a duplicate page for every place.

## 4. Make core information complete and consistent

Check the phone number, website, regular hours, special hours, opening date and available attributes. The phone number should connect to the business or specific location. The website link should lead to the most relevant page on your own site, not a social profile or generic intermediary page when a suitable landing page exists.

Update public holidays, annual leave, temporary closures and seasonal hours in advance. Incorrect hours are more than an SEO problem: they can leave someone standing outside a closed door and damage trust. Also check that the name, address or service area, phone number and hours agree with your website and other important citations. They need not be letter-for-letter identical, but they must not contradict one another.

If the website is slow, insecure or technically unavailable, the profile cannot fix it. Use the [technical SEO checklist](/en/knowledge-base/seo-geo/technical-seo-checklist/) to review crawling and indexing, and read [why a fast website can perform better](/en/knowledge-base/web-design/why-a-fast-website-can-rank-better/) without treating speed as a ranking guarantee.

## 5. Describe services for customers, not a keyword list

Add the real services customers can buy. Group them logically, use recognisable names and include a short description or indicative price where useful. Google's [services documentation](https://support.google.com/business/answer/9455399?hl=en) says that offered services can appear on the profile and may be highlighted for relevant local searches. That does not guarantee visibility for every term.

The business description should quickly explain whom you help, what you offer and what makes your approach recognisable. Avoid empty slogans, excessive capital letters, promotions and lists of municipalities. Your strongest content belongs on the website, where there is room for your offer, process, cases and conditions. The guide to [SEO content for service businesses](/en/knowledge-base/seo-geo/seo-content-for-service-businesses/) shows how a profile, service page, knowledge article and case study each serve a different purpose.

## 6. Use genuine photographs as verifiable evidence

Photographs help customers decide whether they have found the right business and understand what to expect. Where relevant, show the facade, entrance, interior, team, equipment, projects, products and services. For a service business without a customer-facing location, genuine images of the team, work and projects are usually more useful than generic office stock photography.

Google recommends sharp, well-lit images that reflect reality and do not use excessive filters or AI edits. Review the [official photo specifications and examples](https://support.google.com/business/answer/6123536?hl=en). Add only media for which you have the required usage rights and consent. Check regularly whether user photographs still belong to the right location, and report inappropriate material through the available process.

Professional [corporate photography](/en/services/photography/corporate-photography/) can align the visual presentation of your website and profile. Prepare a shot list so that the images do more than look attractive and answer genuine questions: what does the team look like, what work do you deliver, where do you receive customers and what result can someone reasonably expect?

## 7. Build an honest review process

After a genuine experience, ask customers neutrally for feedback. Use the profile's own review link or QR code. Do not send copy that asks only for five stars, and do not offer a discount, gift or competition entry in return for a review. Google's [review guidance](https://support.google.com/business/answer/3474122?hl=en) explicitly classifies incentives for posting, changing or removing reviews as deceptive content.

Respond to reviews when you can add something useful. Give specific thanks, answer a question or explain calmly how you are following up a problem. Do not share personal information or case details. A negative review does not have to end in a lengthy public argument. Acknowledge the experience, invite the customer to use a suitable private channel, and report the review only when the content genuinely breaches policy.

## 8. Measure actions, not only profile views

Depending on the business type and available features, Business Profile Performance may include search queries, unique views, direction requests, call clicks and website clicks. Google explains these definitions in its [profile performance documentation](https://support.google.com/business/answer/9918094?hl=en). Read the definitions before comparing periods: a view is not a lead, and clicking the call button does not prove that a conversation or sale took place.

Review website and business outcomes alongside profile data. Use a recognisable, accurate landing page and measure forms, telephone conversations or bookings in a privacy-conscious way. Ask new enquiries how they found you and connect only the data you genuinely need. Then compare quality: which path produces suitable enquiries, and which action ends without a next step?

A rise or fall rarely has one certain cause. Seasonality, demand, competition, advertising, opening hours, website changes and measurement definitions may all contribute. Record important profile changes with their dates and assess trends over a useful period. Do not treat a Maps search you perform on one device as an objective ranking measurement.

## 9. Establish a maintenance rhythm

An accurate audit starts ageing as soon as the offer, team, opening hours or location changes. Divide management into recurring intervals.

## How the profile fits within SEO, AEO and GEO

A Business Profile is one source of company information, not an isolated growth channel. The website should substantiate the same identity and services through content, case studies and contact paths. To understand how traditional SEO, answer-focused content and generative search experiences fit together, continue with [GEO and AEO for SMEs](/en/knowledge-base/seo-geo/how-to-appear-in-ai-search-results-geo-aeo/), [what AEO means](/en/knowledge-base/seo-geo/what-is-aeo/) and [what Generative Engine Optimisation means](/en/knowledge-base/seo-geo/what-is-generative-engine-optimisation/).

Google offers no separate AI-ranking guarantee for a complete profile. Accurate business information may nevertheless be useful in local and generative experiences when the system considers it relevant. Keep the same facts current on the profile and website without distorting copy specifically for machines.

## From a filled profile to a reliable local path

A good Google Business Profile is not a one-off form-filling exercise. It is a maintained representation of your real business. Start with access and policy, make the identity and offer complete, show credible evidence, and then assess which customer actions genuinely create value. The profile can then support your [local SEO approach](/en/services/seo/local-seo/) and website without making you dependent on tricks.
