# Corporate portraits: practical tips for stronger team photos

<LeadIntro>
A **corporate portrait** is a recognisable photograph that shows customers, candidates or partners who they will be dealing with. The best portrait is not automatically the most formal one. Start by deciding where the image will appear and which impression suits the person, their role and the organisation. Then align clothing, location, lighting, pose, framing and editing with that decision. For a team, the photographs should feel coherent without forcing everyone to play the same part.
</LeadIntro>

Small choices affect whether someone appears approachable, knowledgeable, energetic or distant. A wide environmental portrait can work beautifully on a team page but become unreadable in a small profile circle. A tightly framed headshot suits LinkedIn and press use, while an adviser may need more context on the company's own website.

This guide therefore covers purpose, appearance, clothing, location, lighting, posing, team consistency, selection and usage rights. For the wider image library, read [what corporate photography includes](/en/kennisbank/fotografie/what-is-corporate-photography/). If you are ready to commission a complete set, explore our [corporate portrait photography](/en/services/photography/business-portraits/).

<QuoteBlock>
A corporate portrait does not need to make someone look more important. It should make the right person feel credible and approachable.
</QuoteBlock>

## Tip 1: decide where the portrait needs to work

Do not begin with "formal or spontaneous". Begin with the places where the photograph will be used. A LinkedIn profile image appears small and is usually shown in a square or circle. A team page can accommodate a wider portrait that includes posture and surroundings. A speaker biography, press request or quotation may need a neutral photograph that can be cropped easily. One shoot can cover several uses when they are planned in advance.

List both the fixed and likely applications. Consider email signatures, author boxes, webinars, quotations, trade publications and internal platforms too. For each application, identify the required crop, background and resolution. This prevents an attractive landscape portrait from later being cut down to only part of the person's face.

## Tip 2: translate your brand values into visible behaviour

Brand words such as "professional", "personal" and "innovative" are too broad to direct a portrait. Turn them into visible choices. Personal might mean looking straight into the lens with an open posture. Expertise can come across through composure, a focused expression and a relevant working environment. Energy can be conveyed through a lighter stance and a little more movement, without requiring everyone to smile broadly.

Consider the person's role. A child therapist, industrial project manager and estate agent do not need to use the same visual language. A series within one organisation can still feel connected by keeping lighting, colour, distance and editing consistent.

## Tip 3: choose clothing that suits the role and image library

Clothing advice starts with what someone would normally wear to a professional meeting. A portrait feels more credible when the person recognises themselves. "Corporate" does not automatically mean a suit, tie or blazer. Workwear, a smart jumper, blouse, shirt or uniform may suit the everyday role much better.

Choose clothing that sits well in both standing and seated poses. Avoid very fine patterns if they become distracting on camera. Large lettering or logos draw attention unless the company logo is intentionally part of the image. Very bright colours can cast onto the skin or background. Coordinate colours with the brand identity and with one another when several people will appear together on a team page.

Do not turn clothing into an exam. Send employees a few clear examples in advance and leave room for questions. Anyone who feels uncomfortable in a prescribed outfit will often show it in their posture and expression. A backup option is more useful than a long list of prohibited colours.

## Tip 4: choose between a studio, workplace and environmental portrait

A neutral background creates calm and makes a series easy to repeat. This is useful for large teams, profiles and organisations that regularly welcome new employees. A workplace portrait provides more context and distinction, but requires control over lighting, background, privacy and activity. An environmental portrait reveals even more of the role, location or activity and works well on expertise and campaign pages.

You do not have to choose only one approach. Set up a compact portrait station for the consistent base series, then plan several wider portraits of key people in their working environment. As part of a wider [corporate photo shoot](/en/services/photography/corporate-photography/), these portraits can be produced alongside photographs of people at work.

<NoticeBox variant="tip" title="Test the background at its final size">
A bookcase or production hall can be meaningful at full-screen size yet turn into visual noise in a small profile card. During the test, review both the wider image and the planned crop.
</NoticeBox>

On location, look for lines running through the person's head, emergency signage, confidential documents, computer screens and accidental passers-by. Keep enough distance between the person and background when you want a calmer image. Choose somewhere participants do not have to pose in front of colleagues. Privacy and quiet help more than an impressive entrance hall.

## Tip 5: use lighting that supports skin, eyes and brand

Good portrait lighting shapes the face naturally, keeps the eyes visible and matches the wider image library. Soft light often feels approachable; harder light adds character and more contrast to the skin. Daylight changes with clouds and time of day, so a controlled setup may provide greater consistency for a large team. Avoid accidentally mixing window light, warm ceiling lights and cool screens.

Find out beforehand who wears glasses. Reflections can usually be resolved with a small adjustment to the light, face angle or position of the glasses. Do not routinely ask someone to remove their glasses when they are part of how that person is recognised every day. The goal remains a credible portrait of the individual.

## Tip 6: build pose and expression through small prompts

"Just act naturally" gives most people very little to work with. Good direction uses small prompts: shift your weight, relax your shoulders, give your hands a purpose, or look away briefly and then return. A stable seated or standing position with a slight turn often feels calmer than a stiff, front-facing pose. Comfort and recognisability matter more than a universal formula.

Talking, breathing out or moving briefly can loosen the expression. When selecting, do not automatically choose the broadest smile. An open gaze or calm expression may suit the role better.

## Tip 7: make a team look consistent without creating clones

With a team series, visitors should immediately understand that the portraits belong to the same organisation. Achieve this with a limited set of fixed variables: lighting, background family, camera height, distance, crop, colour treatment and placement on the page. Personality can vary through expression, subtle changes in posture, clothing and relevant context.

Keep a short setup sheet recording the background, light position, focal length, framing, reference file and editing direction. This allows new employees to be added later without recreating the entire series. Review periodically whether the style still suits the website and brand. Technically imitating an old portrait series is not always better than renewing the full image library at a sensible point.

Schedule larger teams in time slots and appoint an internal coordinator. They can monitor attendance and names while the photographer remains focused on the individual. Read the complete guide to [preparing a corporate photo shoot](/en/kennisbank/fotografie/how-to-prepare-a-corporate-photo-shoot/) when the portraits are part of a wider production day.

## Tip 8: leave enough room for different crops

A portrait may sit beside text on desktop, appear above the biography on mobile and become a compact LinkedIn profile image. Do not photograph only the final crop. A slightly wider master image provides room for square, vertical and landscape versions without cutting awkwardly through the chin, crown or shoulders.

LinkedIn's official [profile photo guidelines](https://www.linkedin.com/help/linkedin/answer/a1377087) require a profile photograph to represent the person's likeness and exclude items such as a standalone company logo, landscape or stock image. For a personal profile, use a recognisable photograph of the person. Test whether the face, hair and glasses remain clear in the small circular display, and keep important details away from the outer corners.

Deliver a clear set for each selected portrait, such as a high-resolution master, a web version and the crops agreed beforehand. It is better to manage one reliable source file than several independently edited copies whose origins nobody knows.

## Tip 9: select for recognisability and role, not perfection

Make an initial selection based on technical quality, expression, posture and cohesion. The person photographed can then help choose, but limit the options to strong variations. A hundred almost identical looks mainly create uncertainty.

Use criteria shared in advance:

- the person is recognisable and feels accurately represented;
- the expression suits their role and the brand;
- eyes, posture and clothing do not distract;
- the background remains calm in the final crop;
- the portrait fits the rest of the team;
- there is enough technical room for the agreed channels.

Agree how skin, clothing and backgrounds will be retouched. Correct temporary distractions, but do not alter permanent features or body shape until the person no longer recognises themselves.

## Tip 10: publish portraits accessibly and in context

On a website, a portrait should sit with the person's name, role and relevant information. The [W3C Images Tutorial](https://www.w3.org/WAI/tutorials/images/) stresses that text alternatives depend on an image's function. If a name and role appear directly beside a purely supporting portrait, repeating them in the alt text may be unnecessary. If the image adds meaning or functions as a link, its text alternative needs to communicate that function usefully. Have the specific template checked for accessibility.

Google's [image SEO guidelines](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/google-images) recommend relevant context, descriptive filenames, responsive images and useful alt text without keyword stuffing. Do not use alt text such as "best corporate portrait photographer Limburg professional LinkedIn photo". Describe the person and their role when the context requires it.

Optimise file size and dimensions for each component. Loading a large print master directly into every team card slows the page. Keep the master in the image library and publish responsive web versions. For a new web project, the [new website checklist](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/new-website-checklist/) helps you plan photography, crops, alt text and performance together.

## Tip 11: record consent and usage rights separately

A corporate portrait shows a recognisable person and is often distributed across several channels. In its explanation of the [principles of image rights](https://www.gegevensbeschermingsautoriteit.be/burger/thema-s/recht-op-afbeelding/principes), the Belgian Data Protection Authority states that consent to create an image and consent to publish or distribute it are separate matters. Inform participants about specific purposes and channels, and appoint someone internally to manage evidence, questions and changes. Ask a legal professional to assess exceptions or employment-law situations. This guide is not legal advice.

The photographer and client also need clear agreements. The [FPS Economy copyright FAQ](https://economie.fgov.be/sites/default/files/Files/Publications/files/FAQ-auteursrecht.pdf) provides general information about copyright-protected works and the use of photographs. Define in the assignment which images will be delivered, what licence applies, whether press and partners may receive files, how long their use is planned and which edits third parties may make.

Do not keep consent and licence information only in an old email inbox. Attach relevant restrictions to the image library. A future colleague can then see whether a portrait was permitted only on the team page or could also be used in advertising, press and external campaigns.

## Tip 12: plan how new employees will join the series

Teams change. Agree how new employees will be added and when the complete series should be renewed. A repeatable fixed setup works for growing teams, while a small team can combine portraits with a wider content day. Keep names, roles and publication status current, and replace outdated images across the website, presentations and templates. The photographer documents the setup, while the organisation manages roles, publication decisions and planning.

## A portrait series should be recognisable on two levels

A strong corporate portrait shows a person first and a photographic style second. The person should recognise themselves, while customers or candidates understand the intended appearance. A team series adds a second level: every face is individual, yet the lighting, framing and colour show that everyone belongs to the same organisation.

That balance comes from choosing the purpose and channels in advance, directing each person carefully during the shoot, and making a rigorous but human selection afterwards. Instead of a collection of passport photographs, you build a useful visual introduction for your website, LinkedIn, press and future communications.
