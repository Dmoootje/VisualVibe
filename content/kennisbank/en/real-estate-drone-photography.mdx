# Real estate drone photography: shot list, rules and preparation

<LeadIntro>
**Real estate drone photography is most useful when the location reveals information that cannot be seen from the ground.** A well-planned aerial set shows how the building, plot, access, outdoor space and relevant surroundings relate to one another. It complements ground photography, respects flight and privacy requirements, and avoids viewpoints that distort distances or plot boundaries.
</LeadIntro>

A drone can clearly place a home, commercial property or development in context. That does not mean every listing needs a series of high aerial overviews. For a city apartment, a controlled facade image and one contextual shot may be enough. For a detached home, business site or new development, the relationship between the building, garden, parking and access may be central to the story.

This guide covers image selection, shooting conditions, Belgian and European flight requirements, privacy, delivery and publication. The legal sections provide general orientation only. They are not legal advice or permission for a specific flight.

<QuoteBlock author="Team VisualVibe" role="Creative media agency">
A strong aerial property photograph does not sell height. It makes location, space and access understandable at a glance.
</QuoteBlock>

## When does an aerial image genuinely add value to a property?

An aerial photograph has a clear purpose when a prospective buyer would otherwise struggle to see where the building starts, how the plot is arranged, or how access, parking and outdoor areas connect. It can show how an office sits within a business park, how a new residential block relates to its neighbourhood, or where holiday accommodation lies in relation to the landscape and amenities.

The image is less useful when it shows little more than a generic roof. For every planned shot, ask which buyer, tenant, investor or client question it answers. Could a ground photograph explain the same point more clearly? Will the relevant detail still be visible at the size used on the final page?

## Match the shoot to the property type and decision

A family-home buyer assesses different issues from a business renting a warehouse. Before the flight, record who will view the images, which decision they support and which characteristics must be verifiable on screen. Avoid a vague instruction such as "take a few spectacular drone photographs".

A commercial building may need both a property presentation and employer or brand communication. Plan these objectives separately in the same brief. The first set should orient viewers; the second may use more atmosphere and pace. For a development documented across several phases, combine the plan with [drone video for construction projects](/en/knowledge-base/drone/drone-video-for-construction-projects/).

## Drone photography, ground photography, a floor plan or a 3D tour?

These formats do not replace one another. Aerial images mainly explain external relationships. [Real estate photography](/en/services/photography/real-estate-photography/) presents interiors, finishes and human scale. A floor plan shows layout more abstractly but precisely. A [real estate 3D tour](/en/services/3d-vr-ar/real-estate-3d-tour/) lets visitors move through accessible spaces themselves. Read [what is a 3D tour?](/en/knowledge-base/3d-vr/what-is-a-3d-tour/) for its role and limitations.

Use a combination that answers questions in a logical order. Open with the strongest building image, then show facade and access, move through the interior and outdoor space, and add a higher contextual view where location becomes relevant. Constantly switching between very high aerial shots and small interior details makes a set feel disjointed.

Our [property photography tips](/en/knowledge-base/photography/property-photography-tips/) cover styling, interior shot lists and credible perspective. The guide to [drone imagery for businesses](/en/knowledge-base/drone/drone-imagery-for-businesses/) helps when you are still choosing between photography, conventional drone video and FPV.

## Build a complete but compact shot list

A shot list prevents the pilot from returning with variations of one overview. Divide it into essentials, useful additions and images possible only if conditions and flight requirements allow. For each image, state what it must explain and which crop is needed.

  "A recognisable oblique hero image of the property and immediate outdoor space",
  "Front facade, entrance and relationship with the road or driveway",
  "Rear with garden, terrace, courtyard or functional outdoor area",
  "Parking, loading area or outbuilding when it affects the decision",
  "A wider contextual image showing only relevant surroundings",
  "Vertical or square alternatives for social media and property platforms",
  "A wide composition with a safe text area for a website or campaign",
  "A lower alternative when greater height adds little",
]} />

Plot boundaries are not automatically visible from the air. If they are added during post-production, the client should provide the correct source data and accept responsibility for checking the overlay. A graphic line is an information layer, not a legal boundary established by the drone.

## Prepare the property, grounds and surroundings before the shoot

Small items at ground level can become strong patterns in an overview. Open bins, loose construction materials, badly parked vehicles, a dirty courtyard or bright temporary signage can pull attention away from the building. Assign one person to perform the final location check.

Confirm whether gates need to be open, where staff and visitors should park, and which areas should be empty or shown in representative use. Inform residents, tenants or site users when production affects their plans. The pilot manages the flight; the client still needs to arrange access, property agreements and the factual condition of the site.

<NoticeBox variant="tip" title="Draw a simple site sketch">
Mark the property, desired camera directions, take-off and landing point, public road, neighbours, trees, cables and areas that do not need to be recorded. The sketch supports the brief, but never replaces the operator's current operational site assessment.
</NoticeBox>

## Plan light, season and weather around the building

Light determines whether facades remain legible and outdoor areas look appealing without becoming misleading. A front facade in deep shade may need a different time from the rear garden. Use the sun's position to prioritise. Do not expect every side to receive ideal light in one short slot.

The season changes what an image communicates. Leaves can protect privacy but obscure facades. A wet winter garden tells a different story from a summer terrace. For a sale at a particular moment, current information often matters more than waiting for a theoretically perfect season. Do not use an old green summer view if the site has materially changed.

Wind, rain, mist and low cloud affect both safety and image quality. Agree on a weather decision point and an alternative window. The operator decides whether the specific flight can be conducted responsibly. Commercial pressure does not change that assessment.

## Keep perspective and editing honest

A wide-angle lens and high viewpoint can alter how distances feel. Selective framing may hide a busy road, neighbouring building or industrial setting. Not every element belongs in every photograph, but the full presentation must not create a materially false expectation.

Horizon, colour, contrast and lens correction can improve legibility. Removing a temporary sensor spot differs from erasing a permanent pylon, adjacent building or defect. Agree which retouching is permitted, and clearly identify a commercial visualisation when it shows a future rather than current condition.

## Check European and Belgian flight requirements first

Commercial property work has no blanket exemption. EU rules classify operations by risk. Many lower-risk flights may fit the Open category, but only when the aircraft, subcategory, distance from people, route and all other conditions are appropriate. EASA explains A1, A2 and A3, with the corresponding aircraft and training requirements, in its current [Open category overview](https://www.easa.europa.eu/en/domains/drones-air-mobility/operating-drone/open-category-low-risk-civil-drones).

According to [FPS Mobility](https://mobilit.belgium.be/nl/luchtvaart/vliegen-met/drones-uas/categorieen/categorie-open), Belgian Open category basics include a maximum of 120 metres above ground level, direct visual line of sight, no flight over an assembly of people, no dropping of materials and compliance with geozones. Separation from uninvolved people depends partly on the subcategory and aircraft class.

A camera on a non-toy drone generally makes operator registration relevant, including below 250 grams. The [Belgian overview table](https://mobilit.belgium.be/nl/luchtvaart/vliegen-met/drones-uas/categorieen/categorie-open/overzichtstabel) summarises registration, aircraft class, Remote ID, geo-awareness and pilot requirements. Ask the operator to demonstrate that the planned approach fits the actual flight. Clients should not infer permission from this summary alone.

Permission from an owner or estate agent does not by itself permit use of the airspace. The current geozone, operation category, aircraft conditions, people involved and any additional authorisations determine whether and how a flight may take place. This article is not legal or operational advice.
</NoticeBox>

## Check Droneguide and location-specific restrictions

A property may lie near an airport, heliport, prison, industrial area, nature reserve or another geographical UAS zone. Check the current map in [Droneguide](https://map.droneguide.be/) for every flight. FPS Mobility warns that additional requirements are cumulative where geozones overlap.

Run this check early, before the photographer, agent and owner are waiting on site. Some situations require advance applications, coordination with a geozone manager or another operational approach. A green or apparently unrestricted map area does not remove the need to comply with other rules or assess weather and the site.

There is usually another way to communicate the property: a lower permitted viewpoint, photography from a nearby building with permission, ground photography or a visualisation based on correct project data. Include the alternative in the plan so a rejected or irresponsible flight does not block the entire shoot.

## Protect privacy, image rights and sensitive information

Aerial footage may capture people, number plates, gardens, windows, terraces and activities beyond the commission. Permission to fly is not permission to retain or publish everything recorded. The [EASA Privacy Handbook](https://www.easa.europa.eu/en/domains/civil-drones/privacy/privacy-handbook) advises professional operators to limit privacy risks during planning.

Choose routes and angles that record no more than needed. Restrict access to raw selections, remove failed or unnecessary sensitive images, and check finished photographs for identifiable people, number plates and views into private spaces. Agree who is responsible for purpose, legal basis, information and the publication decision.

The Belgian Data Protection Authority distinguishes between taking and using an image in its guidance on [image rights](https://gegevensbeschermingsautoriteit.be/burger/thema-s/recht-op-afbeelding/principes). Context and exceptions matter, so assuming everyone in public may freely be published is unsafe. Seek specialist legal advice when circumstances are sensitive or uncertain.

## Request delivery formats you can actually use

A high-resolution master helps with print and future crops but is too heavy for direct web use. Request optimised variants with suitable dimensions, compression and colour profile. Discuss landscape, square and vertical compositions before the flight. A vertical crop made afterwards may lose the property or its context.

Consider a wide website image with quiet space for text where the design needs it. Record how many finished images are included, who selects them, which correction round is allowed, and whether plot overlays or labels require separate exports.

Define the usage licence for property platforms, your website, organic social media, paid advertising, print, press and sharing with the owner, architect or project partners. Receiving files does not automatically allow every third party to reuse them without limits.

## Publish aerial photographs accessibly and in context

Place an aerial image near copy that explains what it shows. Useful alt text names the relevant information, such as "Office building with parking at the front and access on the right." A list of keywords does not help a screen-reader user.

The [W3C alt-text decision tree](https://www.w3.org/WAI/tutorials/images/decision-tree/) helps determine whether an image is informative, functional or decorative. If the photograph links to a project, its text alternative should explain that function. If a decorative crop adds no information, empty alt text may be better than repeated marketing copy.

For graphically marked plot boundaries or facilities, repeat the relevant information in page text. Do not rely only on colour or small labels in an image. This keeps the content understandable on mobile and for visitors who cannot perceive the image.

## What determines planning and price?

The price of real estate drone photography is not based only on minutes in the air. Preparation, travel, geozone checks, location complexity, applications, waiting time, number of viewpoints, editing, exports and licensing all matter. A simple home outside complex zones differs from an active business site with several buildings and stakeholders.

Combining aerial and ground photography can be efficient if light, access and operational planning suit the same day. It is not automatically cheaper when each part needs a different time or crew. Ask for a quotation that identifies deliverables and conditions instead of merely offering "one hour of drone".

Weather dependency should also be explicit: when is the decision made, what happens after postponement, and which costs remain? A clear answer prevents safety being weighed against an uncertain last-minute invoice.

## Common mistakes in property drone photography

The most common creative mistake is flying too high. The building becomes small and the photograph consists mostly of roofs and surroundings. A lower oblique viewpoint often provides more recognition, depth and connection with the grounds. Height is a tool, not a measure of quality.

Another mistake is discussing the drone only after the ground shoot has been planned. Vertical variants may be missed, the parking area may be full, or a required check may no longer be possible in time. Include aerial imagery from the first brief, even if the final decision is not to fly.

Finally, publication is sometimes separated from selection. The most attractive image may be privacy-sensitive, out of date or unclear. Ask both the photographer and the owner or file manager to verify the factual presentation.

## Turn a high viewpoint into better property information

Good real estate drone photography starts with a question, not with take-off. Define the purpose, property type and publication channels first. Then build a compact shot list, prepare the grounds and people involved, and let the operator assess current flight conditions and alternatives.

Select for clarity, honest perspective, privacy and technical usability. Aerial images then become more than a visual effect at the top of a listing. They become a credible part of the property's complete story.
