# What is corporate photography and when does your SME need it?

<LeadIntro>
**Corporate photography** is professional photography that shows who your organisation is, what it does and how it works. It covers far more than a group portrait or an attractive building. A useful corporate shoot delivers purposeful images of people, processes, products, locations and completed work, tailored to your website, sales, recruitment and communications.
</LeadIntro>

Purpose is the main difference between corporate photography and an isolated photo shoot. The photographer does more than create attractive pictures. They build an image library that answers different visitor questions. Who carries out the work? What does the process look like? Does the appearance match the promised quality? Is this a business I would work with or apply to join?

For an SME in Limburg, Belgium, such an image library can provide value for several years, but only if it is prepared and managed well. The photographs need to be accurate, available in the right aspect ratios and delivered with clear agreements on publication and use. This guide helps you decide which images you genuinely need and how to turn a brief and selection into one coherent result.

<QuoteBlock>
A strong corporate photograph does not fill an empty box in a design. It gives a customer information that text alone struggles to prove.
</QuoteBlock>

## What does corporate photography include?

Corporate photography is an umbrella term. The right interpretation depends on the organisation and its communication goal. A contractor needs completed projects, tradespeople and construction details. A consultancy shows people, collaboration and expertise. A hospitality business uses atmosphere, food, its team and location. A manufacturer needs to make scale, processes and quality control visible.

## When are original corporate photographs better than stock images?

Stock photography can be useful for an abstract theme, a temporary illustration or a subject you cannot possibly photograph yourself. It becomes less convincing when visitors want evidence of your people, location, product or finish. A generic meeting table does not show how your team collaborates. A random construction photograph does not prove that your business completed the project.

Original images provide specific information. They show a real machine, a recognisable material, the scale of a space or the person who will sit across the table. That can support trust, but photography alone does not guarantee sales. Your copy, service, reputation and contact experience must fulfil the same promise.

## Start with intended uses and channels

A strong brief says more than that you want professional photographs. It describes where the images will appear, which message they support and how long you expect to use them. A desktop hero calls for a different composition from a vertical recruitment advert. A team portrait may need to sit beside copy, while a case-study image needs to show technical detail.

## Turn the brief into a useful shot list

A shot list is not a creative prison. It prevents essential images from being overlooked when a location, employee or process may not be available later. The list describes subjects and uses, not necessarily every precise pose.

A contractor might need a wide view of the completed project, details of the finish, a tradesperson performing a recognisable task, material choices, a conversation with the customer and an on-site portrait. A software company is more likely to need portraits, collaboration, screens in context, a customer conversation and its office environment.

Give every priority image a purpose, location, participants, desired crops and practical conditions. Add a reserve list for extra moments when time and light allow.

## Photograph people without making the result feel forced

Many employees dislike standing in front of a camera. That need not be a problem. Good direction gives people a specific action, a clear position and small prompts. A conversation, demonstration or genuine task often feels more natural than looking into the lens for an extended period.

Allow enough time and explain beforehand how the photographs will be used. Begin with simple situations and build from there. Respect people's workload: an employee monitoring a production process cannot pose indefinitely. Portraits benefit from a calm set, a consistent camera height and attention to clothing, reflections in glasses and the background.

[Corporate portraits](/en/services/photography/corporate-portraits/) require a different approach from reportage images. A portrait should be recognisable and suit the person's role. A reportage image can show movement and surroundings. By combining the two thoughtfully in one day, the entire library can still feel like one visual family.

## Location, timing and styling matter more than the camera

A tidy location is not the same as a sterile one. Keep relevant materials and details in place, but remove distractions, confidential documents, visible waste and objects that do not belong in the story. Check third-party brand names, number plates, information on screens and safety requirements too.

Light changes throughout the day. A showroom with large windows may look different in the morning and afternoon. Production environments have fixed light sources and sometimes flicker. A construction site depends on the schedule, weather and access. A site visit or focused location photographs help the photographer plan equipment and timings more realistically.

Clothing does not have to be uniform unless that is part of the business. Do agree on suitable colours, patterns, workwear and personal protective equipment. Bring spare clothing when portraits and working images are combined.

<NoticeBox variant="tip" title="Plan around what cannot be repeated">
Prioritise people, processes, products or locations that are available only briefly. An empty office or detail shot can often be captured later. A complete team or unique stage of a project cannot.
</NoticeBox>

## Image rights, privacy and copyright

Recognisable people require agreements about both recording and publication. The Belgian Data Protection Authority explains that [image rights](https://www.gegevensbeschermingsautoriteit.be/index.php/burger/thema-s/recht-op-afbeelding/principes) are distinct from the protection of personal data. According to its guidance, agreeing to be photographed or filmed does not automatically mean agreeing to every form of publication or distribution.

Clarify in advance:

- who will be recognisable;
- the channels and purposes for which the material will be used;
- the intended period of use;
- whether paid advertising, press or third-party use is included;
- how an employee or external participant can ask questions;
- who manages consent and any withdrawals.

The photograph itself may also be protected by copyright. Ownership of a file, copyright and a usage licence are not automatically the same thing. The [FPS Economy copyright FAQ](https://economie.fgov.be/sites/default/files/Files/Publications/files/FAQ_Auteursrecht.pdf) provides general information about protected works and lawful use.

Record in the quotation or agreement which images will be delivered, who selects them, which edits are included, where and for how long publication is permitted, and whether transfer to partners is possible. Ask a legal adviser to assess your particular circumstances when employees, models, minors, sensitive locations or broad campaigns are involved. This article is not legal advice.

## Selection is an editorial decision

Delivering more photographs is not automatically better. An image library containing hundreds of almost identical frames makes choosing difficult. A strong selection varies subject, distance, orientation, people and detail without losing visual coherence.

Select for content first. Does the photograph show what the page needs to prove? Then assess technique, expression, background and cropping options. Do not choose only the most spectacular images. A calm photograph with clear space may be more useful for a website hero than a busier, more active frame.

## Use corporate photographs on your website

Place photographs close to the text they support. A machine belongs beside the production explanation, a team photograph beside the people and a case-study image beside the project. Give important images descriptive filenames and suitable alt text. Decorative images can have empty alt text so assistive technology can ignore them.

Google's [image SEO guidance](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/google-images) recommends standard HTML image elements, responsive versions, relevant context and useful alt text. Google also warns against keyword stuffing in alt attributes. Alt text is primarily a text alternative, not a place to list search terms.

[WCAG 2.2](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/) requires text alternatives for meaningful non-text content. Describe what someone needs to understand from the photograph in context. "Contractor checks the pointing on a brick facade" is more informative than "corporate photography Limburg best photographer".

Optimise file size without needlessly sacrificing visible quality. Use responsive dimensions and modern formats where appropriate. Loading large original files directly into a mobile card slows the page. Our knowledge base explains in more detail [why a fast website can rank better](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/why-a-fast-website-can-rank-better/).

## When should you add video?

Photography is quick to scan and easy to use across several designs. Video can communicate movement, voice, sequence and atmosphere more effectively. A production process, customer testimonial or tour sometimes needs time and sound to become understandable.

You do not need to choose as if one medium replaces the other. A combined production day can deliver portraits, reportage images, short clips and an interview, provided there is enough time and a clear order of priority. Photography and video do require different direction, production flows and post-production.

Read [why invest in a corporate video](/en/kennisbank/videografie/why-invest-in-a-corporate-video/) if you are unsure which story benefits from movement. For production, you can combine corporate photography with [corporate video](/en/services/videography/corporate-video/) and decide in advance which moments are reserved for each medium.

## What determines the scope of corporate photography?

Price and lead time depend on more than the hours during which the camera is visibly in use. Preparation, travel, location research, assistance, lighting setup, selection, editing, formats, licensing and archiving may all form part of the scope.

## Corporate photography makes your work visible

A successful corporate photo shoot begins not with equipment, but with the information customers, candidates and partners need. First decide which stories you want to substantiate. Then plan the people and locations, and finally record the selection, formats and use. The result is not a folder of disconnected photographs, but an image library that supports your communications as a whole.
