<LeadIntro>
A fast website can perform better in Google because **Core Web Vitals form part of its page experience signals**. Speed is not an automatic ranking guarantee, however. Google still prioritises relevant, useful content. For an SME, the real benefit is broader: a fast website displays information sooner, responds more quickly to clicks, shifts less and makes it easier to call, order or request a quotation.
</LeadIntro>

A PageSpeed score of 100 is therefore not a business goal in itself. Visitors do not care how high your test score is. They want to understand your offer immediately on a smartphone, view a relevant case study and contact you without frustration. Search engines try to measure aspects of that experience while also assessing each page's content, relevance, reliability and technical accessibility.

In this article you will learn which speed measurements count today, why lab data and real user data can differ from each other and which optimizations usually have the greatest impact. If you want to immediately add speed to the entire structure of a company website, start with our [complete guide to having a website created in Limburg](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/complete-guide-to-building-a-website-in-limburg/).

## Does a fast website automatically rank higher in Google?

No. According to the current documentation from [Google Search Central about page experience](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/page-experience), the ranking systems use Core Web Vitals, but good metrics do not guarantee a top position. There is also no single page experience signal that in itself determines the ranking. Google tries to show the most relevant and helpful information for every search query.

Speed can make a difference when several pages offer similarly relevant content. Technical quality also helps search engines load a page reliably and allows visitors to interact with the content. That makes speed important, but never in isolation from:

- an answer that fits the search question;
- clear services, cases and contact details;
- logical internal links and an understandable site structure;
- mobile readable content and accessible controls;
- safe technology, proper maintenance and up-to-date information.

<NoticeBox variant="warning" title="Optimise for visitors, not for a green score">
A perfect Lighthouse score with thin content remains a weak page. A substantively strong page that is unnecessarily slow or unstable misses opportunities. The right approach improves relevance and user experience together.
</NoticeBox>

## What are Core Web Vitals?

Core Web Vitals are three field measurements that summarize important parts of the real user experience. [web.dev](https://web.dev/articles/vitals) currently lists LCP, INP and CLS as the stable core metrics. A page reaches the 'good' category for a metric when at least 75 percent of the measured visits meet the good threshold. Google views mobile and desktop separately.

### LCP: when is the main content displayed?

Largest Contentful Paint measures when the largest visible text, image, or video element is displayed in the first viewport. This is often a hero image, a large title or a striking content block. [The web.dev guide to LCP](https://web.dev/articles/lcp) also takes into account server delay, connection establishment and redirects. Simply compressing an image does not always solve a slow LCP.

### INP: how responsive is the page?

Interaction to Next Paint assesses the response time of interactions during a visit. Think of opening a menu, choosing a product filter or sending a form. Heavy JavaScript tasks can keep the main thread busy, causing a click to be registered but the visible response to be delayed. The [web.dev guide to improving INP](https://web.dev/articles/optimize-inp) therefore recommends less work per interaction and breaking up long tasks.

### CLS: does the page remain visually stable?

Cumulative Layout Shift measures unexpected shifts. A button that slides away because an image takes up space later is not only annoying but can also lead to a wrong click. Common causes include media without fixed dimensions, late inserted banners, embeds and fonts that cause different text dimensions after loading. The [CLS documentation](https://web.dev/articles/optimize-cls) advises, among other things, to reserve space in advance with width, height or a fixed aspect ratio.

## Field data and lab data are not the same

PageSpeed Insights often tells two different stories. The top section uses Chrome UX Report data, also called CrUX, from real Chrome users over a rolling 28-day period. Lighthouse performs the diagnostics part in a controlled test environment. The official [Chrome explanation of PageSpeed Insights](https://developer.chrome.com/docs/crux/guides/pagespeed-insights) confirms that both types of data appear together in the tool.

A difference between the two results is therefore not automatically an error. Lab data can be green while real mobile users still experience problems due to slower devices, weak connections or interactions after the first screen. Conversely, a recent improvement may already be visible in Lighthouse while CrUX still contains visits from before the change. The explanation of [differences between lab and field data](https://web.dev/articles/lab-and-field-data-differences) describes Total Blocking Time as a useful lab correlation for INP. TBT is not a replacement for INP, because a standard loading audit does not measure a full real visit with all interactions.

For small websites without sufficient CrUX data, lab testing remains valuable. Then test multiple key pages, not just the homepage, and repeat on a real mobile device. A contact page, online shop category and image-rich case study page have different bottlenecks.

## Why mobile usually takes priority

A fast desktop in the office, fiber optic and a warm browser cache give a distorted comfortable image. Your customer can open your website on an older device, via mobile data and with multiple apps active. There, large images, complex animations and a lot of JavaScript take noticeably more time and energy.

Mobile quality also goes beyond speed. Text must be readable without zooming, buttons must be sufficiently large and spaced and the most important answer must not disappear behind a heavy intro or distracting pop-up. A visually impressive website only remains professional if it can also be used under less ideal circumstances.

## The biggest causes of a slow company website

Not every problem requires a complete rebuild. The biggest gain often lies in a few recurring choices around media and front-end technology.

Professional photography does not have to make a website slow. The source file can remain large for printing and archiving, while the website automatically serves suitable variants. With video, a light poster image with a deliberate play action is often smarter than multiple auto-starting embeds. This way, the visual identity remains strong without every visitor immediately downloading all the media bytes.

JavaScript deserves the same discipline. A slider, cookie platform, booking tool and analytics script may each seem reasonable individually, but together block the main thread. [web.dev about external JavaScript](https://web.dev/articles/optimizing-content-efficiency-loading-third-party-javascript) recommends removing scripts without clear value and keeping non-critical scripts off the critical path with appropriate loading strategies.

## Fonts, server response, caching and CDN

Web fonts affect both loading and stability. Limit the number of families, weights and character sets, use a modern format such as WOFF2 and choose an appropriate fallback. According to the [web.dev font best practices](https://web.dev/articles/font-best-practices), late loaded fonts can slow down text rendering and cause CLS. Therefore, only preload really critical fonts: too many preloads compete with other important files.

The browser can only render quickly when the server responds in a timely manner. Slow database queries, uncached pages, heavy plugins or cheap hosting with little capacity can increase Time to First Byte and thus degrade LCP. A CDN brings cacheable files closer to visitors, but doesn't compensate for inefficient applications or bad caching policies.

[MDN on HTTP caching](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/HTTP/Guides/Caching) explains that reusable responses need to be resubmitted from the origin server less often. Static files with a version hash can be cached for a long time; HTML and personalized answers require a different policy. Therefore, have caching configured based on the type of content, not with one general setting for everything.

## Where speed, accessibility and conversion meet

Core Web Vitals are not a complete accessibility or conversion measure. A page can be technically fast and still fail due to low contrast, an unclear form or controls that only work with a mouse. Yet good choices often overlap. A stable layout prevents wrong clicks, fewer unnecessary scripts makes interactions more responsive, and clear HTML helps both assistive technology and search engines.

This is business relevant for an SME. Every additional hurdle between search result and request requires patience from a potential customer. A faster first screen helps someone assess more quickly whether your offer is suitable. A responsive menu shortens the route to contact. A stable form gives more confidence when filling it out. These are logical benefits, not a promise that a certain millisecond will automatically deliver a fixed conversion rate.

A speed trajectory must therefore always protect the most important user flow: from landing page to service or product, from proof to choice and from choice to contact or purchase. With a [webshop for an SME](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/online-shop-development-for-smes/) you also test search, filters, shopping cart and checkout. For a compact site, the comparison between a [one-pager and a full website](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/one-page-or-full-website/) helps to avoid unnecessary complexity even before construction.

## Common mistakes in speed optimization

<DoDontGrid
doTitle="Do give priority"
dontTitle="Don't run blindly"
  dos={[
"Link real field problems to a page, element and user flow.",
"Load the LCP image early and delay media below the fold.",
"Remove unused scripts before fine-tuning small files.",
"Retest on mobile after every meaningful change.",
"Treat performance as an integral part of web design and maintenance.",
  ]}
  donts={[
"Lazy-load all images, including the main image at the top.",
"Installing a plugin without checking what code and network requests are included.",
"Only testing the homepage on one fast laptop.",
"Removing essential functionality to score 100 at all costs.",
"Presenting a green report as a guarantee of higher rankings or more revenue.",
  ]}
/>

Another mistake is publishing multiple changes at once without a baseline. You then don't know which intervention helped or caused an unexpected problem. First make a short hypothesis, adjust one coherent group, check functionality and compare again. For limited time, the improvement that affects the most real visitors and important pages takes priority over cosmetic audit notifications.

## Conclusion: speed is a quality requirement, not a trick

A fast website can contribute to better visibility, but mainly because it is part of a stronger user experience. The best approach combines good Core Web Vitals with relevant content, mobile web design, accessible interactions and a clear route to action. Measure what real visitors experience, use lab tools to find causes and first improve what makes a noticeable difference.
