# Product photography for online shops: from shot list to product feed

<LeadIntro>
Good **product photography for online shops** answers purchase questions consistently across a whole catalogue. Start by defining what customers need to inspect for each product family, then translate those needs into packshots, alternate views, details, scale and context. A repeatable specification, SKU-based workflow and careful publication process matter as much as the individual photographs.
</LeadIntro>

Customers cannot pick up an online product. Images must take over part of that inspection without pretending to replace dimensions, specifications or product data. The aim is not simply a beautiful gallery, but a dependable visual system that can absorb new products and variants.

This guide covers shot planning, colour and materials, batch production, rights, file management, accessibility and online shop performance. VisualVibe can connect [professional product photography](/en/services/photography/product-photography/) with [online shop development](/en/services/web-design/online-shop-development/) so the shoot reflects the actual templates and sales channels.

<QuoteBlock>
A complete product gallery does not contain as many pictures as possible. It shows the views a customer needs to identify, compare and understand the exact item.
</QuoteBlock>

## Begin with the questions a buyer cannot ask

In a physical shop, customers can turn a product around, feel its material and compare dimensions. Online, imagery and copy have to replace that inspection. For every product type, list the uncertainties that need resolving before purchase.

For a bag, these might concern internal pockets, closure, carrying options, material texture and scale. A machine component calls for connector, profile, fixing points and compatibility. Food products need packaging, quantity, preparation and realistic serving context. One generic packshot rarely answers all these questions.

Record dimensions, package contents and compatibility as text and product data too. A photograph conveys a sense of scale but is not a precise measuring tool. Perspective, crop and screen size all influence perceived proportions.

## Packshots and contextual images have different jobs

A packshot is a controlled, isolated presentation of the item. The background, angle and lighting remain calm so people can compare products easily. A contextual image shows use, mood or audience. It can add emotion and application, but also introduces more distractions.

Most online shops need both. Use the packshot as the visual anchor on category and product pages, then add context where it contributes new information. A coffee mug on a laid table shows use, while a close-up of the glaze shows finish. Five nearly identical lifestyle pictures make a gallery longer, not necessarily clearer.

Google Merchant Center has specific main-image rules. Its current [image_link guidance](https://support.google.com/merchants/answer/6324350?hl=en) requires the actual product to be clearly and fully visible and prohibits promotional overlays, placeholders and borders in the main image. It also recommends using the correct image for distinctive variants. Recheck platform rules when preparing Shopping feeds because they can change.

<NoticeBox variant="info" title="Design one production, deliver several applications">
Keep a clean master and derive channel versions from it. That prevents a price badge, watermark or text overlay from being permanently baked into the only available product image.
</NoticeBox>

## Create a repeatable image specification

Consistency does not mean making every product the same number of pixels high. It means applying the same visual rules to comparable items. An earring and a vase need different scales, while every vase in one series can share camera height, orientation, background tone, shadow and clear space.

Document those rules before photographing the first full batch. Create tests for an easy, average and difficult product. Review the results in the real product card, mobile view and gallery. Only then apply the specification to hundreds of SKUs.

## Handle colour, material and scale accurately

Colour is a chain involving the product, light source, camera settings, monitor, retouching, export and the customer's screen. Work with controlled lighting, fixed white balance and a colour reference. Use a calibrated display for selection and correction, and retain a reference product for recurring batches where practical.

No photographer can guarantee identical colour on every screen. Displays vary in brightness, profile and surrounding light. Record colour names and relevant specifications in product data too. For subtle shades, a concise screen-display note may be useful, but it does not excuse poor colour correction.

Different materials need different treatment. Glossy metal reflects the studio. Clear glass needs edge definition and contrast. Dark textile texture disappears if shadows are too deep. A test phase determines which setup shows form and surface without changing the product's appearance.

## Organise the shoot around SKUs and variants

A production day runs more efficiently when the catalogue is correct before products arrive. Export SKU, product name, family, variant, status and required images. Mark missing or unapproved samples. Give physical products the same identifier as the list so files do not have to be guessed afterwards.

Group products by setup, not only alphabetically. Photograph similar matte bottles together, then change lighting or background for reflective packaging. Keeping the base setup within a family limits unwanted variation and simplifies review.

For large ranges, create an exception route. Set aside and record a damaged sample, missing component or unknown variant. Otherwise the studio becomes a decision room and every uncertainty delays the rest of the batch.

## Naming and selection are part of production

Do not use a folder full of files such as `DSC_1042-final-reallyfinal.jpg` as a product database. A simple `SKU_image-type_sequence` pattern makes files easier to connect and retrieve. Examples include `VV-2048_front_01`, `VV-2048_closure-detail_02` and `VV-2048_context_03`. Preserve the exact SKU from the source system.

Track statuses such as awaiting photography, captured, selected, retouched, approved and published. The photographer can assess technique and style, but a product owner must verify variant, packaging and contents. Keep masters apart from derived web exports so future crops start from the best source rather than a previously compressed file.

## Agree usage rights, models and props in advance

Paying for a file does not automatically make every form of reuse transferable. Agree in writing which owned channels, countries, periods and campaigns are covered. State whether marketplaces, resellers, press, agencies and print are included and who may make further edits.

The [FPS Economy copyright FAQ](https://economie.fgov.be/sites/default/files/Files/Publications/files/FAQ-auteursrecht.pdf) provides general information about visual works. Obtain legal advice for broad or international distribution. This article is not legal advice.

If recognisable models, employees or customers appear, define capture and publication purposes. The Belgian Data Protection Authority explains in its [image-rights principles](https://gegevensbeschermingsautoriteit.be/burger/thema-s/recht-op-afbeelding/principes) that consent to take an image does not automatically cover every publication. Also ensure borrowed props and visible brands do not create a false impression about what is included.

## Publish product images quickly and accessibly

Keep high-resolution source files, but do not load the largest master in every product card. Generate responsive versions so the browser can choose an appropriate size. Balance enough quality for zoom and detail with suitable dimensions, compression and file size for each placement.

Google's [image SEO guidance](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/google-images) recommends standard HTML image elements, responsive images, relevant surrounding copy, descriptive filenames and useful alt text. It offers no visibility guarantee. The page must remain technically accessible and the image must genuinely belong to that product.

Alt text describes an image's meaning or function in context. For a main image, product type, colour and a distinguishing visible feature may be useful. Do not repeat the same keyword list for every gallery position. A detail image can name the specific part shown, while a redundant thumbnail may be better with empty alt text. The W3C [alt decision tree](https://www.w3.org/WAI/tutorials/images/decision-tree/) helps distinguish informative, functional, redundant and decorative images.

Structured data does not replace visible information. Google's guide to [Product structured data](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/structured-data/product) explains eligibility for richer search displays. Price, availability, variant and image must describe the same product in markup, feeds and visible content.

## Measure image quality as a catalogue process

Start with operational completeness. What percentage of active SKUs has an approved main image? How many have the specified detail and context images? Which feed warnings concern images, variants or crawlability? How many products still use a placeholder or outdated packaging?

Then examine behaviour such as gallery and zoom interaction, product-page exits, service questions and return reasons that might relate to expectations or scale. Treat these as signals, not automatic proof. Price, promotion, stock and traffic sources can change the same behaviour.

Test one clear change at a time on a sufficiently comparable product group. Record the date, affected SKUs and other changes. A new detail view may help, but movement without a control does not prove photography caused it.

## From a beautiful picture to a dependable product system

Product photography becomes valuable when imagery, product data and workflow align. Define the question each image answers, work with a repeatable set and record what is complete, approved and published for every SKU. A new collection can then fit the visual system without reinventing the process.
