# Creating a testimonial video: from real customer experience to credible evidence

<LeadIntro>
**Creating a testimonial video** starts with a customer who can explain a relevant, genuine experience clearly. Do not simply choose your best-known name. Choose someone whose situation the intended viewer will recognise. Prepare open questions and verifiable context, never script the answer, and agree before recording where, for how long and for what purpose the footage may be used. Then edit for meaning, not superlatives.
</LeadIntro>

A testimonial can help build trust because a customer, rather than the business, describes what happened. That advantage disappears as soon as the video feels like a rehearsed advert. A general statement such as "the collaboration was great" says little about the starting point, the decision, the process or the outcome. A credible customer story provides enough context to understand the experience and retains relevant nuance.

This guide expands on [why to invest in a corporate video](/en/kennisbank/videografie/why-invest-in-a-corporate-video/). It explains how to choose, invite and prepare a customer, which questions produce specific answers, how to manage claims and rights, and which versions remain useful. For production and guidance, explore [customer testimonial video production](/en/services/videography/testimonial-video/).

<QuoteBlock>
A customer testimonial is not a compilation of compliments. It is carefully presented evidence of an experience, with enough context for viewers to form their own judgement.
</QuoteBlock>

## What exactly is a testimonial video?

A testimonial video is a recorded customer statement about a specific experience with an organisation, service or product. The customer speaks from their own role and circumstances. It differs from a corporate video, in which the organisation takes centre stage, and from a detailed case study that often combines several sources, process steps and results.

The strongest testimonials have one clear subject. They may explain why a client chose a particular construction approach, how a software team experienced an implementation or what a hospitality entrepreneur found useful in a collaboration. They do not need to discuss the entire offer. One defined experience is easier to understand and easier to place beside relevant website copy later.

## When does a testimonial video add value?

A testimonial is useful when a potential customer mainly wants to understand what a collaboration feels like in practice or what a comparable organisation experienced. It can make uncertainty about guidance, implementation, communication or suitability more concrete. This works best when the speaker and situation are recognisable to the audience.

Do not use a testimonial to conceal missing product information, conditions or evidence from your own business. A customer experience is one perspective, not a guarantee that every customer will achieve the same result. Place the video beside clear explanations, case studies, services and contact information. If you are still weighing the wider role of video, our videography pillar offers a [framework for choosing the goal and format](/en/kennisbank/videografie/why-invest-in-a-corporate-video/).

## How do you choose the right customer for a testimonial?

Choose for relevance, not name recognition alone. A smaller customer with a recognisable challenge and a clear story may be more meaningful than a famous logo offering only generic praise. Look for similarities in sector, decision-making role, starting point, project type and the intended viewer's objections.

Assess whether the collaboration is sufficiently stable too. A customer still in the middle of a complex delivery may not yet be able to evaluate the complete experience. Check that both the contact and organisation are authorised to participate, and that brand names, locations and results may be made public. Ask which subjects are sensitive or confidential before the shoot.

Create a shortlist and connect each candidate to one viewer question. This prevents six testimonials from all saying that the service was good while none explains a specific barrier or decision.

## How do you invite a customer respectfully?

Explain why you are asking this particular customer, which subject will be central and what participation involves in practice. State who will attend, how much time is planned, where recording will take place, which publication channels are intended and how the content review will work. Give them room to decline without disadvantage.

Do not send an unexpected camera crew to the customer's premises or hide distribution rights in general terms. A testimonial for one case-study page is different from footage used in paid advertising, at trade fairs or in a long-running international campaign. Discuss later extensions again rather than silently treating them as included.

<NoticeBox variant="tip" title="Make the invitation specific">
Do not ask, "Would you say something positive about us?" Ask whether the customer would explain a particular collaboration and their own experience of it. This makes the purpose more honest and helps them decide whether participation is appropriate.
</NoticeBox>

A gift, discount or other benefit may affect how the communication is assessed. Record every form of compensation internally and consider in advance whether and how the commercial context needs to be disclosed. Ask for legal review of the specific campaign if you are unsure.

## How do you prepare the customer without scripting answers?

Share the topics and practical details beforehand, not a list of approved superlatives. Explain who will watch, why the story matters and which phases you intend to cover. Ask the customer to recall examples, documents or project moments. They then arrive prepared without sounding rehearsed.

Explain that answers may be reformulated during recording. Not everyone speaks concisely on camera on the first attempt. The interviewer can ask for more context, a self-contained response or a more precise fact. That is guidance, provided the meaning continues to come from the customer.

## Which interview questions produce specific answers?

Begin with context. What was the speaker's role? What was the situation before the collaboration? Which decision did they face? Then ask about the process and their observations: what happened, what stood out and which step helped? Finish with significance and advice. This creates a story rather than an unsupported rating.

Avoid leading questions such as "How fantastic was our guidance?" The desired adjective is already built in. Use open wording and ask for examples. When someone says "very professional", ask: "What specifically made you notice that?" The second answer often contains the actual evidence.

## How do you create a relaxed interview setting?

Choose a quiet location connected to the story. Filming customers in their own workplace can feel credible, but check sound, sight lines, visitors, screens and business processes. Position the interviewer close to the camera so eye line and conversation feel natural. Limit the number of people offering comments from behind the monitor.

Begin with an ordinary conversation before recording and repeat that no perfect performance is expected. Ask simple context questions first. Give speakers time to complete a thought and allow silence. Do not interrupt as soon as an answer becomes longer. Nuance can be edited concisely later.

Combine the interview with relevant footage of the location, product or collaboration. B-roll prevents visual monotony and can substantiate statements. Do not stage an action the customer never performs. Ask which genuine moments can be recorded safely and representatively.

If portraits are also required that day, use our [corporate portrait tips](/en/kennisbank/fotografie/corporate-portrait-tips/). Align lighting, clothing and timing while protecting enough calm for the conversation.

## How do you keep claims honest and verifiable?

A testimonial becomes advertising when it is used in commercial communications. [FPS Economy](https://economie.fgov.be/en/themes/sales/advertising) explains that Belgian economic law regulates misleading actions and omissions in advertising and commercial practices, among other matters. A genuine customer quotation is therefore not exempt from review. Consider both the factual content and the overall presentation.

The European [Unfair Commercial Practices Directive](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32005L0029) addresses information that is false or may deceive the average consumer, as well as omissions of material information. Do not cut a condition that makes a result seem more general than it was. Never present one person's experience as a guaranteed outcome for everyone.

Keep the sources for figures, project dates and technical statements alongside the approved edit. If the situation changes later, someone must know which video or cut needs review.

## What consent and privacy agreements do you need?

In its guidance on [image rights](https://gegevensbeschermingsautoriteit.be/burger/thema-s/recht-op-afbeelding/principes), the Belgian Data Protection Authority distinguishes consent to create an image from consent to publish or distribute it. A customer who sits in front of the camera has not automatically accepted every future channel or edit. Record the arrangements clearly and in writing.

At minimum, define the person and organisation involved, purpose, channels, territory, duration, types of edit, short cuts, paid use, captions and contact route. Discuss what happens if the person's role changes, the customer relationship ends, facts become outdated or someone asks for the use to be reviewed. The relevant legal basis and consequences depend on the circumstances, so have legal counsel review the agreement.

A recognisable video may also contain personal data. The [GDPR](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32016R0679) includes principles concerning purpose limitation, transparency, data minimisation, accuracy and storage limitation. Turn them into a managed process: restrict access to raw recordings, store permissions where they can be found, register publication locations and set a review date.

<NoticeBox variant="warning" title="Do not treat one agreement as a blank cheque">
Agreement for a case-study page does not automatically cover a later advertising campaign, trade-fair film or edit with a new meaning. Describe the intended use specifically and arrange extensions before publication. This article is not legal advice.
</NoticeBox>

## How do you build the story in the edit?

A clear testimonial follows the customer's experience, not the order of every interview question. Open with a recognisable situation or meaningful observation. Then provide enough context about the role and challenge. Show the decision and collaboration, support the main experience with images, and close with a nuanced insight or logical next step.

Remove filler words and repetition without changing meaning. A sentence from the beginning and one from the end may fit together technically while losing the nuance between them. The editor and content owner should therefore review for meaning. Use on-screen text for the name, role and necessary context, not to make statements larger than what was said.

Preferably let the business CTA follow visually or on the page. The customer need not be forced to tell everyone to buy immediately. The testimonial supplies the experience; the organisation remains responsible for the commercial next step.

## Which short and long versions do you need?

Plan one core edit with enough context, then create derivatives for each use. A case-study page can support a longer version that explains the situation and process. A service page may need one relevant extract. For social media, create a self-contained opening and add context in the caption or accompanying text.

Do not isolate only the most flattering eight seconds from a nuanced response. Check whether the subject, speaker and condition remain understandable. Keep a register of approved versions because every new edit presents the customer statement again.

## How do you make the testimonial accessible?

Record intelligible sound, give name labels sufficient contrast and check captions for accuracy. The [WCAG guidance on captions for prerecorded media](https://www.w3.org/WAI/WCAG22/Understanding/captions-prerecorded) covers relevant non-speech audio as well as dialogue. Include a sound, reaction or change of speaker when it is needed to understand the story.

YouTube warns in its guidance on [automatic captioning](https://support.google.com/youtube/answer/6373554?hl=en) that pronunciation, accents, dialects and background sound can cause errors and that generated captions need review. Personal names, company names and specialist terms are particularly important in testimonials. Ask the customer to confirm the correct spelling beforehand.

Where appropriate, publish a transcript or substantive summary beside the video. This helps visitors who cannot or prefer not to play it and gives the experience written context. Do not leave all essential information solely in moving images.

## Where should you publish a testimonial video?

Place a testimonial close to the question it answers. A customer story about implementation belongs with the relevant service or case study, not only in a generic logo carousel. Provide context around the video about the project, speaker and applicability. Add one logical next step without making the statement compete with several buttons.

In sales, use a version suited to the conversation and avoid sharing an unmanaged large download. On social media, the customer should understand that comments, sharing and advertising use differ from publication on your own website. Agree channels in advance and check after publication that the name, role, captions and accompanying text remain correct.

When video is the main content on its own page, Google's [video SEO guidance](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/video) covers discoverable embeds, stable thumbnails, metadata and indexable watch pages, among other matters. This helps prepare technical discoverability but does not guarantee a rich result or ranking.

## How do you measure whether the customer story is useful?

Begin with qualitative signals. Do viewers understand which situation the story applies to? Does sales share the right extract? Does it generate better follow-up questions? Does the customer still recognise their experience in the edit? These answers reveal more about usefulness than one high view count.

Then connect channel metrics to a hypothesis. On a service page, you can see whether visitors open a relevant case study or contact step after the testimonial. Sales can note which objection the extract helps discuss. On social media, assess the quality of comments and the context of click-throughs. Do not claim hard causation when the page, campaign or audience changes at the same time.

Plan maintenance too. Roles, brands, offers and results can become outdated. A persuasive quotation with obsolete context is not a permanent asset. Periodically check consent, current accuracy, publication locations and links, and document what is replaced.

## From a satisfied customer to useful, honest evidence

A strong testimonial does not begin by asking who is most enthusiastic. It begins by asking which genuine experience will help a future customer make a better-informed decision. Select a relevant speaker, prepare an open conversation and let the specific process and context do the work.

Treat consent, claims and editing as parts of production, not administration to complete afterwards. Then create only versions that preserve the meaning and place them where viewers can connect the experience to clear information. The customer remains recognisable in the story, while the organisation takes responsibility for what it communicates through that story.
