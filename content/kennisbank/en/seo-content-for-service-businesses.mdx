# SEO content for service businesses: from content model to strong brief

<LeadIntro>
**SEO content for service businesses works best when every page has one clear job.** A service page helps a prospect assess the offer. A knowledge-base article answers one independent question. A case study provides verifiable evidence. An FAQ resolves short remaining doubts. Connect them with purposeful internal links, appropriate CTAs and metrics suited to each page type.
</LeadIntro>

Publishing more pages is not a content strategy. The aim is to give prospective clients the right information at each decision point and a logical route forward. A service business often has substantial expertise but distributes it poorly: one service page tries to rank for every question, articles repeat commercial copy, case studies contain no context and FAQs paraphrase keywords. The result may be large without being useful. Our guide to [GEO and AEO for SMEs](/en/kennisbank/seo-geo/how-to-appear-in-ai-search-results-geo-aeo/) explains how this people-first model also supports generative search experiences.

Start from client decisions. Record what a prospect needs to understand, which evidence you can genuinely provide, who owns the facts and what next step suits that moment. Google likewise recommends creating useful, reliable [people-first content](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/creating-helpful-content). Its current [guidance for generative search features](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/ai-optimization-guide) stresses unique, non-commodity content grounded in first-hand expertise. This creates useful content for people and a coherent structure for search and AI systems without promising rankings or citations.

<NoticeBox variant="tip" title="Do not start with a word count">
Google has no preferred word count. Start with the reader's question, the page's task and the evidence required for a responsible answer. A page is long enough when the intended reader can decide or continue without having to search again for the same basic information.
</NoticeBox>

## One subject, four different page functions

The same expertise can support several pages without duplicating them. A company offering technical SEO might have a service page explaining scope and process, an article diagnosing crawl problems, a case study documenting a migration and an FAQ answering whether an audit includes implementation. Each serves a different reader moment.

This division also works in a compact form on a one-page website. The comparison of a [one-page website and a full website](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/one-page-or-full-website/) helps determine how many distinct search intents and pages the offer genuinely needs.

## 1. The service page owns the main commercial question

A service page explains who the service is for, what it covers, how the work proceeds, why the approach is credible and what the next step involves. It may answer practical questions, but its primary role is commercial evaluation rather than a complete textbook chapter.

Write from the client's decision. Replace broad statements such as "tailored quality" with scope, process and relevant limits. Explain what the intake requires, what the client receives, how feedback works and which dependencies affect planning. Strong commercial copy makes an offer easier to assess rather than trying to remove all uncertainty with inflated promises.

## 2. A knowledge-base article answers one genuine question

An article deserves its own URL when the question is independently useful and requires depth. Examples include "when should I redesign my website?", "how should I prepare a corporate shoot?" and "what is the difference between AEO and SEO?" Give the core answer early, then work through nuance, scenarios and next steps.

Research starts with client language. Gather questions from sales calls, email, intake, support, quotation feedback, reviews and Search Console. Record who asks, at which moment and which decision sits behind the words. Someone searching "optimise or redesign a website" wants a diagnosis. A person searching "website design company" may already be comparing providers. One page for both intents quickly becomes vague.

The article need not reproduce the sales pitch. Answer honestly, state boundaries and link to the relevant service afterwards. Use primary sources for factual statements and label practical experience as such. The guide on [when to redesign a website](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/when-to-redesign-your-website/) is decision content that does not automatically recommend the largest intervention.

## 3. A case study provides evidence general copy cannot

A case study is more than a portfolio image and an enthusiastic sentence. Describe the starting point, objective, relevant constraints, chosen approach and what verifiably changed. Use numbers only when their definition, period and source are known. If figures are confidential or not comparable, show another form of evidence: before-and-after structure, delivered functionality, image choices, process improvement or an approved client quotation with context.

Do not reconstruct a success story after the event. A project may be valuable because of a difficult constraint, such as a fixed launch date, consistent photography across locations, migration without losing important URLs or making a complex service understandable. That context helps future clients judge relevance.

Real photography and video may provide stronger evidence than stock images. Use [corporate photography](/en/diensten/photography/corporate-photography/) or a [corporate video](/en/diensten/videography/corporate-video/) when media have a genuine explanatory role.

## 4. An FAQ comes from genuine uncertainty

An FAQ suits concise follow-up questions remaining after the main copy. Build it from conversations, objections and practical uncertainty, not variants of one keyword. Answers should stand alone, but need not compress an entire article. A question requiring many scenarios or substantial evidence deserves its own page.

Since 7 May 2026, Google no longer shows FAQ rich results. Expandable FAQs remain useful to readers, and structured data can describe visible content, but FAQPage should not be treated as a ranking trick. The change appears in the [Google Search documentation updates](https://developers.google.com/search/updates#removing-faq-rich-result). Markup must also match what visitors can read under the [general structured-data guidelines](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/structured-data/sd-policies).

The definitions of [AEO](/en/kennisbank/seo-geo/what-is-aeo/) and [GEO](/en/kennisbank/seo-geo/what-is-generative-engine-optimisation/) provide context for answer-focused and generative search. Neither changes the basic rule: write for the real reader question first.

## 5. Make sources and evidence part of the brief

A writer cannot invent credible expertise. Before drafting, identify the subject-matter expert, statements needing external support and evidence already available. Strong primary sources include official product documentation, legislation, standards, research reports or your own measurements with a clear definition. A blog summarising another blog is rarely the best support for a firm claim.

Keep four categories distinct:

- **Fact:** verifiable information requiring a source or internal record.
- **Experience:** an observation from projects, clearly presented as experience.
- **Advice:** a recommendation with context and conditions.
- **Promise:** language used only when the business can genuinely deliver and define it.

For important claims, record the source, publication date, access date and owner of the final check. Do not use a statistic merely because it sounds impressive. Verify the population, period and meaning. Review time-sensitive platform information when updating, and change `updatedAt` only after a real content review.

## 6. Match the CTA to the decision stage

An introductory article need not shout "request a quotation" after every section. Offer the next step that suits the question. After a definition, further reading may fit. After a checklist, an analysis may help. Contact can be more prominent on a service page. A case study can lead to a comparable approach or the associated service.

Keep the primary action visible and specific. One secondary link may offer depth, but avoid a menu of six competing buttons. Measure not only clicks, but whether the destination and contact process fulfil the promise.

## 7. Build internal links as purposeful routes

Internal links give visitors and crawlers a route through your expertise. Use ordinary crawlable links with descriptive anchors. Google advises that link text should indicate the destination and that internal pages should be reachable through another discoverable page. See its [official link guidance](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/crawling-indexing/links-crawlable).

## 8. Use a content brief that forces decisions

A good brief is not one keyword, two thousand words and three competitors. It explains why the page exists, whom it helps and which information cannot be omitted.

<ChecklistBlock title="Content brief for a service business" items={[
  <span><strong>Page function:</strong> service, article, case study or FAQ, with one primary search intent.</span>,
  <span><strong>Reader and moment:</strong> role, prior knowledge, situation and current decision.</span>,
  <span><strong>Core answer:</strong> one verifiable sentence placed early.</span>,
  <span><strong>Scope:</strong> questions the page addresses and deliberately excludes.</span>,
  <span><strong>Subject expert:</strong> name, role, interview moment and fact-check owner.</span>,
  <span><strong>Sources and evidence:</strong> primary links, cases, imagery, data, examples and permission.</span>,
  <span><strong>Structure:</strong> necessary sections in a logical decision sequence.</span>,

  <span><strong>CTA:</strong> one primary next step and one optional deeper route.</span>,
  <span><strong>Measurement and maintenance:</strong> queries, interaction, conversion, owner and review trigger.</span>,

Add image requirements where relevant: subject, function, aspect ratio, alt-text owner, rights and caption. Plan this before the build. The [new website checklist](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/new-website-checklist/) prevents final content and media being postponed until after design.

## 9. Edit for clarity, not keyword density

Use the language clients need to recognise the subject. Place the primary term naturally in the title, H1 and introduction where appropriate, but do not repeat it mechanically. Synonyms and concrete subquestions emerge as the page covers the subject fully. Remove paragraphs that merely rephrase a target term.

Titles should be unique, descriptive and concise. Google may assemble title links from several page signals and can adjust a supplied title. A meta description is also a proposal, not a guaranteed snippet. Follow its guidance for [title links](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/title-link) and [snippets](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/snippet), while writing an honest summary for prospective visitors.

## 10. Measure according to page function

Search Console's Performance report includes clicks, impressions, average position and queries. Analyse page and query together. An article may build relevant impressions and routes to deeper material, while a service page receives less traffic but more suitable contact actions. One universal KPI cannot assess both fairly. See the [official Performance report guidance](https://support.google.com/webmasters/answer/7576553).

Record a baseline before publication when an existing page is involved. Then note the publication date, material updates and internal links. Combine search data with analytics, forms, calls and CRM information only where necessary and privacy-conscious. Focus on quality: does the page lead to questions the offer can genuinely answer?

A practical model has three layers:

1. **Visibility:** relevant queries, impressions, indexation and pages receiving traffic.
2. **Behaviour:** clicks to articles, cases, services or contact and meaningful content interaction.
3. **Business value:** suitable enquiries, appointments, quotations, sales and recurring client questions.

Do not update simply because six months have passed. Review when the offer, evidence, platform, regulation, search demand or conversion route changes. During a redesign, identify content and URLs that already have value before rewriting or deleting them.

## How this model supports AEO, GEO and technology

Answer-focused and generative search do not change the page's fundamental task. A direct answer, clear provenance, consistent facts and supporting evidence help the visitor first. [What is AEO?](/en/kennisbank/seo-geo/what-is-aeo/) covers the answer perspective, while [what is GEO?](/en/kennisbank/seo-geo/what-is-generative-engine-optimisation/) places generative visibility in context. Neither replaces strong SEO, content or source checking.

Technical accessibility remains essential. An excellent article blocked by robots, noindex, incorrect canonicals or rendering cannot fulfil its role in Search. [Google Search Essentials](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/essentials) outlines the technical and policy baseline. Also use the [technical SEO checklist](/en/kennisbank/seo-geo/technical-seo-checklist/) and seek [technical SEO support](/en/diensten/seo/technical-seo/) where implementation is complex.

## Turn expertise into a maintainable system

A strong content model makes knowledge manageable as well as publishable. Every page has an owner, source, evidence, route and measurement point. Service pages, articles, cases and FAQs complement one another without retelling the same story four times. The knowledge base can then grow with genuine client questions while the commercial website stays clear.

If you do not want to organise the whole process internally, [SEO copywriting](/en/diensten/seo/seo-copywriting/) can bring interviews, source checking and editing together. The expertise remains your team's; the structure helps visitors find, understand and trust it.
