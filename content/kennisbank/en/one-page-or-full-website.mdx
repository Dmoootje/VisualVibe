# One-page or full website: which suits your SME?

<LeadIntro>
Choosing a **one-page or full website** is not a contest between cheap and professional. Either can be well designed, fast and persuasive. The real difference is how much explanation your offer requires, how many different questions your customers ask and how much room your business needs to grow online.
</LeadIntro>

A one-page website brings the essential information together on one continuously scrolling page. A full website divides that information across separate URLs, for example for services, case studies, sectors and contact. The second model is more extensive, but that does not automatically make it better. A consultant with one clear offer may benefit more from a focused one-page site than from seven thin pages. A contractor with five specialisms, dozens of completed projects and several customer types will quickly run out of room on a single page.

The right choice does not start with a page count. It starts with what a visitor needs to understand, compare and do. Our [complete guide to commissioning a website](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/complete-guide-to-building-a-website-in-limburg/) explains how strategy, content, design, technology and media come together. Here, we focus on one decision: how much structure does your story need?

## When is a one-page website a strong choice?

A one-page website works well when almost every visitor needs the same story. Consider a start-up with one service, a temporary campaign, an event, a personal portfolio or a new concept testing its market. Visitors arrive at the top, receive context and proof, and finish at one clear action.

Its strength lies in the editing. Space is limited, so every section must serve a purpose. Having a [one-page website designed](/en/services/web-design/one-page-website/) does not mean placing every piece of company information in one long stack. It means carefully choosing what someone needs to make a decision.

A one-page website can also be a sensible first phase. You launch with a solid brand story and add separate service or knowledge pages once there is enough unique content and demand to justify them. Make provision for that expansion in the technology and content from the outset. Otherwise, growth may force you to rethink everything later.

## When is a full website the better option?

A full website becomes the logical choice when visitors need different routes. Someone looking for an online shop has different questions from someone who needs an informational business website. A private customer looks for different evidence from a professional buyer. And someone ready to request a quotation wants to reach that action faster than someone still comparing solutions.

Separate pages let you give each subject enough context. The homepage no longer needs to carry every detail. It provides direction, while service pages, case studies and knowledge articles offer greater depth. This creates a calmer experience and stronger internal connections.

A [full business website](/en/services/web-design/business-website-design/) is usually the better foundation when you:

- offer several services that each need explanation or proof;
- serve both consumers and businesses, or several sectors;
- want to be found for different search queries;
- regularly publish case studies, vacancies, news or advice;
- have several conversions, such as purchasing, booking and requesting quotations;
- operate across several regions or languages;
- need integrations, reservations, accounts or other functionality;
- expect your offer to continue growing over the coming years.

More pages are useful only when each one has its own purpose. Five almost identical service pages do not help a visitor. A compact site with four strong pages is more valuable than a sitemap containing twenty empty shells.

If you want to sell products directly, the decision extends beyond page structure. Stock, payments, shipping and customer service turn the website into a commercial system. ## Does a one-page website rank worse in Google?

Not automatically. The Google guidance cited here does not identify page type or page count as a ranking criterion in its own right. One strong page can also be crawled, indexed and shown for relevant searches. Content quality, technical accessibility, external and internal signals, and alignment with search intent remain important.

There is, however, a genuine structural difference. One URL shares one title, one description and one link destination, even when the page may be relevant to several related searches. If you try to cover five clearly distinct services on that URL, the same page has to meet several different information needs. Separate URLs allow you to answer each search intent more precisely and link relevant knowledge articles to the right service.

Whichever format you choose, the technical foundations must be sound. Use the [technical SEO checklist](/en/kennisbank/seo-geo/technical-seo-checklist/) to check crawlability, metadata and structured data for either model. Google's [SEO Starter Guide](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/fundamentals/seo-starter-guide) explains that logical organisation helps visitors and search engines understand how pages relate to one another. Google also recommends [standard, crawlable links with descriptive anchor text](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/crawling-indexing/links-crawlable). We can infer that multiple meaningful routes create more opportunities to connect specific topics precisely. Google does not publish a separate rule that devalues one-page websites simply because of their format.

<NoticeBox variant="tip" title="Choose based on search intent">
If you mainly want to be found for one coherent topic, a one-page website may offer enough focus. If you want separate visibility for several services, each serving a different information need, a full website gives you more editorial room.
</NoticeBox>

Anchor links to sections help visitors navigate a one-page website, but they remain anchors within the same URL. They do not replace standalone landing pages when a subject deserves its own answer, evidence and conversion path.

## Is a one-page website always cheaper?

Its scope is often smaller, but the price difference is not simply a matter of page count. A one-page website still requires strategy, copywriting, photography, responsive design, development, performance work, [accessibility](https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG22/), forms, analytics and testing. A bespoke one-page site with strong visual storytelling can require more work than a straightforward website based on a standard structure.

## Think beyond launch

The best choice does more than suit you today. It also prevents ordinary growth from becoming unnecessarily expensive. Perhaps you begin with one consultancy service but add training courses and programmes within a year. Or you initially operate locally and later serve several regions. Your website must be able to accommodate new information logically.

A one-page website can grow up to a point. You can add a case study, an extra FAQ or a new section. Once navigation becomes confusing, the page grows heavy or different audiences must scroll past irrelevant sections, splitting it up makes more sense. That is not a failure. It is the normal evolution from a validated offer to a richer website.

A full website requires more decisions from the outset: URL structure, navigation, templates and internal links. That demands more thought, but creates room for a knowledge base, case studies and new services. For an SME with clear growth ambitions, that scalability is often more important than the smallest possible launch scope.

If you are replacing an existing site, the question is not only which new structure you want. Existing URLs, search traffic, forms and valuable content need to migrate safely. Our guide to [when to redesign your website](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/when-to-redesign-your-website/) covers the warning signs and migration decisions involved.

Technology matters in either model. Google recommends good [Core Web Vitals](https://developers.google.com/search/docs/appearance/core-web-vitals), while stressing that performance is only one of several factors involved in page experience and ranking. A one-page site with heavy video can be slow; a full website with well-optimised media can be fast. Format alone does not determine performance. Our detailed guide explains [how website speed and SEO work together](/en/kennisbank/webdesign/why-a-fast-website-can-rank-better/).

## Choose the smallest structure that carries your complete story

A one-page website is effective when simplicity is a deliberate choice. A full website is effective when visitors genuinely need greater depth and different routes. Neither can compensate for unclear positioning, weak copy or missing evidence.

Do not choose fewer pages solely to reduce the investment, and do not choose more pages to make the business appear larger. Choose the structure in which a prospective customer quickly understands what you do, why it matters and what to do next.
